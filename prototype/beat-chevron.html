He took the check. Then he defended it.

$39,200 from Chevron USA Inc. landed in Xavier Becerra's 2026 California governor account on June 16, 2025. It is the legal max. Ten months later, asked at a public forum why he kept it, he told voters Chevron was not the bad guy. Two of his rivals signed pledges refusing fossil fuel money. He took it.

The Chevron timeline · California AG to California governor candidate

Becerra inherited an Exxon climate-fraud file from Kamala Harris in 2017 and never filed it. His successor filed the broader climate-deception suit in 2023, naming Chevron. In 2025 Chevron sent Becerra the legal max. In 2026 Becerra defended the company.

The receipt is the cleanest part of this story. Cal-Access transaction 5869881, filing 3071361. Chevron USA Inc., $39,200, dated June 16, 2025, recipient committee 1480025 (Becerra for Governor 2026). The amount is the maximum a corporation can give a California gubernatorial candidate per election. Chevron gave the max.

What Becerra did with the check is the rest of this page. He did not return it. He did not say, when asked, that taking it had been a mistake. He used a public stage to defend the donor. Two of his rivals refused fossil fuel money. He took it and told voters Chevron is not the bad guy.

The receipt.

One transaction. One date. One donor. The 2026 California gubernatorial contribution limit is $39,200 per source per election. Chevron USA Inc., the U.S. operating subsidiary of Chevron Corporation, filed a single contribution to Becerra for Governor 2026 at exactly that amount. The Cal-Access record is below.

No other Chevron USA Inc. transaction appears in the committee receipts file across the 12,259-row Cal-Access bulk extract for committee 1480025 through April 18, 2026. No employees of Chevron Corporation appear at the $1,000-or-more threshold under any "Chevron" employer string in the receipts data, but the employer field is unpopulated for the vast majority of the committee's individual donors. Western States Petroleum Association, the trade body Chevron belongs to, is not in the file. American Fuel & Petrochemical Manufacturers is not in the file. The single $39,200 corporate check is the entire fossil-fuel direct exposure on Becerra's filed receipts as of mid-April 2026.

That clean isolation is part of what makes the donation editorially significant. There is no plausible accident. There is no entity-name confusion to litigate. A single corporate check for the legal max from one of California's most lobbied-against companies sits on Becerra's contribution list. Chevron knew what it was doing. Becerra's campaign accepted it. That is the document the rest of this page rests on.

The defense.

On April 29, 2026, Becerra was at a League of California Cities forum when KQED's Scott Schafer asked him directly why he had accepted Chevron money. The answer is in the record. Politico's California Climate newsletter published it the same day.

"Chevron, that's the problem with politics, they're not the bad guy. Does everybody here drive an electric vehicle? You need Chevron, I need Chevron, my people of the state of California need Chevron." Xavier Becerra · League of California Cities forum · April 29, 2026 · reported by Politico California Climate

Read the answer slowly. The question is whether Becerra should have taken the donation. The answer is a question back to the audience about whether they drive electric vehicles. The framing inverts the accountability: the audience is asked to justify needing oil, not the candidate to justify taking oil money. Then the company is reclassified from donor under scrutiny to civic necessity in one phrase. "They're not the bad guy" closes the loop.

California Environmental Voters published a same-week response titled "Becerra is wrong about Big Oil." The headline is the argument. Climate Action California, a 6,500-member grassroots organization, voted to endorse Tom Steyer for governor and declined to place Becerra on the endorsement ballot at all, citing the Chevron donation as the disqualifying fact. These are not anonymous critics. They are named-organization positions on a record-of-events page. Both are sourced in the Politico story.

Becerra's spokesperson Jonathan Underland defended the donation in the same Politico piece, telling the newsletter Becerra "has never been bought by a check" and pointing to Becerra's record as California Attorney General challenging Trump-era rollbacks of fuel-efficiency standards, methane regulations, and fracking lease expansions. That defense is a real record. The next two sections weigh it.

What two rivals did.

Steyer's pledge does not exist in a vacuum. He is being attacked by approximately $22 million of utility-and-industry independent expenditure spending in the same race, much of it organized by PG&E and the California Chamber of Commerce, with the explicit message that he is not the kind of billionaire he claims to be. That spending pattern is its own beat. What it demonstrates here is that the candidate Chevron-and-utility-aligned interests are spending tens of millions to defeat is the candidate who pledged not to take their money. The candidate they sent the check to is the one who took it.

A May 1, 2026 Sacramento Bee analysis added quantitative context. Across the 2024 and 2025 election cycles, Chevron gave directly to 21 and 11 California Democratic lawmakers respectively. Chevron donated more than $100,000 each to two Latino-leadership-caucus PACs over three years; those PACs in turn contributed more than $29,000 to Assemblymember Alex Lee, an early Steyer endorser. Chevron's three-year California political-causes total was $10.8 million. The company's 2025 California lobbying expenditure alone was $12,935,584, the highest of any single entity in the state. The $39,200 to Becerra is one transaction inside a much larger California political-influence infrastructure. Becerra is the candidate Chevron decided to fund directly at the legal limit in the 2026 governor primary.

The Exxon file.

Becerra's posture toward the oil majors as California Attorney General is the historical pattern the 2026 Chevron defense fits inside. The clearest single test of that posture was an inherited investigation he allowed to stall.

In January 2016, then-California Attorney General Kamala Harris opened an investigation into ExxonMobil after the LA Times and Inside Climate News reported that Exxon's internal documents conflicted with the company's public statements minimizing climate science. New York and Massachusetts had already issued public subpoenas. When Harris won her U.S. Senate seat and Becerra was appointed Attorney General by Governor Jerry Brown in January 2017, the file was on his desk.

Eighteen California Democratic members of Congress urged Becerra in 2017 to continue the investigation. More than thirty environmental groups joined the call. By May 2017, the LA Times editorial board wrote that Becerra had not "uttered a peep" about the probe and that his office declined to confirm whether the investigation was even ongoing. He served as Attorney General until March 2021. No California lawsuit against ExxonMobil for climate fraud was filed during his four years in the office.

What was filed during those four years was a December 2020 amicus brief, a friend-of-the-court filing supporting Baltimore's separate climate-damages lawsuit against oil companies. An amicus brief is not a lawsuit. It is a legal opinion submitted in someone else's case. The substantive California state action against the oil majors was not brought.

Becerra's successor, Attorney General Rob Bonta, filed it. On September 16, 2023, Bonta sued BP, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, ExxonMobil, Shell, and the American Petroleum Institute on behalf of the people of California, alleging decades of deceptive marketing about the climate impact of fossil fuels. The complaint was amended on June 10, 2024, to add additional defendants. As of April 2026 the case is paused pending a U.S. Supreme Court decision on whether the cases belong in state or federal court. The substantive theory is the same theory the Harris investigation had been developing eight years earlier, before Becerra inherited the file.

The historical record here is not contested. The two structural facts are (1) Becerra inherited an active climate-fraud investigation against the oil majors and did not bring a state action in four years, and (2) his successor brought the action two years after Becerra left, naming Chevron among the defendants. The same Chevron then sent Becerra the legal max for his gubernatorial campaign. The Politico defense quote is what the candidate said when asked about that sequence in public.

What Chevron is asking the next governor for.

Donors do not write checks for sentiment. Chevron's policy asks for the next California governor are documented in the public record. Six are material as of May 2026.

Cap-and-Invest amendments at CARB. California reauthorized the cap-and-trade program through 2045 in September 2025 (SB 840 and AB 1207, signed by Newsom). The California Air Resources Board is now developing implementing regulations. In March 2026, Chevron sent a letter to Newsom and CARB warning that proposed implementing amendments would make California refinery operations uneconomic and threatening to shut its El Segundo and Richmond refineries if the rules pass. WSPA, Chevron's trade body, called the September 2025 reauthorization vote rushed and lobbied against it. The next governor sets CARB's appointment direction.

Low Carbon Fuel Standard tightening. CARB's amended LCFS regulation took effect July 1, 2025. It tightens carbon-intensity benchmarks toward a 30 percent reduction by 2030 and 90 percent by 2045. Tighter benchmarks make conventional petroleum-fuel blending more expensive. Chevron's stake is in delaying or weakening compliance schedules.

El Segundo refinery oversight after the October 2 fire. Detailed in the next section.

Richmond refinery permitting and air-quality settlements. The Bay Area Air Quality Management District reached a 2024 settlement with Chevron on Rule 6-5 violations requiring up to $138 million in payments and a community air-quality fund. A June 2025 settlement required additional air monitoring transparency. In December 2025, Chevron paid the air district $900,000 for monitoring failures. The City of Richmond's Community Benefits Agreement ties annual payments to the Richmond refinery's conditional use permit. Chevron's stake is in whether the next governor's appointees on CalGEM and the California Energy Commission tighten or loosen permits and enforcement.

The climate-deception lawsuit Bonta filed. The case naming Chevron is paused pending the Supreme Court federal-jurisdiction question. A new governor's Attorney General would manage the litigation strategy on remand. A governor who said publicly that Chevron is not the bad guy is signaling, however indirectly, what direction that strategy might take.

CalGEM oil-well permitting acceleration. In the first three weeks of January 2026, CalGEM approved more new drilling permits than in all of 2025, following the January 1, 2026 implementation of SB 237 (signed September 2025), which gave Kern County new authority over drilling decisions. The next governor's appointments to CalGEM and the CEC determine whether this acceleration continues or reverses. Chevron is one of the largest operators affected.

Six asks. One $39,200 check. The check does not buy any of these outcomes. The check buys access to the candidate who will make appointments and statements about all of them.

The El Segundo refinery.

The El Segundo refinery is the largest oil refinery on the West Coast. It produces approximately one-fifth of Southern California's motor vehicle fuel and roughly 40 percent of its jet fuel. On October 2, 2025, a massive fire broke out in the refinery's jet fuel Isomax unit. As of December 2025, neither Chevron nor state regulators had publicly released the cause of the fire.

The LA Times reported on October 4, 2025 that over the prior five years the El Segundo refinery had accumulated forty-six violations of environmental safety rules, and that over the prior decade it had accumulated seventeen OSHA violations across at least fifteen OSHA inspections, including September 2023 citations for heat illness prevention and missing ladderway guardrails. The South Coast Air Quality Management District filed a petition for variance against Chevron's El Segundo facility on October 24, 2025 (Case 831-408), following an earlier August 25, 2025 petition (Case 831-406).

CalMatters reported on October 9, 2025 that with the U.S. Chemical Safety Board defunded by the Trump administration, no state agency moved to fill the cause-investigation gap. The next governor's posture on whether California creates its own systematic refinery-safety investigation authority is now an open policy question with a real refinery fire and forty-six recent violations sitting in its file.

The donation arrived in June 2025. The fire arrived in October 2025. The defense quote arrived in April 2026. The donor's most material California facility is currently subject to the regulatory regime the next governor will inherit, with a recent industrial fire whose cause is still publicly undisclosed.

What the defense credits.

Becerra's spokesperson Underland pointed reporters to a real record. Becerra's California Attorney General office filed and joined a number of environmental cases against the Trump administration. The record is not an invention. It is also not what the question asked.

Cases his office filed or joined against the federal government on environmental matters during his AG tenure (January 2017 through March 2021) include: a January 2020 lawsuit jointly with Newsom and CARB challenging a federal Bureau of Land Management decision to open Central California to fracking; a September 2020 lawsuit leading 24 states against the EPA's rule gutting methane and VOC standards; an August 2020 lawsuit leading 15 states against the federal rule allowing rail transport of liquefied natural gas; a May 2020 gas-price-manipulation suit against Vitol and Freepoint Commodities related to the 2015 Torrance refinery explosion; a January 2021 challenge to the federal rule discounting public-health benefits of reducing air pollution; a December 2020 amicus brief supporting Baltimore's climate-damages lawsuit; comments opposing offshore lease expansion (March 2018) and supporting the City of Arvin's setback ordinance (June 2018).

Each of those is a defensive action against a federal Trump-era rollback or a procedural intervention in someone else's litigation. The category that is almost entirely absent is affirmative California state enforcement against the named oil majors for state-law violations. A January 2021 Gibson Dunn end-of-term review of Becerra's tenure characterized his office as having filed or joined more than one hundred suits against the Trump administration, with over half on environmental policy, and acknowledged that the review did not identify affirmative enforcement actions against California-based oil and gas companies for state-law violations during the same period.

The pattern is consistent across his AG record. Maximum aggression at the federal government on climate policy. Conspicuous restraint on direct enforcement against the California-headquartered or California-operating fossil-fuel companies whose conduct an attorney general has standing to pursue. Bonta filed the climate-deception suit. Becerra did not.

That distinction is not editorial. It is the difference between joining a defensive coalition against a federal regulation rollback and bringing a California state action against a California-operating oil company. Becerra did the first and not the second. Underland's defense of the donation cites the first record. The record that would have answered the Politico question is the second one.

What this page does not claim.

Investigative work names what is not in the record as much as what is. Five things this page does not claim, listed plainly so a reader can challenge any of them.

  1. This page does not claim Chevron bought a policy outcome. A $39,200 contribution to a campaign account is access. It is not, by itself, evidence of a policy commitment from the candidate to the donor. The defense quote is the candidate's own characterization of the donor; it is a posture, not a vote, and Becerra has no votes to cast as a candidate. The structural alignment between the donor's asks and the candidate's public defense of the donor is the editorially relevant pattern. Coordination would require evidence beyond the public record.
  2. This page does not claim Becerra refused to bring the Exxon case for any specific reason. The contemporaneous internal decision-making at the California DOJ during 2017 through 2021 is not publicly documented. What is documented is that the file was open when he took office, that California members of Congress and named environmental groups asked him to continue it, and that no state action against ExxonMobil was filed during his tenure. The reasons for the non-action are not on the public record. Bonta's eventual filing in 2023, naming Chevron alongside Exxon, used the same evidentiary theory the inherited file had been developing.
  3. This page does not claim Becerra's anti-Trump environmental record is fictional. It is real. It is documented above. It is also a record of defensive litigation against federal rollbacks, not a record of state enforcement against the donor companies. The defense Underland offered to Politico cites the first category. The question Schafer asked was about the second.
  4. This page does not claim the El Segundo fire was caused by Chevron's donation history. The fire is a separate industrial-safety fact. Its cause has not been publicly determined as of December 2025. The reason it appears on this page is that the next governor's posture on refinery oversight is part of what the donation pays for in access terms, and the El Segundo facility is the donor's largest California asset under active regulatory scrutiny.
  5. This page does not claim Steyer or Porter are without donor problems of their own. Steyer's self-funding of his own campaign is its own structural fact. Both candidates' donor universes are auditable on the same Cal-Access. The contrast on this page is narrowly about the fossil-fuel pledge: Steyer signed it, Porter signed it, Becerra did not, and Becerra took the max check from a fossil-fuel company. Other axes of comparison belong on other pages.
