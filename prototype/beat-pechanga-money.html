Pechanga spent more on politics last year than every union except NEA.

The Pechanga Band of Luiseño Mission Indians, headquartered in Temecula in Riverside County and operator of the largest tribal casino in California, was the second-largest political donor in the United States in the most recent cycle. Their priorities map to two policy fights and one quiet land transfer pending in Congress.

Most national-money stories begin in New York or Silicon Valley. This one begins in Temecula, ninety minutes east of the coast, on a reservation that operates a casino producing some of the largest tribal political contributions in American politics. The Pechanga Band's giving doesn't fit the typical industry-PAC frame. It maps to specific battles over what tribal gaming is allowed to be, who gets to compete with it, and what land the tribe can place into federal trust.

The scale of Pechanga's political giving

Per Consumer Watchdog reporting, the Pechanga Band of Luiseño Mission Indians was the number-two political donor in the United States in the most recent reported cycle, behind only the National Education Association. The tribe spent approximately $44 million on political activities in that cycle, the bulk of it directed at California state ballot measures and California state-level candidate races.

Per OpenSecrets, the tribe spent $822,989 on federal contributions and $220,000 on federal lobbying in the 2024 cycle alone. The bulk of its political activity occurs at the California state level, which is not aggregated in federal databases.

Cumulatively across the 2000-2009 decade, the Pechanga Band was the fifth-largest special-interest spender in California state politics, with approximately $69.3 million in disclosed expenditures.

The sports-betting fights

The single largest concentrated Pechanga political expenditure on record was approximately $46 million directed at California Propositions 94, 95, 96, and 97 on the 2008 ballot, which sought to expand tribal slot machines under amended tribal-state gaming compacts.

In 2022, Pechanga was a leading member of the tribal coalition opposing California Proposition 26 (online sports betting under tribal exclusivity) and the parallel cardroom-backed proposition. Combined tribal and gambling-industry spending on the 2022 sports-betting ballot fight ranked among the most expensive ballot-measure campaigns in California history.

SB 549 and the cardroom war

In September 2024, Governor Gavin Newsom signed Senate Bill 549, the Tribal Nations Access to Justice Act. Pechanga was part of the leading tribal coalition that supported the bill. SB 549 enables tribes to bring legal action against California cardrooms over the cardrooms' use of player-dealer / banked-game mechanisms that tribes contend infringe on tribal gaming exclusivity.

On January 1, 2025, seven California tribes including Pechanga filed legal action against multiple California cardrooms under the new statute. The litigation, if successful, would unwind one of California's largest non-tribal gambling industries.

The land-into-trust legislation

In the 119th Congress, two parallel bills pending placement of certain California land into federal trust for the benefit of the Pechanga Band of Indians are under consideration: H.R. 5682 in the U.S. House and S. 4053 in the U.S. Senate. Land-into-trust transfers carry significant federal regulatory and tax consequences, and are typically a tribal priority requiring careful federal political support.

Who Pechanga funds in California

Per Cal-Access state-level data, Pechanga's California state political contributions in the 2024 cycle followed a "max out every legislator we want" pattern. The legislative maximum per-candidate contribution is $11,800; Pechanga delivered that maximum to a wide slate of Assembly and Senate candidates. The standout recipient is Xavier Becerra for Governor 2026, the leading Democratic candidate for the California governor's race. Pechanga max-funded his committee at $39,200, the corporate-entity maximum for a statewide candidate per the FPPC. Becerra is the former U.S. Secretary of Health and Human Services and a former California Attorney General.

Note: Natasha Johnson, the recently sworn-in Assembly member for District 63 (Norco, Menifee, Lake Elsinore, Canyon Lake, parts of Eastvale/Riverside/Corona), received $17,700 from Pechanga and $4,000 from Moving California Forward, the Riverside-based developer PAC. The cross-fund between tribal-gaming money and Inland Empire developer money is documented on a single candidate.

Open questions

The Pechanga Band has not publicly addressed whether its support for Xavier Becerra at the statewide maximum reflects a specific tribal-policy commitment Becerra has made on cardroom litigation, sports betting, or federal land-into-trust legislation.

Xavier Becerra has not publicly addressed the policy expectations that accompany maximum-out tribal gaming contributions or how he would handle conflicts between tribal gaming exclusivity and California's other gambling industries.

The California legislators on Pechanga's max-fund list have not publicly addressed their positions on the pending Pechanga land-into-trust legislation in Congress or on potential California statutes affecting tribal-gaming exclusivity.
