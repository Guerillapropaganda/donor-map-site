The Wesson Pipeline.

Karen Bass's 2026-27 budget loosens short-term rental rules and lets Airbnb prepay lodging tax through the 2028 Olympics. LAist reported both provisions were initially suggested by Airbnb. Airbnb's lobbyist on the deal is the son of the former LA Council President. The father's firm bundled to Bass weeks after the son registered.

The Mayor's office moved on a deal Airbnb suggested. The lobbyist Airbnb sent to push it is the son of the Council President who governed LA for eight years. The son's father runs the California office of a major public affairs firm. The firm's employees became the Mayor's largest single donor cluster in fall 2025, three months after the son registered to lobby for Airbnb.

The mechanism here is not corruption. It is access. The same family, the same office, the same favors.

What's in the budget

Mayor Karen Bass's proposed 2026-27 budget includes two provisions that affect short-term rentals. The first directs the LA City Planning Department to develop a "limited vacation rental program" that would sunset on December 31, 2028, the closing date of the Los Angeles Summer Olympics. The program would loosen current LA rules that prohibit owners from renting out second homes as short-term rentals. The second directs staff to allow "pre-payment of transient occupancy tax in advance of the 2028 Olympics from any payer that wishes to assist the City."

Per LAist reporting, both proposals were "initially suggested by Airbnb." The company has indicated it would prepay approximately $50 million in occupancy tax under the second provision, though specific terms are still being negotiated. Bass's budget projects $313.5 million in transient occupancy tax revenue for 2026-2027.

The LA City Council's Planning and Land Use Management Committee is scheduled to consider the vacation rental ordinance on May 12, 2026. The Council itself is scheduled to vote on the broader budget on May 21, 2026.

Who Justin Wesson is

Justin Wesson is Airbnb's Senior Public Policy Manager for California. He registered as a Los Angeles City lobbyist for Airbnb on August 1, 2025, per the LA City Ethics Commission's lobbyist registry, and renewed his registration for 2026.

Justin Wesson is the son of Herb Wesson, who served on the LA City Council from 2005 to 2020 and as Council President from 2012 to 2020. Justin Wesson worked as a senior aide to his father throughout most of his father's Council tenure, including a period as Floor Director earning approximately $103,000 annually.

Per Our Weekly reporting in 2018, taxpayers paid $2,768 to have Los Angeles Police Department officers provide security at Justin Wesson's September 2017 wedding; he and his wife later reimbursed the city for that cost. CityWatch LA separately raised questions about his voter registration after disclosures showed he had registered using his parents' address despite a six-figure city salary.

Mercury Public Affairs and Herb Wesson

After leaving the Council in December 2020, Herb Wesson became California co-chair of Mercury Public Affairs, a national bipartisan public-affairs firm in January 2024. He registered as a Los Angeles City lobbyist for Mercury on May 1, 2024, and renewed for 2025 and 2026 per the LA City Ethics registry.

Mercury Public Affairs's LA City lobbying clients in the 2022-2026 period include Phillips 66 ($511,560), McDonald's Corporation ($270,000), Cellco Partnership doing business as Verizon Wireless ($225,000), Fox Corporation ($170,000), Arcadis US Inc. ($150,000), Los Angeles World Airports ($125,000 plus $110,000 via subcontract), the Los Angeles County Federation of Labor, AFL-CIO ($120,000), Valley Vista Services ($100,000), Los Angeles Community College District ($95,000), Deloitte Consulting LLP ($60,000), Meta Housing ($50,000), and others, per the LA City Ethics Commission's payments-to-lobbying-firms dataset.

Mercury Public Affairs was Airbnb's contract LA City lobbying firm from 2017 through 2019, with payments totaling $335,407 per the same dataset. The relationship was not active during the 2026 cycle, when Justin Wesson's in-house Airbnb registration replaced it. Airbnb's current contract lobbying firm at LA City level is M Strategic Communications.

The Mercury bundle to Bass

Between September 30, 2025 and October 30, 2025, nine Mercury Public Affairs employees made nine individual contributions to "Re-Elect Karen Bass for Mayor 2026" totaling $3,500. The timing is significant: Justin Wesson registered as an Airbnb lobbyist on August 1, 2025. The bundle to Bass arrived two months later.

The $3,500 total is small in the context of Bass's overall fundraising. As a cluster from a single employer, it is the largest single-employer concentration in her 2026 cycle by employee count, per the LA City Ethics Commission contributions dataset. Airbnb itself has contributed nothing to Bass at the LA City level in any cycle. Airbnb-affiliated donors did contribute to other LA councilmembers, including $2,600 to Tim McOsker, $2,300 to Ysabel Jurado, and $1,100 to Nithya Raman, who has been a public opponent of the budget provisions.

The relationship runs through the family. The money runs through the firm. The Mayor's policy lane runs through the budget.

Save Our Services and the coalition that isn't

Airbnb has publicly framed the budget provisions through a campaign called Save Our Services, which it describes as a coalition supporting tourism revenue for city services. Per LA Times reporting, Airbnb is the campaign's funder and coordinator. The coalition's name does not appear on the mailers as the sponsor; Airbnb's role was disclosed only after press inquiry.

Coalition members include Teamsters Joint Council 42, the Los Angeles/Orange Counties Building and Construction Trades Council, IATSE California Council, IUPAT District Council 36, Central City Association of Los Angeles, Valley Industry and Commerce Association, the Crenshaw Chamber of Commerce, the California Hispanic Chambers of Commerce, the CalAsian Chambers of Commerce, the Greater Los Angeles African American Chamber, NAACP California/Hawaii State Conference, the Brotherhood Crusade, the Koreatown Youth and Community Center, Cristo Viene Ministries, and EXPOSE. Airbnb has declined to disclose how much it has paid to fund the campaign.

Who's actually opposed

The hotel workers' union, UNITE HERE Local 11, has been the loudest organized opposition. Local 11 president Kurt Petersen, per LAist, called the deal "a ruse to build a larger short-term market, which means less housing for Angelenos in our city." Earlier reporting attributed to Petersen the framing that Airbnb is "bribing the city with $50 million in order to get an expansion of vacation rentals which is the last thing our city needs."

Three sitting LA City Councilmembers are on record opposed: Monica Rodriguez, Bob Blumenfield, and Nithya Raman, who is also a 2026 Mayoral primary challenger to Bass. Outside the Council, Better Neighbors LA organizer Noah Suarez-Sikes has been the consistent housing-advocate voice opposed to the deal.

The hotel-worker union and the building-trades unions are split. UNITE HERE Local 11 opposes the deal. The Building Trades, the Teamsters, and IATSE all sit in the Airbnb-funded coalition. The split is real and reflects the structural divide in the LA labor movement on tourism vs. housing.

Open questions

Karen Bass has not publicly addressed why a budget provision that the city's Planning Department is now being instructed to develop originated as a suggestion from a single corporate interest, or whether her office disclosed Airbnb's role in initiating both provisions to the Council.

Justin Wesson has not publicly addressed whether his prior decade of paid service on his father's Council staff, including his role as Floor Director, creates any conflict in his current capacity as Airbnb's lobbyist to that same Council.

Herb Wesson has not publicly addressed whether Mercury Public Affairs's California operation, which he co-chairs, has any current professional relationship with Airbnb at the state or federal level, or whether the timing of the Mercury employee bundle to Re-Elect Karen Bass for Mayor 2026 between September 30 and October 30, 2025 reflects coordinated bundling.

Mercury Public Affairs has not publicly addressed its current Airbnb client relationship, if any, at state or federal levels following the end of its LA City contract in 2019, or whether the family relationship between its California co-chair and Airbnb's in-house LA City lobbyist creates any internal conflict-of-interest policy considerations.
