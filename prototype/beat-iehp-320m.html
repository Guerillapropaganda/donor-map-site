The $320 million the Inland Empire kept.

In September 2025, the U.S. Department of Justice filed a False Claims Act lawsuit alleging the Inland Empire Health Plan held back $320 million in federal Medi-Cal expansion funds it was required to return. The smoking-gun email from then-CEO Bradley Gilbert is the central exhibit. The board that was supposed to oversee the money is composed of elected supervisors. Gilbert was later appointed by Governor Newsom to run the state agency that oversees Medi-Cal.

This is not a complicated story. A public health plan got federal Medi-Cal expansion money. The law required it to spend the money on the patients the expansion covered or return what it didn't spend. The plan did not return what it didn't spend. Per the U.S. Department of Justice, the plan instead routed the money through a structure that let it appear the funds had been spent on medical care when they had not. A whistleblower preserved an internal email from the plan's then-CEO that, the government alleges, says exactly that out loud.

The case

The Inland Empire Health Plan (IEHP) is a Joint Powers Agency that provides Medi-Cal coverage to approximately 1.6 million residents of Riverside and San Bernardino Counties, representing about thirty-five percent of the combined population of those two counties. It is the second-largest public health plan in California.

Per CalMatters reporting in September 2025, the United States Department of Justice filed a False Claims Act lawsuit alleging IEHP held back approximately $320 million in federal Medi-Cal expansion funds it was required to return. IEHP received approximately $3.5 billion in expansion funds under the Affordable Care Act. The agency had returned $30 million, which the federal complaint alleges falls $320 million short of what should have been returned.

The complaint alleges IEHP routed expansion funds through "the two county hospitals" so the money could be reported as medical costs even when it was used for non-medical purposes including attorneys, consultants, and technology vendors, and as bargaining-chip leverage in negotiations with regional health systems.

The email

The DOJ complaint quotes an internal email from then-IEHP CEO Bradley Gilbert that the government will introduce at trial as direct evidence of intent. Per the complaint:

"The funds have to flow through the two county hospitals from IEHP so I can get credit for them as medical costs or the dollars go back to the state."

The complaint alleges this email reflects the central scheme: route money through the county hospitals back to providers and to IEHP-controlled vendor accounts so the disbursements would book on IEHP's audited financials as medical expenditures, thereby avoiding the federal return requirement.

The CEO who became the regulator

Bradley P. Gilbert, MD, MPP served as IEHP's CEO from 2008 through July 2019, a tenure of more than eleven years. Under his leadership, IEHP membership grew from approximately 365,000 to more than 1.2 million members per PR Newswire reporting.

In 2020, after retiring from IEHP, Gilbert was appointed by Governor Gavin Newsom as Director of the California Department of Health Care Services, the state agency that administers California's Medi-Cal program. He held that position before transitioning to private-sector roles.

The federal complaint alleges fraud at the program Gilbert was later appointed to regulate from the state's regulatory chair.

The board: sitting supervisors with fiduciary duty

The IEHP Governing Board is comprised of four sitting county supervisors (two from Riverside County, two from San Bernardino County) and three appointed public members. The board has fiduciary duty over IEHP's operations. Members during the alleged-fraud period included:

The Affordable Care Act Medi-Cal expansion took effect January 2014. The period the DOJ alleges fraud occurred therefore largely overlaps with Spiegel's chairmanship (2021 through early 2025) and Hagman's prior chairmanship (2018-2020). Both are sitting elected officials currently in office. Williams has been on the board the entire 30-year window.

The donor-map angle

Spiegel and Gutierrez also receive money from Moving California Forward (FPPC #1455936), a Riverside-based political action committee funded by Inland Empire developers including Stronghold Power Systems, Highland Fairview, Lewis Management, Palm Communities, and Jeffrey Burum, the San Bernardino developer at the center of the Colonies corruption case. Per Cal-Access, Spiegel received $1,000 and Gutierrez received $9,500 from that committee in the 2026 cycle.

The PAC's treasurer files from the second floor of the Mission Inn Hotel & Spa in downtown Riverside. The full structural map is at /second-floor.

Hagman, meanwhile, sits on the California University of Science and Medicine Board of Trustees. IEHP committed $1.2 million annually to fund medical-school debt relief at CUSM for students who agree to serve in needy communities. The chair of one institution sits on the board of the other.

Open questions

Karen Spiegel has not publicly addressed what the IEHP Governing Board was told about the federal expansion-fund routing pattern during her chairmanship, what board minutes record about any audit or whistleblower concerns prior to the September 2025 DOJ filing, or how the fiduciary duty owed by sitting elected officials to the public health plan should be understood in light of the federal allegations.

Curt Hagman has not publicly addressed the IEHP-to-CUSM funding stream during his prior board chairmanship and his current chairmanship, in light of his concurrent service on the CUSM Board of Trustees.

Yxstian Gutierrez has not publicly addressed his fiduciary review of IEHP financials since joining the board in January 2023, his receipt of $9,500 from Moving California Forward during a period when IEHP was already under federal investigation, or his position on the lawsuit.

Bradley Gilbert has not publicly addressed the email quoted in the DOJ complaint or whether the routing structure described in the complaint continued in any form during his subsequent tenure as Director of the California Department of Health Care Services.
