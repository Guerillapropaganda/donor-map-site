Katie Porter's brand is built on rejecting the donor class. The whiteboard. The corporate accountability hearings. The pledge to refuse corporate PACs and Wall Street executives. The rule has narrow definitions. The receipts show wealthy individuals writing maximum legal checks anyway.

Porter is a Democratic candidate for California governor in the June 2026 primary. She represented California's 47th Congressional District in the U.S. House from 2019 to 2025. She ran for U.S. Senate in 2024 and finished third in the primary. Her central political brand is built on a fundraising rule: no corporate PACs, no donations from executives at the largest oil, drug, and Wall Street bank companies. The rule is real. Her 2018 House campaign raised $7.9 million from about 66,000 individual donors who gave an average of $68 each. By 2022 she was a top House Democratic fundraiser at more than $22 million, still on the same model.

The rule has limits. The pledge applies to corporate PACs and to executives at specific industries. It does not extend to individual investors, venture capitalists, hedge fund founders, crypto-industry founders, medical-device CEOs, or wealth-management banks. Each of those categories appears on Porter's publicly searchable Cal-Access donor list for the 2026 governor's race.

The five names above are not hidden. Cal-Access is California's official campaign-finance database. Anyone can search Porter for Governor 2026, committee number 1479597, and pull up the full list. The names above are pulled from that public record.

What the whiteboard rule actually says

Porter's fundraising pledge, repeated in her campaign emails and press appearances, has three pieces: no corporate PACs, no donations from executives at Big Oil, Big Pharma, or Wall Street banks, and no lobbyist donations. The pledge has been the spine of her national brand since 2018. It is the reason the small-dollar fundraising machine works the way it does. A donor who gives Porter $20 is buying into a story about politicians who refuse the donor class.

Each piece of the rule has a definition, and each definition has an edge. "Corporate PAC" means a political action committee registered to a corporation. It does not mean a wealthy individual who runs that corporation, gives from a personal account, and writes the check the same week. "Big Oil, Big Pharma, Wall Street banks" is a list of specific industries. It does not include venture capital, private equity, hedge funds, crypto exchanges, medical-device manufacturers, or wealth-management banks. "Lobbyist" means a registered federal lobbyist. It does not include lawyers, executives, board members, or major shareholders who hire lobbyists to do their lobbying for them.

The pledge is real. The receipts above pass through every part of it.

What passes through it

Six donors. Each one shows the shape of the rule's limits.

Christian Larsen. Co-founded Ripple Labs, the company behind the XRP cryptocurrency. Crypto-industry founder. Wrote Porter $39,200 in the 2026 cycle and also wrote $39,200 to the leading Republican in the same race. The same individual hedged across both party primaries. The "no corporate PAC" rule does not stop a crypto founder writing personal checks.

Joe Kiani. Founded Masimo Corporation, a publicly traded medical-device company. Personal stake in the company. Wrote Porter $39,200. The "no Big Pharma" piece of the rule covers pharmaceutical executives. It does not cover medical-device CEOs, even when their company sells equipment to the same hospitals that buy from drug companies.

Karla Jurvetson. One of the most prominent individual Democratic donors in California. Family fortune connected to Silicon Valley venture capital. Wrote Porter $40,100. Jurvetson funds progressive causes broadly. The point here is not that she is hostile to Porter's politics. The point is that the rule against "the donor class" defines the donor class narrowly enough that one of the largest individual Democratic donors in the state still passes through.

Seth Klarman. Founded Baupost Group, a hedge fund managing tens of billions of dollars. Forbes lists his estimated net worth in the billions. Wrote Porter $13,900 in cumulative federal contributions across her House and Senate campaigns. Documented in Porter's own master profile as one of the gaps in her "clean cash" claim. The hedge fund founder is not on the rejected list because the rejected list says "Wall Street banks," not "Wall Street."

Donald Mullen. Former Goldman Sachs partner. Ran the trading desk that profited from betting against the 2008 subprime mortgage collapse, the trade that became the central story of the financial crisis. Left Goldman in 2012 and founded a private investment firm. Wrote Porter $8,000 or more in cumulative federal contributions. Documented in Porter's own master profile. The "no Wall Street executive" rule covers people currently working at Wall Street banks. It does not cover former Goldman partners now running their own firms.

First Foundation Bank. A regional private bank specializing in wealth management for high-net-worth individuals. Porter's single largest direct organizational contribution in the 2026 cycle, at $57,200. The bank itself is not a Wall Street bank. Its business is managing the personal fortunes of the people who would be subject to a wealth tax.

Two of the six are documented in Porter's own master profile as known limits to the "clean cash" claim. The other four are pulled directly from her 2026 Cal-Access top-donor list. Anyone with a browser can verify them on the state's public website.

What that donor list would ask for

Porter's overall donor base has three big blocs. Each one has a different stake in the same set of state-level tax decisions a California governor would make.

Wealthy individuals · the donor class
$200K+ combined
Christian Larsen · Joe Kiani · Karla Jurvetson · Seth Klarman · Donald Mullen · First Foundation Bank
Their stake
Direct annual hit. A federal wealth tax above $50 million would apply to most of these donors every year. Hedge fund founders, crypto-industry founders, and venture-fortune individuals are who a wealth tax targets.
What they want
No wealth tax. No carried-interest reform. No capital-gains hike. No new SEC rules on private capital. The exact set of policy positions an "anti-corporate" candidate would presumably oppose.

The contradiction is structural, not personal. Porter's small-dollar base and her industrial-union endorsements push her one way. Her individual maximum-level donors push her the other. The wealth tax is the cleanest single test of where the loyalty actually lands because it is a policy where the two camps cannot both get what they want.

The labor counterweight

Porter's labor coalition is the broadest of any candidate in the 2026 California governor's race. The Teamsters of California endorsed her for governor. UAW Region 6, representing more than 100,000 workers, was the first major union to endorse. The National Union of Healthcare Workers, the Amalgamated Transit Union, the Sheet Metal and Air Workers, and an IBEW local also endorsed her. The California Federation of Labor endorsed her too, though that was a shared endorsement with three other Democratic candidates.

Industrial-union members are not the donor class. They are the people on whose behalf the donor class is supposed to be opposed. The labor endorsements are the strongest signal that Porter's politics are real. Industrial unions did not endorse Eric Swalwell or Antonio Villaraigosa. They endorsed her.

That is why the question of the wealthy individual donors is interesting, not damning. It is not a story of a politician selling out. It is a story of a fundraising rule that defines the rejected class narrowly enough that the contradiction becomes survivable: industrial-union members get the public statements about consumer protection and corporate accountability, and a smaller pocket of wealthy individual donors get a candidate who is unlikely to push the actual structural policy that would touch their fortunes.

Why a statewide run tightens this

Two structural pressures make this worse for Porter, not better, as the 2026 governor's race progresses.

The donor universe gets wealthier. A 47th-district House race in Orange County is mostly a small-dollar grassroots base. A statewide run for California governor needs to grow into a much bigger donor pool, and the pool that has $39,200 to spare lives in San Francisco, Silicon Valley, and Beverly Hills. The marginal new max-level donor is more likely to be a Karla Jurvetson type than a Wisconsin teacher. The pressure points the wrong way exactly when Porter most needs to grow the donor list.

Fairshake's $116 million war chest is already pre-positioned against her. The crypto industry's federal super-PAC spent more than $10 million against Porter in the 2024 Senate primary. She finished third. The same PAC has $116 million sitting in reserve for the 2026 cycle. If Porter leans hard into wealth-tax framing or any policy that touches private investor wealth, the spend against her goes up. Soft-pedal in the general lets her survive the crypto attack. The donor pressure and the opposition pressure point the same direction.

For context

The crypto industry's $10 million message

Fairshake PAC, funded primarily by Coinbase ($75 million), Andreessen Horowitz ($60 million-plus), and Ripple ($50 million), spent more than $10 million against Porter in the 2024 California Senate primary. She finished third. The PAC holds about $116 million in reserve for the 2026 cycle.

The donor class does not spend $116 million against politicians who do not threaten them. The opposition spending is itself the strongest evidence that Porter's politics are real. The spending also tells her exactly what costs her votes if she pushes harder on industry-touching policy.

Why this matters

A wealth tax is not a hypothetical. Multiple federal proposals have been introduced in the U.S. Senate and House over the past five years. State-level wealth taxes have been proposed in California specifically, including a 2023 bill in the State Assembly that would have applied to fortunes above $50 million. The next California governor would have to decide whether to back, oppose, or remain silent on the next iteration.

The point of this beat is not to predict what Porter will do. The point is to make the donor map legible. Wealthy individuals who would owe a wealth tax are on her donor list. Industrial unions whose members would benefit from one are also on it. Both blocs have writing contributions to the same campaign. Whichever direction she lands, the contradiction has receipts.

The donor class does not spend $39,200 on candidates who will tax them every year. The whiteboard rule lets Porter take the money without that being obvious. The wealth-tax test is one of the few policy questions where the contradiction would have to resolve.

What this beat asserts. Six named donors are on Porter's publicly searchable donor list. Each is wealthy enough that a federal wealth tax would apply to them. The "no corporate PAC, no Wall Street executive" rule has narrow definitions that admit them.

What this beat does not assert. No claim is made about Porter's stated position on a specific wealth-tax bill. No claim is made that any of these donors are personally hostile to her policy goals. Karla Jurvetson, in particular, is a long-time progressive donor across many campaigns, and her position on a wealth tax is not the subject of this beat. The subject is the structure of the donor list, not the politics of any individual donor.
