Rick Caruso lost the 2022 Los Angeles mayor's race to Karen Bass by nearly 10 points. He spent more than $100 million of his own money. After the January 2025 Palisades fire, he founded Steadfast LA. By April 2025 he was on stage with Bass announcing a public-private partnership to rebuild the Palisades. By 2026 his organization had originated three documented policy interventions in Bass's rebuild apparatus: a $40 million city recreation-center handover, a documentary-transfer-tax exemption for Palisades homeowners that Bass took to City Council, and an AI permitting tool now embedded in the city's permit-review workflow. His organization has not given a single dollar to her 2026 reelection campaign or to either of her independent expenditure committees.

That last fact matters. The structural beat would be easier to write if Caruso were quietly funding Bass while she signed off on his projects. He is not. The donor-money story does not exist. What exists is something that is harder to make snappy and more interesting once you see it. Caruso is not influencing the rebuild by donating to the politician who runs it. He is influencing the rebuild by running the private organization that the politician has officially partnered with to deliver it.

The candidate she beat is now her main private partner. The mayor who won is the mayor who delivers. The apparatus that connects them was founded by the loser. The policy outcomes are increasingly shaped by the loser's organization. The election outcome was that one of them won. The governance outcome is that they are running the rebuild together.

The origin · 2022 to 2025

Rick Caruso is the developer behind The Grove, the Americana at Brand, and the Palisades Village shopping center. His 2022 mayoral campaign was funded substantially out of his own pocket. He lost to Karen Bass on a closing ad cycle that emphasized homelessness and public safety. He has not held public office.

The Palisades fire began January 7, 2025, eventually destroying or damaging thousands of structures across Pacific Palisades, Altadena, and Malibu. Caruso's own development, Palisades Village, suffered partial damage. Caruso publicly criticized Bass's emergency response in the immediate aftermath. He told the LA Times on February 3, 2025: "This has nothing to do about politics, and I will gladly have her be the hero, because this is all about the thousands and thousands of people that are displaced." He launched Steadfast LA the same day, with himself as founder and chair, Najla Kayyem (a former Caruso executive) as Executive Director, and a 25-person coalition of LA business and civic figures.

Two months later, on April 10, 2025, Bass and Caruso appeared together at a joint press event to announce a public-private partnership for the Palisades Recreation Center rebuild. The relationship from that point forward is documented in city records, mayoral press releases, and the board approvals required to transfer privately-funded city assets back into the city's care.

Three documented policy interventions

Steadfast LA's role is described publicly as a rebuild accelerator. The specific interventions documented in primary sources are concrete, narrow, and policy-shaping. Each one is a place where the organization has substituted itself for, supplemented, or steered a city function.

Palisades Recreation Center
≈ $40 MILLION
What is happening
A $40 million donor-funded rebuild of the Palisades Recreation Center, structured as a city asset donation. The named donor is "PPRC Development Project, LLC," whose contact on the city paperwork is Nick Geller, listed at ngeller@steadfastla.com. The architects are Gensler and SWA Group; Gensler's CEO Andy Cohen sits on the Steadfast LA coalition.
Why it matters structurally
A private organization is delivering a major public asset under city approval and acceptance terms. The Recreation and Parks Board report notes that privately funded elements would be ineligible for FEMA reimbursement and that the city would withdraw those elements from FEMA-funded work. The policy decision of whether the city accepts the asset, on what terms, and how it interfaces with FEMA recovery is now a Steadfast-shaped question.
Measure ULA exemption proposal
CITY TAX POLICY
What is happening
On October 24, 2025, a City Council motion was introduced citing a letter from Mayor Bass that "followed a proposal presented by Steadfast LA" to create a one-time Measure ULA documentary-transfer-tax exemption or rebate for Pacific Palisades homeowners. Mayor Bass's release confirms she met with Caruso about the proposal. The proposal is to give Palisades fire survivors relief from Measure ULA's 4% / 5.5% transfer tax on property sales over $5 million / $10 million.
Why it matters structurally
The city's tax-policy move was authored as a Steadfast LA proposal that the mayor adopted and forwarded to council. Measure ULA was passed by LA voters in 2022 to fund affordable housing and homelessness prevention through a tax on high-value property sales. A Palisades exemption is structurally a hole in the same tax revenue base. The proposal route is private to public, not public to public.
Archistar AI permitting tool
CITY VENDOR · ZERO COST
What is happening
Steadfast LA funded Archistar's "eCheck" AI permitting tool and made it available to the City and County of Los Angeles at no cost. Mayor Bass's office announced on November 13, 2025 that Archistar's eCheck AI Pilot is "available at no cost to homeowners and design professionals" for pre-submission building-plan review.
Why it matters structurally
A city function (permit review) is now partly executed by a private vendor selected and funded by a private organization, deployed at no cost to the city, embedded in the rebuild permitting flow. The vendor decision was Steadfast LA's. The integration with city processes is a city decision that followed. The dependency runs from city to private apparatus.

Three policy interventions. One private organization. One mayor's administration. Zero campaign donations linking the two. The relationship is not transactional. It is structural.

The donor question · zero direct contributions

A donor-map analysis of LA Ethics Commission campaign-finance filings tested whether any of the 25 named Steadfast LA participants have personally donated to Mayor Karen Bass's 2026 reelection committee or to either of her two independent expenditure committees ("Unidos con Karen Bass" and "Vecinos Unidos por Bass"). It also tested donations to the three other 2026 LA mayoral candidates: Spencer Pratt, Nithya Raman, and Adam Miller.

The exact-name search returned zero contributions from any named Steadfast LA participant to any of those six committees. Rick Caruso has not given to Bass. He has not given to Pratt, Raman, or Miller. Andy Cohen has not given. Carey Smith has not given. Jared Wolff has not given. Twenty-five names checked. Zero direct contributions. The donor-money explanation for the alignment between Steadfast LA and Bass's mayor's office does not exist.

Corporate-cluster searches do find money from people who work at Steadfast-affiliated companies. Disney-employed contributors gave Bass $8,000, Raman $9,844, Pratt $1,885, and Miller $464. Netflix-employed contributors gave Bass $5,700 and Raman $6,971. Amazon-employed contributors gave Bass $2,000 and Raman $3,728. Gensler-employed contributors gave Raman $300 and Pratt $150. These are individual employees giving small amounts spread across multiple candidates. They are not the named board CEOs writing personal checks. They are not corporate PAC contributions. They are background noise.

The structural connection between Steadfast LA and Bass's mayor's office does not run through campaign donations. It runs through the three policy interventions documented above. The apparatus is the relationship.

The shared address with the wealth-tax coalition

One more administrative finding deserves mention because it cross-connects this beat to the donor map's other current investigation. The IRS record for Steadfast LA lists the organization's care-of address as 2350 Kerner Boulevard, Suite 250, San Rafael, California 94901-5596, with David Lazarus as the contact.

Building a Better California, the political action committee that Sergey Brin, Stewart Resnick, Christian Larsen, and roughly six other named billionaires have funded with a reported $35 million to defeat California's 2026 Billionaire Tax Act, is administered out of the same building. Cal-Access Form 497 Filing 3139587 lists "Building a Better California Incorporated" at 2350 Kerner Blvd., Suite 250, San Rafael CA 94901, alongside an entity called Compass4 Inc at the same suite, as the two intermediaries through which Brin's $9 million April 10, 2026 contribution flowed.

The shared address is administratively normal in California political finance. Lazarus & Associates is a long-established political treasurer firm that services many statewide committees. Many committees use one treasurer firm. The administrative point is not the conspiracy. The administrative point is the colocation. The same operational apparatus that handles the $35 million billionaire tax-defense effort also handles the private rebuild apparatus that is currently the lead public-private partner to Mayor Bass on the Palisades fire reconstruction.

What Caruso has said publicly

Caruso has been on record about his own position. The donor map quotes him directly to avoid framing his stated motivations.

To the LA Times on February 3, 2025, the day Steadfast LA launched: "This has nothing to do about politics, and I will gladly have her be the hero, because this is all about the thousands and thousands of people that are displaced."

Also on February 3, 2025: "If Steadfast's work helps Bass come out as a great mayor of the city for moving this along, I'll be thrilled."

Both quotes are on record. They do not change the structural facts documented above, but they do clarify the publicly-stated intent. Caruso says the motivation is rebuild, not politics. The structural alignment described in this piece is the receipt of what has happened, not a claim about what Caruso wants.

The signal

The donor-money frame for political accountability assumes that influence runs through campaign contributions. Donor gives money to candidate. Candidate becomes officeholder. Officeholder makes decisions favorable to donor. Many investigations the donor map publishes follow that frame because the receipts are public and the math is clean.

The Steadfast LA case shows a second route that the receipt-based donor-money frame misses. An organization whose board is composed of CEOs of architecture, engineering, banking, real estate, technology, and entertainment firms can shape policy without writing campaign checks at all. The intervention happens through structural participation in the public process: through donor-funded city projects, through proposed tax-policy carve-outs that the mayor's office adopts and forwards, through technology vendors who become embedded in city workflows. The campaign-contribution receipt is not the receipt. The policy receipt is the receipt.

The candidate Karen Bass beat in 2022 is not her financial backer in 2026. He is running a parallel rebuild government. The mayor administers it. The voters will eventually decide whether they think that arrangement should continue.

No one named in this piece has been charged with any crime. Steadfast LA's activities described here are operationally legal under California public-private partnership rules. The structural pattern, not the legal status, is what this investigation maps.
