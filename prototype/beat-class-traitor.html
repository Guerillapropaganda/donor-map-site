$31 million to bury a class traitor.

California's donor class organized against him: utility, realtors, chamber, developers, prison guards. The only billionaire in the race willing to break with them.

Anti-Steyer money flow · 2026 California Governor primary

Five industries. One committee. $21,025,000 raised to bury one candidate. The 2026 California Governor's primary, mapped from FPPC primary-source filings.

Tom Steyer is a billionaire. In American politics, that usually means his money is the story. In California's 2026 Governor's primary, his money isn't the story. The donor class organizing against him is.

Steyer has personally contributed $133.8 million to his own campaign committee. That figure is Cal-Access primary-source verified, 41 separate contribution events from his own checkbook. By every reasonable metric, he should be running away with the race. He isn't. The latest poll has him at 15 percent, in third place. He hasn't moved in two weeks while every other major candidate has.

The reason is sitting in the FPPC committee filings. A coalition of California's biggest regulated industries has put $21 million into a single committee whose entire purpose is defeating Tom Steyer. The members: utilities, realtors, the chamber of commerce, developers, and the prison guards union.

Who's funding the anti-Steyer campaign

The committee is named, with the strange transparency California requires of opposition committees, "California Is Not For Sale, NO ON Steyer for Governor 2026." That suffix isn't optional. State law requires any committee primarily formed against a specific candidate to disclose the target in its registered name. So when you see "NO ON [CANDIDATE]" in a committee's title, you're looking at the political-money equivalent of a hit list.

Pacific Gas & Electric Corporation is the single largest donor. PG&E pleaded guilty in 2020 to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter for the Camp Fire, the deadliest wildfire in California history. The same utility is now spending more than $10 million to defeat the only major candidate in the race running on utility-and-climate accountability.

The structure runs through two committees, not one. PG&E gave directly to a committee called "Californians for Resilient and Affordable Energy, NO ON Steyer for Governor 2026" (FPPC #1490270). That committee then transferred $8 million of its $10.05 million to the larger spending committee, where the rest of the donor class also dropped its money. Two committees in series. The legal infrastructure is handled by two different compliance firms. Nielsen Merksamer files for the upstream funnel. Deane & Company files for the downstream spending committee. They're working the same target.

What the money is buying

The committee has reported approximately $13.9 million in actual independent expenditures against Steyer through April 17. TV spots, mailers, digital advertising, opposition research. The remaining $7 million sits in the committee, ready to spend in the final weeks before the June 2 primary.

Among the verified expenditures: $30,000 paid to David Binder Research, Inc. of San Francisco on April 2 for a survey filed under the description "Survey/Oppose/Tom Steyer/Governor/Statewide." That is California's regulated-industry coalition paying for private opposition polling against a single candidate. The Camp Fire utility, the realtors, the chamber, and the developers want to know which lines of attack work best.

A regulated monopoly with billions in rate-payer revenue is using that money to choose the governor who picks its regulators. That's the structural definition of corporate capture.

The polling layer

The opposition coalition is not just running TV ads. It is also generating the polling that justifies the TV ads. Two committees, two pollsters, two layers of the same operation.

Layer one is opposition message-testing. Filing 3138008 records the $30,000 the anti-Steyer committee paid David Binder Research, Inc. on April 2 with the description "Survey/Oppose/Tom Steyer/Governor/Statewide." The subvendors on the Schedule G of the same filing are KGS Research and Political Data Inc., the dominant California voter-file vendor. This is a full survey project with sampling and weighting, not a focus group. It is paid opposition research dressed up as polling so the coalition can find out which attacks work before it spends the remaining $7 million.

Layer two is public-narrative polling. A second pollster, Gudelunas Strategies, has released two polls during the same window, both showing Becerra surging into the top tier of the field. The Sacramento Bee identified the Gudelunas sponsor as an anti-Steyer independent expenditure backing Becerra, with the same coalition stack: PG&E, the California Realtors, the Building Industry Association, the Chamber of Commerce, IBEW Local 1245, the prison guards.

The Gudelunas numbers diverge from the rest of the polling field in a specific direction. Same field dates, different pollsters, the candidate the donor coalition prefers polls eleven points higher than the consensus:

The pattern is operational. The Gudelunas releases timed immediately after Swalwell's exit on April 12 and Yee's withdrawal-and-Steyer-endorsement on April 20. The narrative effect is to consolidate non-Steyer Democratic voters behind the candidate the donor coalition prefers, because his policy platform is less aggressive toward PG&E and corporate property taxes than Steyer's.

The companies that don't want Steyer to win are paying for the polls that say Steyer is losing. The polls aren't measuring the race. The polls are part of the race.

The same firm works for both sides of the same race. David Binder Research is the firm taking the $30,000 to oppose Steyer. The same firm is also being paid $50,000 by Eric Swalwell's gubernatorial campaign for pro-Swalwell polling. The same firm is the official tracker for the California Democratic Party's VOTER Index. While doing all three contracts, the firm continues to release public horserace polls on the same race that anyone can read in the news. Three paying clients, three different angles on the same primary, no FPPC complaint on file.

This is legal. California's disclosure regime makes the conflict visible in the filings. It does not prohibit the conflict. The disclosure shows up in a two-hundred-page filing on a state website almost nobody reads. The poll shows up on TV with a headline. The gap between technically disclosed and actually visible to the public is where the entire polling industry operates.

The structural problem. Approximately one in four California governor polls released this cycle came from Democratic-aligned firms with no disclosed sponsor. The poll aggregators that average all the polls together do not down-weight anonymous-sponsor work. Every undisclosed-sponsor poll lands in the average with the same statistical weight as a public-interest PPIC or Berkeley IGS release. The coalition does not need to forge the polls. It only needs to commission enough of them.

Why this is class war, not just opposition spending

Every candidate has donors. Most candidates serve their donor class quietly, on policy after policy, vote after vote, until the donor class is satisfied and the campaign returns the favor. The system runs smoothly when politicians stay loyal to the class that funded them.

The system breaks down when a politician refuses to play. Particularly one who comes from the donor class himself. Steyer's policy positions on utility accountability, fossil-fuel divestment, housing affordability, and labor protections threaten the operating interests of every industry on that funder list. He is, in the language his opposition will not say out loud, a class traitor.

And the donor class punishes class traitors. Not with disagreement. Not with policy debate. With $21 million in opposition spending, organized through two coordinated committees and run by two of the largest political-compliance firms in California. The point isn't to beat him in an argument. The point is to bury him before voters get to weigh in.

The polling is telling them it's working.

Open questions

The Cal-Access record names every funder above. There are still unanswered questions:

The "California Is Not For Sale" committee names its sponsor as "Business Owners and Concerned Citizens." That is generic placeholder language. It conceals which specific business owners signed the original organizing paperwork. The principal officers behind that phrase are not disclosed at the primary-source level. Piercing that veil requires a public-records request to the FPPC.
