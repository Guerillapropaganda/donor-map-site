§ 1 · The hedge bets

Donors funding two or more candidates in the same race.

Most named donors pick a candidate and stop. The names below picked more than one. Some are same-party hedges (a Silicon Valley operator backing two competing Democrats). Some are cross-party hedges (a Riverside developer backing a Republican AND a Democrat). The pattern signals what the donor actually wants: an outcome that's neutral on partisan label, specific on policy.

§ 2 · The industry wars

Money flowing in opposite directions on a single industry.

An industry war is what happens when one industry's money is unified behind a candidate AND another industry's money is unified against that same candidate. Below are five active wars in the 2026 California governor race. Each one is structurally the same: pick a side, follow the money on both sides.

War 1 · Energy + utilities vs. Tom Steyer

The utility monopoly is funding $10 million against the climate billionaire.

California's investor-owned utilities and the trades that depend on them have organized the largest single opposition campaign of the 2026 race. The vehicle is "Californians Against Tom Steyer," a coalition IE committee. Steyer is the only billionaire in the race who built his pre-political brand on closing fossil-fuel plants. The donor class has decided he is the one to bury.
Funded · pro-Steyer
$133.8M
Self-funded by the candidate's personal fortune. Outside spending in support is small relative to self-fund.

Opposed · anti-Steyer
$10M+
PG&E Corporation $9.975M · IBEW Local 1245 $75K · plus realtor, chamber, building industry, and prison-guard money on the same side.
Full breakdown: /class-traitor · the $31M-to-bury-a-class-traitor story.
War 2 · Oil and gas vs. the Democratic field

Five oil majors, one Democratic candidate taking their money.

Antonio Villaraigosa signed a 2018 public pledge not to take money from oil companies. By 2026 he is the principal Democratic beneficiary of fossil-fuel money in the gubernatorial race. The pattern is a singleton: the rest of the Democratic field has stayed off oil-industry checks. Becerra accepted one Chevron max contribution; the rest declined.

War 3 · Silicon Valley billionaire class vs. labor-backed Dems

Tech VCs put $8M+ behind a single mayor's IE PAC.

The Silicon Valley billionaire class has unified around Matt Mahan as their state-level vehicle. The Mahan IE PAC ("Back to Basics California") is funded by a who's-who of unicorn-builders. The candidates this money runs against, structurally, are the labor-backed Democrats (Becerra, Porter) on tech-regulation, AI safety, and public-sector compensation policy.

War 4 · AI policy · the Common Sense lobby vs. AI investors

Two leading candidates. Two opposite AI policy stakes.

Tom Steyer's brother Jim runs Common Sense Media, the named advocate behind California's three biggest AI bills (AB-1064, AB-1709, AB-2023). Steve Hilton's wife Rachel Whetstone heads communications at Sierra Technologies, a $10B AI company. Steyer's family has donated $5M+ to the lobby pushing the bills. Hilton owns more than $1M of Sierra equity. Same race, opposite stakes, both candidates have a family member professionally inside the AI-policy ecosystem.

War 5 · Police unions hedging across the GOP-and-D divide

PORAC dual-endorsed Bianco AND Villaraigosa. Same money, both sides.

The Peace Officers Research Association of California (PORAC) made a deliberate cross-party endorsement decision in the 2026 governor's race. The same money, from the same union, flows to a Republican Riverside sheriff AND to a Democratic former Los Angeles mayor. The signal: California's largest law-enforcement organization is hedging the same way every other industry hedges. Whichever side wins the runoff, law enforcement has a door.

§ 3 · The aggregation layer

One source. Counted as many.

A candidate's "donor count" only means what it sounds like if every donor is independent. The clusters below are places where the count is misleading — multiple LLCs sharing an address, an entire C-suite of one company giving on the same two days, a family making the same six-figure bet split across legally distinct people. Every donor below is real. The independence between them is what's missing.

Bianco · Sibling-managed LLCs

M&D Development + Downs Energy

$156,800
Two California LLCs, both at the same Corona address (1296 Magnolia Ave per CPUC service-list filings 2019, 2020). Mike Downs publicly described as managing partner at M&D Development by Bill Essayli (2023). Sibling Sherry Messner is M&D's responsible agent.
If FPPC treats them as one source under the common-control rule, the contribution exceeds the legal limit by approximately $117,600. SF Chronicle floated the question March 31, 2026.
Hilton · Single-employer C-suite cluster

Lighthouse Worldwide Solutions Oregon

~$196,000
Six employees of a single Oregon-based corporation gave Hilton lockstep contributions on the same two days in December 2025. Tae Yun Kim (chairman), Paul Newman (president), Thomas Saunders (SVP), Scott Salton (manager), Michael Chunhan (engineer), each at $39,200. The corporation itself gave nothing.
The classic "corporate cluster" giving pattern: company can't legally give above the limit, so the C-suite gives individually on coordinated dates. Looks like six donors, reads as one company.
Mahan · Crypto-industry corporate cluster

Coinbase senior leadership

$578,400+
Brian Armstrong (Coinbase CEO) $500,000 to Mahan IE PAC + Elena Nadolinski (former Coinbase, now Beam founder) $78,400. Same-cycle giving. The corporation does not appear directly; the unified crypto-industry posture flows through individuals.
The federal counterpart, Fairshake PAC, spent $10M against Porter in 2024 — same crypto-industry money operating across federal-state-and-IE layers with consistent candidate selection.
Bianco · Real-estate family cluster

Haagen Company family

$78,400
Alexander Haagen III + Betty Haagen (spouse) + Haagen Company LLC contributing in coordinated max increments to Bianco. Same family also gave to Nathan Hochman (LA District Attorney 2024) on the same model.
Family-plus-company structure: the holding company and the named individuals each max out, doubling or tripling the effective contribution from a single household.
Becerra · Physician-owned hospital cluster

Cantu / DHR Health network

$47,300
Same-day giving on June 12, 2025: Alonzo Cantu $39,200 + Carlos Garcia-Cantu MD $1,000 + Leonel Cantu MD $5,000 + Melissa Cantu-Garza PA $2,100. All four affiliated with DHR Health, a physician-owned Texas hospital network.
Mirrors Cantu's 2008 Hillary Clinton fundraising model from the same physician network. Healthcare-industry money routed through individual physicians and family members rather than corporate-PAC giving.
Steyer · Self-fund as cluster

Self-funded billionaire campaigns

$133.8M
Tom Steyer's 2026 campaign is 99.4% self-funded out of his personal fortune. The campaign reports 131 named donors but every meaningful dollar comes from a single individual. Same pattern in his 2020 presidential campaign.
The inverse aggregation problem: instead of one source counted as many donors, one source IS the donor. The "donor count" of 131 obscures that the funding source is one person.

What this story does not say

The donor map shows what is on the public record. It does not assert any of the following:

  • Not corruption. Every dollar above is publicly disclosed under California's campaign-finance regime. Public disclosure is the system working as designed. Documenting structural conflict is not the same as documenting wrongdoing.
  • Not a quid pro quo claim. Cross-candidate giving and industry-vs-candidate giving are common patterns in California politics. We are documenting the patterns, not asserting that any individual contribution purchased any individual policy outcome.
  • Not equivalence. A self-funded billionaire's $134M is structurally different from a labor union's max contribution, and both are different from a single LLC at the contribution limit. The rows in the table are not comparable on a single axis. Each row is its own story.
  • Not exhaustive. This is a curated map of the most editorially significant flows. Top-fifteen donor lists per candidate are in the per-candidate dossiers; the full Cal-Access data is publicly searchable.
