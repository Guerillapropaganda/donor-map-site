§ 0 · How to read this map

Each box is a candidate. Each circle is a donor. The lines show who paid whom.

Eight people are running for California governor in 2026. The map plots the 60 named donors who have written the largest checks. When the same donor pays more than one candidate, a line crosses the field. When two industries fund opposite sides, the colors collide. The shape of the room is the story.

§ 2 · The industry wars

Money flowing in opposite directions on a single industry.

An industry war is what happens when one industry's money is unified behind a candidate AND another industry's money is unified against that same candidate. Below are five active wars in the 2026 California governor race. Each one is structurally the same: pick a side, follow the money on both sides.

War 1 · The donor-class coalition vs. Tom Steyer

Five regulated industries plus the prison-guard PAC are funding $30 million+ against the climate billionaire.

California's investor-owned utilities, the largest trade groups, the developer lobby, and the prison-guard PAC have organized the largest single opposition campaign of the 2026 race. The vehicle is two coordinated FPPC committees: 1490270 (Californians for Resilient and Affordable Energy, No on Steyer for Governor 2026) and 1489677 (California is Not for Sale, No on Steyer for Governor 2026). Steyer is the only billionaire in the race who built his pre-political brand on holding utilities accountable for climate disasters. The donor class has decided he is the one to bury.

§ 3 · The aggregation layer

One source. Counted as many.

A candidate's "donor count" only means what it sounds like if every donor is independent. The clusters below are places where the count is misleading — multiple LLCs sharing an address, an entire C-suite of one company giving on the same two days, a family making the same six-figure bet split across legally distinct people. Every donor below is real. The independence between them is what's missing.

Bianco · Linked Downs-family Corona entities

M&D Development + Downs Energy

$156,800
Two California entities filed at related Corona addresses. Per a City of Corona staff report, M&D Development LLC is the owner-operator of Downs Energy. California SOS Bizfile lists Michael J. Downs as Downs Energy's CEO and Sharon Messner as its secretary/CFO. Downs Energy's cardlock fueling and lubricants assets were acquired by SC Fuels (a Pilot Travel Centers subsidiary) on October 1, 2025.
If FPPC treats the two contributions as one source under the common-control rule, the household contribution exceeds the legal limit by approximately $117,600. SF Chronicle floated the question March 31, 2026. The "sibling-managed" framing is in the press reporting; primary records support the officer overlap but not independently the family relationship.

↑ Wait, why is Matt Mahan's row $0?

Mahan hasn't started a candidate fundraising committee. The $43 million on his side belongs to a separate group he is legally barred from coordinating with.

Most candidates open a "candidate-controlled committee" the day they declare. The candidate raises money into it directly, lists it on the website, and the contribution limit is $39,200 per donor. That's the column the table above is measuring. Mahan has not opened one for the 2026 governor's race. So his row on a candidate-committee table is a real, accurate $0.

The $43 million on Mahan's side is in a different kind of group: an independent expenditure committee called Back to Basics California. Under California campaign-finance law that kind of committee can take unlimited contributions from a single person, but it legally cannot coordinate with the candidate or his staff. The candidate cannot tell it what to do. The committee writes its own ads, picks its own targets, and reports its own spending.

So Mahan's name is on the campaign, but the money on his side belongs to the outside committee, not to him. The 61 people who wrote into that committee are the Silicon Valley founder-and-investor class: Sequoia chairman Michael Moritz wrote $2,000,000. Y Combinator partner Michael Seibel wrote $1,000,000. Stripe CEO Patrick Collison wrote $990,000. Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong wrote $500,000. Pritzker family heir John Pritzker wrote $500,000.

Every other candidate in the table runs a normal candidate committee that voters can write small-dollar checks into. Mahan does not. The donor base IS the candidate.

§ 5 · Why this matters

The map does not show corruption. It shows incentives. And incentives explain messaging.

When the same donors fund multiple outcomes, you do not get clear positions. You get flexible ones. The candidate-by-candidate stories show that flexibility. The map shows where it comes from.

The argument is not that anyone in the table is uniquely inconsistent. The argument is that the system produces this behavior. A candidate funded by industries with overlapping but contradictory regulatory interests cannot commit to a clear position. The receipts make ambiguity rational.

When the same donors fund both sides, those donors do not need either side to win. They need the policy outcome. That is what the lines crossing the field show. Same donors → flexible positions. Different donors → different positions. The receipts pick which version of the candidate is structurally available.

Six of the eight candidates have zero voter-facing outside money against them. Mahan has $61,000 of research and polling from a public-sector-labor coalition (no ads). Steyer has more than thirty million dollars from a coalition of regulated industries.

The funders of that anti-Steyer coalition are the industries with the most regulatory exposure to a state government that no longer takes their money. PG&E pleaded guilty to 84 counts of involuntary manslaughter for the 2018 Camp Fire; Steyer ran on utility accountability. The Chamber of Commerce, the Realtors, the Building Industry Association, and the prison-guards' PAC each have specific regulatory interests that depend on which Democrat wins.

When the same donors fund both sides, those donors do not need either side to win. When the same donors attack only one side, those donors need that one side to lose. Six candidates have $0 against them. One has thirty million. That is what the map shows.

What this story does not say

The donor map shows what is on the public record. It does not assert any of the following:

  • Not corruption. Every dollar above is publicly disclosed under California's campaign-finance regime. Public disclosure is the system working as designed. Documenting structural conflict is not the same as documenting wrongdoing.
  • Not a quid pro quo claim. Cross-candidate giving and industry-vs-candidate giving are common patterns in California politics. We are documenting the patterns, not asserting that any individual contribution purchased any individual policy outcome.
  • Not equivalence. A self-funded billionaire's $134M is structurally different from a labor union's max contribution, and both are different from a single LLC at the contribution limit. The rows in the table are not comparable on a single axis. Each row is its own story.
  • Not exhaustive. This is a curated map of the most editorially significant flows. Top-fifteen donor lists per candidate are in the per-candidate dossiers; the full Cal-Access data is publicly searchable.
