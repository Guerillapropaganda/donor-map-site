Ken Calvert's $100 million in earmarks.

Calvert chairs the House Defense Appropriations Subcommittee. He has been a real estate business owner in Riverside County for seventeen years. Since 2021, he has secured more than $100 million in federal Community Project Funding for his district. Multiple earmarks land within three miles of properties he owns. The 2007 precedent shows what the playbook looks like: ask the House Ethics Committee for permission first.

Ken Calvert has been doing this for twenty years. The pattern is not a single self-dealing episode. It is a documented, congressional-ethics-tested practice in which federal earmarks for transportation, infrastructure, and economic development projects land in Riverside County near land the congressman owns. In 2007 he asked the House Ethics Committee whether he could submit one such earmark. The Committee said yes if other local businesses benefited too. He has been operating on that template ever since.

Who Calvert is

Ken Calvert is the Republican U.S. Representative for California's 41st Congressional District, which covers most of central Riverside County including the cities of Corona, Norco, Lake Elsinore, Murrieta, Menifee, and parts of Temecula. Per his own biography, he is a "lifelong resident of Riverside County and 17-year small business owner in the restaurant and real estate industries." He is chair of the House Defense Appropriations Subcommittee, which drafts the United States Department of Defense annual budget.

Calvert was elected to Congress in 1992. He has run for re-election every two years since. In 2024, he defeated Democratic challenger Will Rollins by a narrow margin. Rollins is widely expected to run again in 2026.

$100 million in earmarks since 2021

Congressional earmarks, technically known as "Community Project Funding" under the modern rules adopted in 2021, are directed federal appropriations for specific local projects. Per End Citizens United's 2024 complaint to the Office of Congressional Ethics, since Community Project Funding was reinstated in 2021, Calvert has secured more than $100 million in earmarks for projects in his Riverside County district.

Two specific earmarks named in the ECU complaint are most clearly linked to land Calvert owns:

  • $2 million for widening a bridge along Magnolia Avenue in Riverside County
  • $9 million for improvements along Interstate 15 in the Corona/Norco corridor

Both projects fall within three miles of Calvert's rental properties at 1210 and 1212 East 6th Street, Corona, California. Per the ECU complaint, Calvert failed to disclose the purchase of those properties on his congressional financial disclosures, an omission that, if confirmed, would constitute a violation of the Ethics in Government Act.

The properties

Calvert's congressional financial disclosures, available through the U.S. House Clerk's Public Disclosure office, list multiple real estate holdings in Riverside County. The ECU complaint identifies the East 6th Street properties as undisclosed but does not exhaustively catalog Calvert's full property portfolio. A complete inventory would require a Riverside County Assessor records pull cross-referenced against his House financial-disclosure filings, an exercise this beat draft does not undertake but recommends.

The general pattern documented in a 2024 LA Times report is that Calvert's earmarks systematically cluster around real estate he owns, real estate his business partners own, or real estate near transit corridors he votes federal funds for. The pattern is not coincidental and not isolated.

The Office of Congressional Ethics complaint

In August 2024, End Citizens United filed a formal complaint with the Office of Congressional Ethics alleging that Calvert violated House ethics rules by securing earmarks while owning real estate that benefited from those earmarks, and by failing to disclose the relevant property purchases. Will Rollins, then Calvert's 2024 Democratic challenger, separately asked the House Ethics Committee to investigate.

The 2007 precedent is instructive. Per ECU's filing, Calvert that year submitted an earmark for a transportation hub in Corona that would raise the value of the surrounding area; he had seven properties in the vicinity. Calvert wrote to the House Ethics Committee in advance asking whether he could submit it. The Committee told him it was fine, as long as other local businesses also benefited. That is the template the modern pattern fits.

The 2007 ethics-clearance letter is the playbook. Ask permission first. Make sure other people benefit too. Then take the earmark.

Defense contractor money + the Iran war

Calvert chairs the House Defense Appropriations Subcommittee. Per Times of San Diego reporting in May 2026, Calvert's campaign committee received more than $200,000 during the first three months of 2026 from defense contractor political action committees and direct contributions from defense executives.

The reporting documents specific contributions in the days immediately following the start of the Iran war: on March 2 and March 3, 2026, Calvert received two $1,000 donations from leaders at military vehicle manufacturer AM General. The political action committees of Lockheed Martin and RTX (Raytheon's parent) donated $5,000 each on March 4 and March 9, respectively. In the 2022 election cycle, Calvert received at least $70,000 from the top five U.S. defense contractors: Lockheed Martin, Raytheon Technologies, Boeing, Northrop Grumman, and General Dynamics.

Cross-link: The Bailey family

The Bailey family, owners of Stronghold Engineering Inc. (the Riverside firm that paid a $2.5 million Department of Justice Non-Prosecution Agreement in 2021 for SDVOSB program fraud), have a documented history of federal political giving to Calvert. Beverly Bailey contributed multiple times to Calvert in 2009 and 2015. Charles Gossage, an Executive Vice President at Stronghold Engineering, gave Calvert $2,400 in June 2009. Scott Bailey is a member of the Lincoln Club of Riverside County.

The Bailey family's other business, Stronghold Power Systems, is a major contributor to Moving California Forward, the Inland Empire developer PAC at the center of The Second Floor. The cross-fund pattern documents a single donor family operating at both federal (Calvert) and state/local (Moving California Forward) levels.

Open questions

Ken Calvert has not publicly addressed the specific ECU complaint allegations regarding the 1210 and 1212 East 6th Street properties, the disclosure status of those properties, or whether the systematic clustering of his earmarks around properties he owns reflects a pattern beyond the 2007 ethics-clearance precedent.

The Office of Congressional Ethics has not publicly disclosed the status of its review of the ECU complaint, though OCE matters typically take eighteen to twenty-four months from filing to public outcome.

The House Ethics Committee has not publicly addressed whether the 2007 ethics-clearance letter that authorized Calvert's transportation-hub earmark continues to govern the modern pattern, or whether Community Project Funding rules adopted in 2021 alter the analysis.
