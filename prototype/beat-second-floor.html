The Second Floor.

One office above the lobby of a 124-year-old Riverside hotel. Three political action committees. Three candidate campaigns. A developer consulting firm. The Riverside Sheriffs' Association's lobbying shop. The hotel is being sold to a casino tribe whose donations also flow through this floor.

3649 MISSION INN AVE, FLOOR 2 · RIVERSIDE, CA 92501

Inland Empire developer money flows in. Two treasurers on one floor route it back out to a gubernatorial candidate, two sitting county supervisors, two assembly candidates, and a slate of local races. The building itself was sold to a tribal gaming investment authority on May 4, 2026.

If you want to know who's building an Inland Empire, look up. The second floor of the Mission Inn Hotel & Spa in downtown Riverside is the address on file for one developer-funded political action committee, one ballot measure committee, three candidate campaigns, a political consulting firm, and the Riverside Sheriffs' Association's lobbying operation. Two treasurers do all the paperwork. The same Inland Empire developers send in the money. The same Inland Empire politicians take it out.

The Mission Inn is a 124-year-old hotel that anchors downtown Riverside. Presidents have stayed there. The Festival of Lights brings millions of visitors every winter. Duane Roberts, the man who invented the mass-market frozen burrito and then bought the shuttered hotel out of bankruptcy in 1992, restored it for $55 million and ran it with his wife Kelly for thirty-three years. He died on November 1, 2025. Six months later, on May 4, 2026, his widow announced a deal to sell the hotel to the San Manuel Investment Authority, the investment arm of the Yuhaaviatam of San Manuel Nation, the federally recognized tribe that operates the largest casino in California.

The hotel's lobby and dining rooms are not the story. The second floor is.

What's on the second floor

The mailing address 3649 Mission Inn Ave, Floor 2, Riverside, CA 92501 appears on the cover page of at least five California Fair Political Practices Commission filings.

Two of those filings are political action committees that share a treasurer of record at this addressverify Form 410 treasurer name: Moving California Forward (FPPC #1455936), a general-purpose recipient committee, and Pacheco Moving California Forward A Ballot Measure Committee (FPPC #1461025). One PAC sends money to candidates. The other sends money to ballot measures. The same person is the principal officer and treasurer of both.

Three more filings at the same address are candidate-controlled campaign committees, each listing Jennifer Mitchell of Campaign Finance Services LLC as their treasurer of recordverify Form 460:

  • V. Manuel Perez for Supervisor 2026 (FPPC #1396909). Riverside County Board of Supervisors, District 4 (Coachella Valley).
  • Yxstian Gutierrez for Supervisor 2026 (FPPC #1439760). Riverside County Board of Supervisors, District 5 (Moreno Valley).
  • Alvin DeJohnette for City Council 2024 (FPPC #1473823). City of Riverside, Ward 6.

Mitchell's firm is itself a paid Moving California Forward vendor. Campaign Finance Services LLC received $5,000 from MCF in the 2026 cycle (see payee table below). The same firm that handles the candidate committees' paperwork at this address is also collecting a check from the PAC funding those candidates.

Two more tenants on the floor, both Rod Pacheco's people

The same suite, listed in California Secretary of State records as the "2nd Floor Rotunda" of the Mission Inn, is also the address of two professional firms staffed by Republican Party veterans of Riverside County politics.

Pacheco & Neach pc is a litigation law firm with offices in Irvine and at 3649 Mission Inn Avenue, Second Floor Rotunda, Riverside, CA 92501verify suite directory. The firm's named partner is Rod Pacheco, the former Riverside County District Attorney (2007-2011), former California State Assembly Republican Leader, and former California Assemblyman who represented western Riverside County from 1996 to 2002. Pacheco was the first Latino Republican elected to the California State Assembly. He is a graduate of UC Riverside.

Troast & Associates LLC is a Republican-side political fundraising and consulting firm founded in December 2017 by Marc Troast. Its address on file with the California Secretary of State (LLC filing #201736210359) is the same 3649 Mission Inn Ave, 2nd Floor Rotunda. Marc Troast's career includes ten years as District Director and Political Director for Republican Congresswoman Mary Bono, service as Southern California Director of the California Republican Party, Executive Director of the Riverside County Republican Party, and staff for Rod Pacheco, Russ Bogh, and Brian Nestande when each served in the State Assembly. He was Inland Empire Chair for Meg Whitman's 2010 gubernatorial campaign and a 2012 Romney delegate. Troast & Associates is the single largest vendor on Moving California Forward's payee schedule, receiving $60,620 in the 2026 cycle.

The Riverside Sheriffs' Association also runs its state lobbying operation out of this buildingverify exact suite. RSA's registered legislative advocate is Ryan Sherman of Sherman Consulting, who also represents the California Narcotic Officers' Association and multiple other law enforcement unions in Sacramento.

One suite. A former Riverside County DA's law firm. A Republican Party operative's consulting shop. Two treasurers. Five campaign and PAC committees. A sheriffs' union lobbying operation. Every dollar in this story moves through this one room.

Two names on this list change the meaning of every other name.

Jeffrey Burum is the San Bernardino County developer at the center of the Colonies corruption case, the longest-running political bribery scandal in San Bernardino County history. The single biggest line item on the payee list is not a candidate. It is Troast & Associates LLC, the political consulting firm that operates from the same floor as the PAC paying it. $60,620 of $182,329 in total payouts went to that one firm. The PAC's money is moving thirty feet from one office to another.

The candidate payouts add up to a Republican Inland Empire political bloc. Two sitting Riverside County supervisors. A gubernatorial candidate who is the sitting Riverside County Sheriff. Two state Assembly candidates, both endorsed by the California Republican Assembly. A city council candidate in San Bernardino. A school board candidate in Riverside. A Riverside County Superior Court judge candidate.

The Assembly candidates are the tightest part of this network. Natasha Johnson, the AD63 incumbent who received $4,000 from MCF, was sworn in to the State Assembly in September 2025 to fill the seat vacated by Bill Essayli, who left to become interim U.S. Attorney for Los Angeles. Johnson's endorsement slate for that special election included Riverside County Sheriff and gubernatorial candidate Chad Bianco, Congressman Ken Calvert, Riverside County District Attorney Mike Hestrin, and Essayli himself. The same Inland Empire Republican machine that endorsed her also funded her through Moving California Forward. Bianco's gubernatorial campaign, which the PAC supports directly, sits at the center of that endorsement web. Johnson also received $17,700 from the Pechanga Band of Indians at the state level, putting her on both the developer-PAC payee list and the tribal-gaming priority list.

Ed Delgado, the AD60 candidate who received $5,900 from MCF, is a 25-year veteran of the Riverside County Sheriff's Department who rose to Assistant Sheriff. He was elected to the Moreno Valley City Council in 2021. He is the California Republican Assembly's endorsed AD60 candidate and a target seat on Reform California's "26 for 2026" pickup list.

Two of the payees are not in Riverside County at all. Theodore Sanchez for City Council 2026 is a San Bernardino City Council Ward 1 race. The PAC's reach extends across the Riverside-San Bernardino county line. The Pechanga Band's $11,800 maximum-out donations to assembly candidates similarly cross county lines, but the Moving California Forward developer pool is the unusual case: it brings the Burum + Devereaux San Bernardino political alumni back together with active Riverside County races.

A convicted developer's circle is in the same pool

Jeffrey Burum's $5,000 contribution to Moving California Forward is small as a dollar figure. As a signal, it is not.

The Colonies case is the central political corruption story of San Bernardino County in the 2010s. Burum was indicted on charges of bribing three county supervisors with a total of $400,000 in exchange for their votes on a $102 million settlement to his development company. The case ran from 2011 through 2017. Burum was acquitted; one supervisor was acquitted; two pleaded no contest to lesser charges. The case did not end the careers of the people involved. It restructured the donor map of the Inland Empire.

Burum publicly bankrolled the 2022 San Bernardino County ballot measure (Measure EE) that asked county voters whether they wanted to study seceding from California and form an "Empire State." The measure passed 51.3% to 48.7%. It is a non-binding statement of grievance against Sacramento. The Moving California Forward brand maps directly to that grievance: pull California's attention back to the Inland Empire.

Gregory Devereaux ran the San Bernardino County administration during the Colonies era. He is now donating, alongside Burum, to a PAC funding Riverside County supervisors.

Athens Services brings its 21-city playbook to the floor

Athens Services and its affiliated entities contributed $25,000 to Moving California Forward, tied with Stronghold Power Systems for the second-largest entity contribution. Athens is a fourth-generation waste hauler founded in 1957 by Jack Arakelian. Michael Arakelian holds the controlling fifty percent of shares. The company collects trash and recycling in roughly twenty-one Southern California municipalities.

In multiple jurisdictions where Athens holds franchise agreements, the company and individual members of the Arakelian family have used a documented pay-to-play pattern. In West Hollywood, then-councilmember John Duran received more than $26,000 from the company, its officers, and the Arakelians in the two years preceding a long contract extension vote. In West Covina, Athens made a $1,000 direct corporate contribution to three councilmembers, supplemented by $999 donations from each of three different Arakelian-family LLCs, a pattern that splits a single source's giving across multiple entities to remain under any individual aggregation flag.

The Athens $25,000 to Moving California Forward in 2026 fits the established playbook: support the political action committee that supports the local elected officials who eventually vote on franchise renewals, exclusive contracts, and rate adjustments. The PAC is the wrapper. The franchise vote is the ask.

Bianco for Governor: a $5.26 million orbit

Bianco for Governor 2026 is Riverside County Sheriff Chad Bianco's gubernatorial campaign. By April 2026, it had raised $5.26 million from over 7,000 donors, spent $4.16 million, and held $1.4 million in cash on hand.

Moving California Forward's $10,000 to Bianco is small in context of his total. But the donor pool of Moving California Forward overlaps the donor pool of Bianco's own committee. Two specific overlaps:

Charles B Wood, the top contributor to Moving California Forward at $50,800, also gave $39,382 directly to Bianco for Governor. His combined support, across the two committees, is approximately $90,000.

Highland Fairview Operating Company, Iddo Benzeevi's company, gave $20,000 to Moving California Forward and an additional $39,200 directly to Bianco for Governor. That second number is the legal maximum a single corporate entity can give a state candidate per cycle. Combined: $59,200.

The shared donor pattern is the integration. Money flows two ways to the same candidate: through the developer PAC on the second floor, and directly from the same names. The candidate's committee shows the second piece. The PAC's payees show the first piece. Neither shows the full picture on its own.

Highland Fairview and Yxstian Gutierrez go back ten years

Iddo Benzeevi's Highland Fairview is one of Moving California Forward's largest contributors at $20,000. Benzeevi's combined direct giving to Bianco for Governor adds another $39,200. But his political relationship with one specific supervisor on this beat goes back a decade.

In August 2015, Yxstian Gutierrez was a Moreno Valley councilmember. The city council voted 3-2 to approve Highland Fairview's World Logistics Center, a 2,610-acre warehouse park covering roughly ten percent of Moreno Valley. Gutierrez voted yes. After the vote, project supporters chanted Benzeevi's first name in the council chambers: "Iddo, Iddo, Iddo."

Subsequent reporting documented the financial relationship that followed the vote. According to BuzzFeed News reporting and contemporaneous coverage, Benzeevi gave more than $400,000 to independent expenditure committees that helped Gutierrez and another councilmember win their elections. Benzeevi or his linked committees gave Gutierrez approximately $47,000 in 2016, accounting for about a third of Gutierrez's campaign funds that year. Highland Fairview was under FBI criminal investigation in 2013, a probe that went dormant by 2015 without charges.

Gutierrez went on to become Moreno Valley mayor, then Riverside County Supervisor in District 5, taking office in December 2022. The Inland Empire Health Plan appointed him to its Governing Board in January 2023.

The largest developer in his former city is among the largest contributors to the political action committee that now funds his county supervisor campaign, run from an office shared with the law firm of the former county DA.

Two of these supervisors sit on a $320 million fraud board

Karen Spiegel (Riverside County Supervisor, District 2) and Yxstian Gutierrez (Riverside County Supervisor, District 5) both received money from Moving California Forward in the 2026 cycle. Both supervisors also sit on the Governing Board of the Inland Empire Health Plan, a Joint Powers Agency that provides Medi-Cal coverage to about 1.6 million residents of Riverside and San Bernardino Counties.

In September 2025, the United States Department of Justice filed a False Claims Act lawsuit against the Inland Empire Health Plan, alleging the plan kept approximately $320 million in federal Medi-Cal expansion funds that it should have returned. The complaint quoted an internal email from then-CEO Bradley Gilbert:

"The funds have to flow through the two county hospitals from IEHP so I can get credit for them as medical costs or the dollars go back to the state."

Spiegel chaired the IEHP Governing Board from 2021 through early 2025, covering the alleged-fraud window. Gutierrez joined the board in January 2023. After leaving IEHP in 2019, Bradley Gilbert was appointed by Governor Newsom as Director of the California Department of Health Care Services, the very state agency that oversees Medi-Cal. He oversaw, from the state's regulatory chair, the program he is alleged to have defrauded from the agency's chair.

The data center, the felony mayor, and the federal fraud settlement

Stronghold Power Systems Inc, listed on Moving California Forward's donor schedule for $25,000, is one of two private companies behind a proposed 270-megawatt data center campus on 240 acres of agricultural land in the city of Coachella. The deal is structured as a public-private municipal utility partnership. The Coachella City Council approved the Municipal Utility Development agreement on February 11, 2026. Residents began organizing for a moratorium in April.

Two facts about Stronghold and its sister company change the picture.

First: the parent firm, Stronghold Engineering Inc, founded by Scott and Beverly Bailey, paid $2.5 million to the United States Department of Justice in April 2021 to settle a federal False Claims Act case. The DOJ alleged that, from August 2007 to October 2013, the company exploited the Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business program by using a veteran-owned business as a false front to qualify for federal contracts the company was not eligible for.

Second: the Coachella mayor who took a $4,900 campaign contribution from Stronghold Power Systems and presided over the February 11 vote, Steven Hernandez, was indicted on October 30, 2025 on a nine-count grand jury indictment. The charges: one felony count of conflict of interest, four felony counts of perjury on his Form 700 economic-interest disclosures, and four misdemeanor counts of conflict of interest. He pleaded guilty in March 2026 to one felony conflict-of-interest charge under Government Code §1090. The District Attorney dropped the other eight charges. Hernandez was sentenced to two years probation and 200 hours of community service. He resigned after twelve years as mayor.

The indictment scope, per the Riverside County District Attorney, explicitly covered Hernandez's "votes and advocacy related to downtown Coachella development projects." It was not limited to the contract that triggered the original investigation.

The hotel sells to a tribe that also funds the floor

On May 4, 2026, the San Manuel Investment Authority, the investment arm of the Yuhaaviatam of San Manuel Nation, announced an agreement to purchase the Mission Inn Hotel & Spa. The Roberts family had owned the hotel for thirty-three years. Duane Roberts restored it after Chemical Bank's foreclosure; his widow Kelly Roberts is selling. Financial terms were not disclosed. The deal is expected to close by the end of May.

The San Manuel Band of Mission Indians operates the largest tribal casino in California. They are an active political donor at the federal and state levels. The closing of this hotel sale will install a tribal gaming investment authority as the landlord of an office building that, on its second floor, runs a developer-funded political action committee whose money flows to a sitting Riverside County supervisor, a sitting Riverside County supervisor's IEHP-board colleague, and a Republican gubernatorial candidate who is the sitting Riverside County Sheriff.

The new owners of the hotel will be the landlords of the floor where the Inland Empire's developer money is sorted.

Open questions

V. Manuel Perez has not publicly addressed why his campaign committee's mailing address is the same as the developer-funded political action committee at the center of his district's most controversial projects, or whether his vote on Riverside County matters involving Stronghold Power Systems, Highland Fairview, or other Moving California Forward donors would constitute a conflict of interest under Government Code §1090.

Karen Spiegel has not publicly addressed how the Inland Empire Health Plan Governing Board, which she chaired from 2021 through early 2025, failed to flag the federal Medi-Cal expansion fund routing pattern that the United States Department of Justice now alleges constituted $320 million in unreturned federal money.

Yxstian Gutierrez has not publicly addressed his position on the IEHP federal lawsuit, his role on the IEHP board during the period the DOJ alleges fraud was occurring, or whether his $9,500 in support from Moving California Forward creates any conflict with his fiduciary duty to the public health plan.

Chad Bianco has not publicly addressed whether the donor concentration around Moving California Forward, his direct contributions from the same names, and his candidacy for governor reflect a structural pattern of Inland Empire developer money supporting his political career, or whether he will recuse from any matter involving these donors should he be elected governor.

The Pacheco who serves as treasurer of Moving California Forward and its sister ballot measure committee has not publicly identified any relationship to former Riverside County District Attorney Rod Pacheco, whose law firm Pacheco & Neach pc shares the same Mission Inn 2nd Floor Rotunda address.

Marc Troast has not publicly addressed the operating relationship between Troast & Associates LLC, the candidate committees that file from his suite, and the Moving California Forward committee whose largest vendor payments flow to his firm.

Iddo Benzeevi has not publicly addressed the structural pattern documented across the past decade in which his political contributions to Yxstian Gutierrez preceded city council and county supervisor votes on his Moreno Valley development projects.

Stronghold Power Systems has not publicly addressed how its parent company's $2.5 million Department of Justice False Claims Act settlement for exploiting the Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business program affects its current public-private partnership agreements with California municipalities.

Athens Services has not publicly addressed whether its $25,000 contribution to Moving California Forward, made while the company holds active franchise agreements with municipalities whose elected officials Moving California Forward supports, raises any of the same pay-to-play questions raised by reporting in West Hollywood and West Covina.
