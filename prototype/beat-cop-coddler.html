SB 1421 and the Department of Justice exception

SB 1421, the Right to Know Act, was signed into law in 2018 and took effect January 1, 2019. The statute amended the California Public Records Act to require disclosure of records concerning serious officer misconduct, including incidents of dishonesty, sexual assault by an officer, and officer-involved use of deadly force. The Legislature's intent was unambiguous: SB 1421 applied to all California law enforcement agencies, including the California Department of Justice.

KQED reported on February 5, 2019 that Becerra's office had refused to release SB 1421 records on its own DOJ agents in response to public records requests. The stated rationale was a retroactivity argument: that records of misconduct incidents predating the statute's effective date should not be subject to release. KQED's reporting documented the position as putting Becerra's office at odds with the Legislature's stated intent and out of step with the local agencies that had begun complying.

The legal posture mattered more than the technical retroactivity question. Becerra was the state's chief law enforcement officer. His office's public refusal to apply SB 1421 to its own DOJ officers signaled to local agencies that resistance was the official position of the Department of Justice itself.

The First Amendment Coalition lawsuit

The First Amendment Coalition (FAC) is a Northern-California-based nonprofit that advocates for press and public access to government records. On February 14, 2019, the FAC filed suit against Becerra to force compliance with SB 1421's disclosure requirements for DOJ misconduct files. KQED joined the suit on March 5, 2019.

The First Amendment Coalition's public characterization of the Becerra office was, in plain language, that the Attorney General had set "the worst example of any law enforcement agency in the state" on SB 1421 compliance. That assessment came from the press-rights organization most directly engaged with California public-records litigation. It carried weight because the FAC's docket regularly includes municipal police departments, sheriffs' offices, and county DA offices that are individually resisting disclosure. The FAC's verdict was that the chief state-level law enforcement office was worse than any of them.

The criminal-prosecution threat to journalists

In January and February 2019, two Berkeley-based investigative reporters obtained a list of nearly 12,000 California law enforcement officers convicted of crimes through a lawful public records request. The list had been compiled internally by California state agencies and was responsive to a Public Records Act request the reporters had submitted.

KQED reported on February 26, 2019 that Becerra's office threatened the two journalists with criminal prosecution and demanded they destroy the document. The Freedom of the Press Foundation reported on March 5, 2019 that Becerra's office had refused to rule out legal action against the reporters in response to direct questions. The criminal-threat angle was unprecedented in modern California: a state attorney general's office threatening journalists with criminal prosecution for material the journalists had obtained through a lawful records request.

The state's chief law enforcement officer threatened criminal prosecution against journalists who lawfully obtained a list of convicted California cops. The list was not classified. The list was not stolen. The list documented officers a court had already found guilty of crimes.

The First Amendment implications were straightforward: even if the underlying records had been improperly released to the journalists by a state agency clerical error (a contested question), the journalists had no legal exposure for receiving the document, and the AG's prosecution threat constituted a direct attempt to suppress lawfully-obtained newsworthy material. Press-rights organizations immediately characterized the threat as constitutionally unsupportable.

"The state's top coddler of bad cops"

The phrase comes from a San Jose Mercury News editorial board assessment of Becerra's tenure, summarized in CalMatters' February 22, 2021 retrospective on his California attorney general record as he was being confirmed to head the federal Department of Health and Human Services in the Biden administration. The editorial language was notable because the Mercury News editorial board is not a left-of-center police-abolition outlet. It is a mainstream Bay Area institutional editorial voice. Its judgment that Becerra had been the state's top coddler of bad cops is the institutional press's summary verdict on the four-year arc.

CalMatters' February 22, 2021 retrospective placed the SB 1421 refusal and the journalist-prosecution threat in the broader context of Becerra's tenure: he had filed approximately 110 lawsuits against the Trump administration over four years (more than twice the number of suits Texas filed against Obama in eight years), with over half targeting environmental policy and an additional half-dozen defending the Affordable Care Act. The federal litigation posture won him national Democratic acclaim. The state-level enforcement record on California businesses, law enforcement agencies, and institutions where California had legal exposure drew civil-liberties criticism.

Police union money: 2018 and 2026

Per CalMatters' February 22, 2021 reporting, law enforcement unions had spent approximately $300,000 in independent expenditures and contributions supporting Becerra's 2018 California Attorney General campaign. The Mercury News editorial language about him being the state's top coddler of bad cops sat directly alongside the financial relationship: the candidate the police unions had backed had become the state's chief law enforcement officer, and his office had set the worst example of any California law enforcement agency on misconduct-records disclosure.

The 2026 governor campaign continues the pattern. Becerra's 2026 committee accepts contributions from law enforcement unions and from individual law enforcement officers. The full categorical totals are pending complete Cal-Access aggregation by union-PAC category; the structural fact is that the campaign has not refused this funding pool, and the candidate has not publicly addressed the SB 1421 / journalist-threat record from his AG tenure as part of his 2026 platform.

The asymmetry: federal aggression, state restraint

Becerra's institutional brand from his California attorney general tenure was federal litigation aggression: 110 lawsuits against the Trump administration, the most aggressive state-AG resistance to a federal administration in modern California history, with a litigation cost CalMatters estimated at $41 million in state resources.

The asymmetry is the subject of this beat. The same office that filed 110 federal lawsuits in four years against the Trump administration was the office that refused to comply with the Legislature's police-misconduct-records statute, sued by First Amendment Coalition and KQED, threatened journalists with criminal prosecution for receiving a lawfully-obtained convicted-officer list, and (per CalMatters citing the San Jose Mercury News editorial board) became "the state's top coddler of bad cops."

The asymmetry is not random. Federal litigation against a Republican administration produced national Democratic acclaim, Cabinet recruitment by the Biden administration, and a clear path to higher political office. State-level enforcement against California law enforcement agencies, by contrast, would have alienated the police-union donor base that had funded the 2018 AG run. The same calculus, applied consistently, produces both halves of the record. That is the structural reading.

KQED's February 26, 2019 reporting referenced two Berkeley investigative reporters but did not name them in the published account. Their names, current employment, and whether they retained the 12,000-name list (or destroyed it under the AG's threat) are open questions worth following up.

His campaign website and the public-record positions he has taken in 2026 forums on police accountability have not, in the named-publication record reviewed, included a retrospective on the SB 1421 enforcement decision he made as AG. Whether he stands by the retroactivity argument or has revised his view is open.
