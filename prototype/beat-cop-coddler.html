The state's top coddler of bad cops.

The San Jose Mercury News editorial board's verdict on Xavier Becerra's tenure as California Attorney General. Police unions paid for that career. Then he refused them records, threatened reporters, and lost in court twice. Now they've endorsed his rivals.

SB 1421 enforcement timeline · January 2019 to February 2019 · attorney general office of Xavier Becerra

The law took effect January 1. The state's chief law enforcement officer refused to comply with it for his own agents 35 days later. Press-rights groups sued. He threatened the journalists exposing the scope. The San Jose Mercury News editorial board called him the state's "top coddler of bad cops."

In 2018, police unions spent nearly $300,000 backing Xavier Becerra's run for California Attorney General. Then in his first weeks as the state's chief law enforcement officer, the legislature passed a law requiring his agency to release records of serious officer misconduct. Becerra refused to release the records on his own Department of Justice agents. The First Amendment Coalition and KQED sued him. He threatened two Berkeley journalists with criminal prosecution for obtaining a list of 12,000 California cops convicted of crimes. The San Jose Mercury News editorial board called him "the state's top coddler of bad cops."

He lost in court twice. Once on whether the law applied to records from before 2019. A second time on whether his department had to release records it held. His successor as Attorney General settled the lawsuits within weeks of taking office.

In August 2025, the Peace Officers Research Association of California, the state's largest law enforcement organization and the same union that bankrolled Becerra's 2018 run, endorsed two of his rivals for governor. They endorsed Antonio Villaraigosa, a Democrat. They endorsed Chad Bianco, a Republican. They did not endorse Xavier Becerra. Across the entire 2026 cycle, Becerra has not received a single dollar from any police union, sheriff association, or law-enforcement PAC. His campaign platform does not address police accountability, public records, or law enforcement misconduct. He is running for governor on healthcare, housing, and Donald Trump.

The Mercury News verdict from 2021 stands. The donor base that financed it has moved on. The candidate has not addressed it.

Chevron documents his $39,200 from Chevron USA Inc. and his "they're not the bad guy" defense quote. This page documents his record as the state's chief law enforcement officer.

Act I: They funded him

Xavier Becerra was appointed California Attorney General in January 2017 to fill the seat Kamala Harris vacated for the U.S. Senate. He had to win it outright in November 2018 to keep it.

Police unions backed him. By CalMatters' count in 2021, they spent about $300,000 backing his 2018 run. A mix of direct campaign checks and outside ad spending. The biggest direct check came from the California Correctional Peace Officers Association: $52,600 to his AG committee. The other roughly $247,000 was outside ads run by police-aligned groups on his behalf.

He won the November 2018 election. Six weeks later, the law that would blow up that relationship took effect.

Act II: He refused them records

SB 1421, known as the Right to Know Act, was signed into law in 2018 and took effect January 1, 2019. It changed the California Public Records Act to require police departments to release records of serious officer misconduct: dishonesty findings, sexual assault by an officer, and officer-involved use of deadly force. The legislature's intent was clear. The law applied to every California law enforcement agency, including the Department of Justice that Becerra ran.

Five weeks later, on February 5, 2019, KQED reported that Becerra's office had refused to release SB 1421 records on its own DOJ agents. His office said the law did not apply to records of misconduct that occurred before January 1, 2019. Lawyers call that a "retroactivity" argument. Local agencies were already complying. The state's own chief law enforcement office was the holdout.

The legal argument was technical. The signal was political. Becerra was the state's top cop. When his office publicly refused to apply the new misconduct-records law to its own agents, that became the official position of the Department of Justice, and a permission slip for local agencies to resist.

Local agencies that did comply were already turning up serious findings under the new law. KQED's reporting laid out what those early disclosures had exposed.

An officer who had tried to sexually exploit a woman he arrested. Prosecutors reopened the case to look at filing criminal charges.

A former Southern California sheriff's deputy, who also worked as an investigator for the state Department of Consumer Affairs, admitted to stealing thousands of rounds of ammunition over decades. He was never charged with theft.

A Rio Vista woman whose criminal charges were dismissed after reporting on the records exposed the conduct of the police K-9 unit that had mauled her.

These were the kinds of records Becerra's office was refusing to release on its own DOJ agents.

The First Amendment Coalition lawsuit

The First Amendment Coalition (FAC) is a Northern-California-based nonprofit that advocates for press and public access to government records. On February 14, 2019, the FAC filed suit against Becerra to force compliance with SB 1421's disclosure requirements for DOJ misconduct files. KQED joined the suit on March 5, 2019.

The First Amendment Coalition's public characterization of the Becerra office was, in plain language, that the Attorney General had set "the worst example of any law enforcement agency in the state" on SB 1421 compliance. That assessment came from the press-rights organization most directly engaged with California public-records litigation. It carried weight because the FAC's docket regularly includes municipal police departments, sheriffs' offices, and county DA offices that are individually resisting disclosure. The FAC's verdict was that the chief state-level law enforcement office was worse than any of them.

The criminal-prosecution threat to journalists

In January 2019, two reporters at UC Berkeley's Investigative Reporting Program, Robert Lewis and Jason Paladino, obtained a list of nearly 12,000 California law enforcement officers convicted of crimes through a lawful public records request. The list had been compiled internally by California state agencies and was responsive to a request the reporters had filed under the Public Records Act.

The list was not released under SB 1421. It came out under a separate California statute covering convicted peace officers, and Becerra's office argued the records had been "inadvertently provided" by the state agency that disclosed them. On January 29, 2019, his office sent the journalists a demand: destroy the records or face legal action.

Lewis and Paladino refused. They began publishing preliminary findings in partnership with KQED and the Bay Area News Group while the threat hung over them. KQED reported on February 26, 2019 that Becerra's office had threatened the reporters with criminal prosecution. The Freedom of the Press Foundation reported on March 5, 2019 that the office had refused to rule out legal action when asked directly. A state attorney general's office threatening journalists with criminal prosecution over material they had obtained through a lawful records request was without modern California precedent.

The state's chief law enforcement officer threatened criminal prosecution against journalists who lawfully obtained a list of convicted California cops. The list was not classified. The list was not stolen. The list documented officers a court had already found guilty of crimes.

Lewis and Paladino did not destroy the list. They continued publishing as the legal threat persisted. Robert Lewis is now an investigative reporter at CalMatters; Jason Paladino remains affiliated with UC Berkeley's Investigative Reporting Program. Press-rights groups said at the time that the threat was illegal on its face: receiving lawfully-obtained newsworthy material is not a crime in the United States, and the prosecution threat was a direct attempt to suppress reporting about California cops the courts had already convicted.

Two arguments. Two laws. Same direction. On the SB 1421 misconduct records, Becerra's office argued the law did not apply to old records. On the 12,000-name conviction list, his office argued the records had been released by mistake and demanded their destruction. Both positions came out of the same office in the same six-week window. Both pointed at keeping the same kind of information about California cops out of the public record.

"The state's top coddler of bad cops"

The phrase comes from the San Jose Mercury News editorial board. CalMatters quoted it in a February 22, 2021 piece looking back at Becerra's California AG record. The piece ran as the U.S. Senate was preparing to confirm him as Health and Human Services Secretary under President Biden.

The Mercury News is not a left-wing outlet. It is a mainstream Bay Area editorial voice. Its summary of Becerra's four years was that he had been the state's top coddler of bad cops.

The same CalMatters piece compared his SB 1421 record to his federal lawsuit record. By his own count on his 2026 campaign website, Becerra sued the Trump administration 122 times as California Attorney General. That's more than twice the number of times Texas had sued the Obama administration in eight years. Those lawsuits made him a national Democratic star. They got him into the Cabinet. The state-level enforcement record went the other way.

↑ Top

He lost in court, twice

The retroactivity argument failed in court. On March 12, 2019, a California appeals court ruled in Walnut Creek Police Officers' Association v. City of Walnut Creek that SB 1421 applied even to misconduct that happened before the law took effect. The reasoning: what triggered disclosure was the records request, not the misconduct itself. Becerra's "old records don't count" argument lost.

A second ruling closed the door for the DOJ specifically. In Becerra v. Superior Court (2020), a California appeals court held that Becerra's department had to release every SB 1421 record it had on file, even when the records came from other agencies and the officers worked elsewhere. His office's argument for keeping the records private got tossed.

Becerra was confirmed as U.S. Secretary of Health and Human Services in March 2021. Rob Bonta took over as California Attorney General. On May 7, 2021, within weeks of taking office, Bonta settled the lawsuits with the First Amendment Coalition and KQED. His office would review, redact, and release the records the lawsuit had been demanding by September 26, 2021. The fight Becerra's office had picked, his successor settled.

Act III: They moved on

Becerra is running for governor in 2026. His campaign committee, Becerra for Governor 2026 (FPPC ID 1480025), has raised $5.7 million from 5,061 donors as of the latest filings. Zero of those dollars came from a police union, a sheriff's association, or any law-enforcement PAC.

Not one police-affiliated donor shows up in his 2026 top 50. Going back through every campaign Becerra has ever run (four committees, four cycles, since 2018), only one police union appears in his career top 50: the California Correctional Peace Officers Association, with the same $52,600 from his 2018 AG run. The other roughly $247,000 in CalMatters' $300,000 total came from outside ad spending by police-aligned groups. None of that has come back in 2026.

On August 21, 2025, the state's biggest police union, the Peace Officers Research Association of California (PORAC), endorsed two of Becerra's rivals. PORAC President Brian R. Marvel signed endorsement statements for Antonio Villaraigosa, a Democrat, and Chad Bianco, a Republican. The release explained the dual endorsement: the union backs "proven public safety champions regardless of where they stand on the political spectrum."

And the money matches. Villaraigosa has received $78,400 from PORAC PAC. That's the legal maximum. Bianco has received $39,200 from PORAC plus $93,122 from three California county deputy-sheriff associations (Riverside, San Diego, Santa Barbara). Combined, that's $132,322 in police-union money. The other 2026 candidates (Mahan, Hilton, Porter, Steyer) show zero in their top 50 donor lists, the same as Becerra. The police-union lane in this race belongs to Villaraigosa and Bianco. Becerra was not invited.

And the candidate himself has not addressed his AG record on policing in the 2026 campaign. His official campaign website at xavierbecerra2026.com lists nine priority topics: healthcare, fighting Donald Trump, defending rights, housing, economy and affordability, energy and utilities, disaster preparedness, AI, and homelessness. Police accountability does not appear. SB 1421 does not appear. The criminal-prosecution threat to Lewis and Paladino does not appear. The Mercury News verdict does not appear. As of May 9, 2026, no named publication has documented Becerra publicly addressing his attorney-general police-accountability record in the 2026 cycle.

The asymmetry: federal aggression, state silence

What Becerra was known for as Attorney General was suing the Trump administration. By his own count, 122 times. That's more than twice as many lawsuits as Texas filed against the Obama administration in eight years. CalMatters put the cost to California taxpayers at $41 million in state legal resources. Those lawsuits built his national Democratic profile and led directly to his Cabinet job.

The same office spent the same four years refusing to release police misconduct records on its own DOJ agents, getting sued by the press, threatening reporters with criminal prosecution, and losing in court twice. When the Mercury News editorial board summed up Becerra's four years, the line they used was "top coddler of bad cops." The line they did not use was "champion of police accountability."

The split is not random. Suing a Republican president made him a national Democratic star and got him into the Cabinet. Going hard on California police would have cost him the police-union donor base that paid for the 2018 run. Same political calculus, both halves of the record. The donor base he protected then has now moved on to other candidates. The record stays on his ledger.

Open questions

Whether any DOJ misconduct records are still being held back. Bonta's May 7, 2021 settlement with the First Amendment Coalition and KQED set a September 26, 2021 deadline for the DOJ to release the records the lawsuit had been demanding. Whether that release was complete, partial, or whether more records still exist that Becerra's office had argued were off-limits, and whether any of those are still sitting in the DOJ's files in 2026, is open.

Whether Becerra still stands by the retroactivity argument his office lost in court. Two California appellate courts rejected the legal theory his AG office used to refuse SB 1421 records on his own DOJ agents. His successor settled the lawsuits. Becerra has not publicly stated whether he believes the courts decided correctly, whether he would make the same call again, or whether his approach to police-misconduct disclosure as governor would differ from the approach he took as Attorney General.

Whether Becerra will address the AG record before the June 2 primary. As of May 9, 2026, no named publication has documented Becerra publicly addressing his SB 1421 enforcement decisions, the criminal-prosecution threat to Lewis and Paladino, or the Mercury News editorial language. His campaign platform does not mention police accountability. Whether a debate question, an interview, or his own campaign forces him to engage the record before voters cast ballots is the open question of the cycle.
