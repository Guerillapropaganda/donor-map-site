Two filings. Two max-out checks. $78,400 the day before he debated on national TV.

The Resnicks paid Xavier Becerra to perform. Their checks cleared the day before the May 5 CNN debate. The 24-hour disclosure rule, designed to expose exactly this kind of last-minute money, forced the filing the next day. The public knows because California law required them to tell us. The interesting part is not the $78,400 itself. It is what else the Resnicks paid for the same week.

On April 10, 2026, Sergey Brin, the co-founder of Google, gave $9 million to a political action committee called Building a Better California. On the same day, Building a Better California sent $4.5 million to one ballot initiative committee and $4.5 million to another. The two ballot committees exist to kill California's 2026 Billionaire Tax Act, a one-time 5% tax on Californians worth over $1 billion. Stewart Resnick has put $2 million into the same anti-tax coalition. Christian Larsen, the co-founder of Ripple Labs, has put $2.5 million in. So have John Doerr, Michael Moritz, Patrick Collison, Eric Schmidt, Tony Xu, Max Levchin, and Peter Thiel. The coalition has raised $35 million.

Many of the same billionaires are also writing max-out checks to the people running for governor. Christian Larsen gave Katie Porter $117,600. He also gave Matt Mahan $500,000 and Steve Hilton $39,200. Three candidates. Two parties. One donor. Stewart and Lynda Resnick gave Antonio Villaraigosa $72,800 in 2024. They gave Matt Mahan $50,000 in March 2026. They gave Eric Swalwell $156,800 before he dropped out. They gave $250,000 to Gavin Newsom's ballot committee last fall. And on May 4, 2026, they gave Xavier Becerra $78,400.

The candidates are not enemies. They are hedges. The wealth tax is the actual thing the donors care about. The candidates exist to determine which version of "billionaires keep their money" gets enacted.

The $35 million coalition

Building a Better California is a political action committee registered with the California Secretary of State as filer ID 1486767. It is administered out of 2350 Kerner Boulevard, Suite 250, San Rafael, California, an address shared with two intermediaries named in its filings: Building a Better California Incorporated, the related corporate entity, and Compass4 Inc. The PAC's spokesperson is Abby Lunardi, formerly of the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative. Its legal work is handled by Nielsen Merksamer, a California political law firm. It was founded in January 2026, three months after California's 2026 Billionaire Tax Act began signature collection.

What they are defending

The 2026 Billionaire Tax Act, also known as Initiative 25-0024, is a proposed California ballot measure. It would impose a one-time 5% excise tax on the net worth of any California resident with assets over $1 billion, with a phase-out between $1 billion and $1.1 billion. Real estate, pensions, and retirement accounts are excluded. The Legislative Analyst's Office estimates it would raise tens of billions of dollars. Ninety percent of the revenue would go to healthcare. Ten percent would go to education, food assistance, and tax administration.

The proponent is the United Healthcare Workers West, a chapter of the Service Employees International Union. The signature-collection campaign reached 25% of its required signatures by March 2026. The measure is targeted for the November 2026 ballot.

The math is the part that explains the spending. The coalition has put $35 million into killing a tax that would cost the named donors several billion dollars combined. Brin's Alphabet stake alone would owe roughly $13 billion under the proposed tax. Resnick's Wonderful Company stake would owe roughly $250 million. The math justifies the political spend by orders of magnitude. Spending $35 million to avoid paying ten times that much is not extraordinary in this context. It is the cost of doing business.

The Brin flow · April 10, 2026

One Cal-Access filing shows the coalition's apparatus working in real time. On April 24, 2026, Building a Better California filed Form 497 Filing 3139587, a 24-hour late contribution report. The filing covers transactions on April 10, 2026.

In: Sergey Brin (CO-FOUNDER, GOOGLE), reported address Reno, Nevada, gave Building a Better California (filer 1486767) $9,000,000. The filing memo notes the contribution was "received through intermediaries, Building a Better California Incorporated" and "Compass4, Inc," both at the same San Rafael address.

Out, same day: Building a Better California gave $4.5 million to Californians for a More Transparent and Effective Government, the ballot committee for Initiative 25-0040A1. That initiative would require pre-election audits of programs funded by new state special taxes and prohibit certain new state taxes from being excluded from the existing voter-approved spending limit.

Out, same day: Building a Better California gave $4.5 million to Californians to Protect Retirement and Life Savings, the ballot committee for Initiative 25-0041A1. That initiative would prohibit new state personal property taxes and certain retroactive state taxes.

Both committees are sponsored by Building a Better California. Both initiatives are designed to either block the wealth tax procedurally or carve out the asset categories billionaires hold. The parent committee is a pass-through. Money in, money out, same day. The counter-initiatives are the actual spending vehicles.

The same office handles both

The Cal-Access filings give the address: 2350 Kerner Boulevard, Suite 250, San Rafael, California 94901. The contact name listed for the corporate intermediary is David Lazarus, a long-established California political treasurer whose firm services many statewide political committees. Compass4 Inc operates out of the same suite.

The same address appears on a different IRS filing. Steadfast LA, the rebuilding nonprofit Rick Caruso founded after the 2025 Pacific Palisades fire, is registered with the IRS at 2350 Kerner Boulevard, Suite 250, San Rafael, California 94901-5596, with David Lazarus listed as the contact. The same political-treasurer firm services Caruso's rebuilding nonprofit and the billionaire coalition's anti-wealth-tax PAC. The shared address is administratively normal in California political finance. Many committees use one treasurer firm. The substantive point is the colocation. The same operational apparatus that handles the $35 million billionaire tax-defense effort also handles the private rebuilding apparatus that is currently the lead public-private partner to Mayor Karen Bass on the Palisades fire reconstruction.

The signal

Five gubernatorial candidates have stated they oppose the wealth tax. Two have not stated a position. None have stated they support it. The coalition's $35 million is therefore not buying a change in policy outcomes. The policy outcome was already aligned.

What the money is buying is the certainty of alignment. The Resnick check on May 4 was not a bet that Becerra would win. It was a deposit, in case he does. The Larsen checks to Porter, Mahan, and Hilton are not endorsements of three different politicians. They are three independent insurance policies on the same regulatory question. If any one of them wins, the donor still has a relationship with the governor's office.

The donors are not picking sides because the sides are not where the actual fight is. The actual fight is Initiative 25-0024 versus the two counter-initiatives Building a Better California is funding into the same November 2026 ballot. The candidates exist downstream of that fight, as the people who will administer whatever survives. The donors are buying both ends of the lever.

The Resnicks paid Becerra $78,400 to perform on debate night. They paid the same week to kill the tax that would tax them. Both expenditures buy the same thing.

None of the donors named in this piece have been charged with any crime. Every contribution documented is a legal political contribution disclosed under California's Political Reform Act. The structural pattern, not the individual transaction, is what this investigation maps.
