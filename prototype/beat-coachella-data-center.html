The mayor who voted yes pleaded guilty.

Stronghold Power Systems wants to build a 270-megawatt data center on 240 acres of Coachella farmland. The mayor who took $4,900 from Stronghold and presided over the Feb 11 vote approving the deal pleaded guilty to a felony three weeks later. Stronghold's parent firm settled a $2.5 million federal fraud case in 2021 for using a sham veteran-owned company to win VA contracts.

The Coachella data center deal is a textbook donor-map case in three parts. A developer with a federal fraud settlement on its record. A mayor who took the developer's money and voted yes, then pleaded guilty to a felony on a different conflict. A new mayor and a community asking for a moratorium. Every fact in this story is in the public record. Nobody has yet put them in the same sentence.

The deal

The proposed project is the Coachella Valley Technology Center Project, also known as the Coachella Valley Technology Campus. It would occupy 240 acres at Avenue 52 and Fillmore Street, with potential expansion to 450 acres. The first phase includes three data center buildings of approximately one million square feet each, with a combined capacity of approximately 270 megawatts. Full build-out projections range up to 600 megawatts per DataCenterDynamics.

The developer is Stronghold Power Systems Inc., partnered with project-rep firm DMK Project Solutions Services, headquartered in Dulles, Virginia. The deal is structured as a public-private partnership creating a new municipal utility called the Coachella Municipal Utility. The Coachella City Council approved the Municipal Utility Development Agreement on February 11, 2026. Per Stronghold, the partnership had been in development since 2019.

The donor: Stronghold and the Bailey family

Stronghold Power Systems is a subsidiary of Stronghold Engineering Inc., a Riverside-based construction and engineering firm founded in 1991 by Scott Bailey and Beverly Bailey. Beverly Bailey is the company's CEO. Scott Bailey founded Stronghold Power Systems as a separate utility-infrastructure entity and serves as its founder while remaining COO at Stronghold Engineering.

In April 2021, Stronghold Engineering paid $2.5 million to the United States Department of Justice to resolve both criminal and civil investigations under a Non-Prosecution Agreement. The DOJ alleged that, from August 2007 to October 2013, Stronghold Engineering used a related shell entity called KPI, with a nominal veteran owner named Neff who did not actually control KPI day-to-day, to obtain fourteen federal construction contracts under the Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business set-aside program at the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs. The non-prosecution agreement required Stronghold Engineering to maintain an ethics and compliance program for two years.

The Bailey family has a documented history of political giving to Republican Inland Empire candidates. Beverly Bailey contributed multiple times to Congressman Ken Calvert's federal campaigns during 2009 and 2015. Charles Gossage, an Executive Vice President at Stronghold Engineering, gave Calvert $2,400 in June 2009. Scott Bailey is a member of the Lincoln Club of Riverside County, the regional Republican donor organization.

The mayor: Hernandez's conviction

Steven Hernandez served as Mayor of Coachella for twelve years. Stronghold Power Systems contributed $4,900 to his campaign. Hernandez presided over the February 11, 2026 City Council meeting that approved the Municipal Utility Development Agreement with Stronghold.

Three months earlier, on October 30, 2025, a Riverside County criminal grand jury returned a nine-count indictment against Hernandez: one felony count of violating Government Code Section 1090 (conflict of interest in government contracts), four felony counts of perjury under Penal Code Section 118 (false Statement of Economic Interests, the Form 700 every California elected official files annually), and four misdemeanor counts under Government Code Section 87100 (conflict of interest in governmental decisions).

In March 2026, Hernandez pleaded guilty to one felony conflict-of-interest charge. The Riverside County District Attorney's Office dropped the other eight charges as part of the plea agreement. Hernandez was sentenced to two years of formal probation and 200 hours of community service. He resigned from the mayor's office.

The Riverside County District Attorney's announcement of the indictment described the charges as relating to a contract between the City of Coachella and the Coachella Valley Association of Governments' Housing First Program, as well as Hernandez's "votes and advocacy related to downtown Coachella development projects." The Stronghold Municipal Utility Development Agreement is one of the major downtown Coachella development decisions of Hernandez's tenure.

The opposition

Per KESQ reporting, more than 100 Coachella residents gathered near Veterans Park on April 22, 2026, then packed the public comment slots at the City Council meeting calling for an immediate data center moratorium. Concerns raised in resident testimony included water consumption, energy demand, air quality, noise, and the loss of agricultural farmland. Across April's council meetings, more than 250 residents spoke against the project.

On April 22, 2026, the City Council appointed Dr. Frank Figueroa as the new mayor. Figueroa told KESQ that as a Coachella resident he had concerns about rising electricity rates and water use, and that his first priority as mayor would be to bring the moratorium question back to the council. The city held a community town hall on the proposal on May 11, 2026, at the Boys & Girls Club of Coachella Valley.

Open questions

Stronghold Power Systems has not publicly addressed how the parent company's $2.5 million Department of Justice Non-Prosecution Agreement and the documented use of a shell entity for federal contracting fraud bears on its current public-private partnership agreements with California municipalities.

The new Coachella City Council has not publicly addressed whether the Stronghold Municipal Utility Development Agreement approved by Hernandez before his felony plea will be reopened, reviewed, or rescinded.

DMK Project Solutions Services has not publicly addressed its due diligence into Stronghold Engineering's federal-contracting record before entering the Coachella partnership.
