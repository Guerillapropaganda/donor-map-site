He's the new Caruso.

Spencer Pratt is running for mayor of Los Angeles. He is also suing the City of Los Angeles. His donor list is the same Republican LA money that backed Rick Caruso against Karen Bass in 2022. Two February in-kind contributions came from corporate-named entities, which the city prohibits. And his $24,050 merchandise vendor is the long-time Pratt family graphic designer who has been producing their merch since the Pratt Daddy Crystals era five years ago.

Five Republican LA donors · 2022 Caruso · 2026 Pratt · same names, different candidate

Five donors who wrote checks to Rick Caruso's 2022 mayoral run against Karen Bass are now writing checks to Spencer Pratt's 2026 run against Karen Bass. Same Republican LA donor lane. Different candidate.

Spencer Pratt is running for Los Angeles mayor. He is also a plaintiff in a lawsuit against the City of Los Angeles and the Department of Water and Power, which he blames for the destruction of his and his parents' homes in the January 2025 Palisades fire. The Hills cast member, 41 years old, registered as a Republican in 2020, currently polling at about 11 percent. He qualified for the May 6 NBC4 mayoral debate. His campaign pitch is fire-victim grievance against Mayor Karen Bass. His parallel litigation seeks damages from the city he is asking voters to elect him to govern.

That is the simplest way to describe the structural posture of the Pratt 2026 campaign. The picture sharpens further when you read the LA City Ethics campaign-finance filings instead of the campaign website.

The committee Pratt for Mayor 2026 has raised $539,617 from 1,634 donors through April 18. Roughly a quarter of that, $131,251, is unitemized small-dollar contributions. The remaining three-quarters tells a different story than fire-victim populism. The top donor list is loaded with luxury real-estate developers from Scottsdale, Kamas, and Phoenix. With a Disney heir. With a Schwab. With the Gateway Computers founder. With Casamigos co-founder Michael Meldman, whose Discovery Land Company spokesperson now publicly denies the contribution the campaign filed under his name. And five of those names also wrote max-out checks to Rick Caruso when he ran against Karen Bass in 2022.

The campaign filed two non-monetary contributions in February from entities whose filed names are corporate. Los Angeles prohibits corporate contributions to candidates. And the campaign paid $24,050 to a Shopify-based merchandise brand operated by a longtime Pratt family graphic designer whose own portfolio identifies Pratt Daddy Crystals as a former client and whose current product line includes 28 Heidi Montag-themed celebrity tees alongside PRATT2026 campaign tees on the same store.

This investigation reads Pratt's CA Form 460 Schedule A and Schedule C filings, his Schedule E expenditure record, and cross-references against the same database for Rick Caruso's 2022 mayoral committee. All dollar figures and donor names are sourced to the LA City Ethics Commission campaign-finance public dataset on data.lacity.org. The full source list and methodology appear at the bottom of this page.

Act I: The pitch

Spencer Pratt and his wife Heidi Montag became reality-TV figures on MTV's The Hills from 2006 to 2010. After the show, they stayed in Pacific Palisades. They ran a crystal-merchandise business called Pratt Daddy. They had children. They appeared on a 2019 spinoff show called The Hills: New Beginnings. Pratt registered as a Republican in 2020, switching from Independent.

In January 2025, the Palisades fire destroyed their home and the home of Pratt's parents. By the spring of 2026 he was running for Los Angeles mayor as a Republican against incumbent Karen Bass and progressive councilmember Nithya Raman. UCLA Luskin polling around the May 6 NBC4 mayoral debate had Pratt at 11 percent, ahead of Raman's 9. He qualified for the debate stage. Bass had publicly said before the debate she had "never heard of" him and accused him of "exploiting the grief of people in the Palisades." Pratt responded, on Fox News and in an emotional ad featuring his mother, that Bass had "let my house burn down."

That is the campaign pitch. Fire-victim populism. The donor map's job is to show what the receipts say next to that pitch.

Act II: He is suing the city

Spencer and Heidi Pratt filed suit against the City of Los Angeles and the Department of Water and Power on January 21, 2025, two weeks after the Palisades fire destroyed their home. Co-plaintiffs include Spencer's parents Janet Pratt and William Pratt, plus 20+ additional Palisades property owners. The case was filed in Los Angeles County Superior Court. Plaintiffs' counsel: the McNulty Law Firm of Los Angeles. Lead attorney Peter J. McNulty is also Plaintiffs' Liaison Counsel for the broader consolidated Palisades-fire litigation. The Pratt complaint was consolidated under the lead case Grigsby v. City of Los Angeles, No. 25STCV00832.

The legal theory: that around 10:30 p.m. on January 7, 2025, power lines downed by the Palisades fire could have caused a secondary fire that destroyed additional homes including the Pratts'. LADWP's stated defense is that the lines in question were manually de-energized around 2:15 p.m. earlier that day. Judge Samantha Jessner ruled on February 19, 2026 that property and business owners could pursue inverse-condemnation claims against the city and LADWP. The California Court of Appeal denied the City's and the State's petitions to halt those claims. The litigation is active and in discovery. No trial date has been set. More than 10,000 residents, businesses, and insurers are now part of the coordinated litigation.

Spencer Pratt is a plaintiff in that consolidated lawsuit. He is a plaintiff against the city government he is now asking voters to elect him to lead. He is a plaintiff against LADWP, the city-owned utility that the next mayor will appoint commissioners to oversee.

Spencer Pratt is running for mayor of Los Angeles. He is also suing the City of Los Angeles.

Pratt has been asked publicly whether he would drop the lawsuit if elected. On March 27, 2026, in an interview with Fox 11 LA reporter Marla Tellez, he answered:

"Absolutely not. The city failed over 10,000 people. 12 of them died, 7,000 structures. And that lawsuit is going forward. Thankfully, I'm not running to be the judge. The case is gonna be in front of a judge, and the judge will have the ruling. So there would be no conflict because the mayor does not control the outcome of that lawsuit."

That is Pratt's own framing of the conflict-of-interest question. The position rests on a narrow interpretation of mayoral authority. The mayor of Los Angeles appoints LADWP commissioners (subject to City Council confirmation), directs the city attorney's office, controls the city's litigation-defense budget, and signs or vetoes settlement appropriations. Whether the mayor "does not control the outcome" of a city-versus-resident lawsuit in which the mayor is the named plaintiff is a structural question voters can weigh. The donor map's job is to show that he has been asked, and that this is what he said.

Whether Spencer Pratt and his co-plaintiffs would receive any settlement or judgment from the litigation, and whether any portion of those proceeds would benefit the candidate personally or his immediate family, is open. The complaint seeks damages "according to proof" with no specified dollar figure.

Los Angeles City Charter Section 470 and Los Angeles Municipal Code Section 49.7.13 prohibit contributions to candidates for elective city office from "corporations, including foreign corporations, business entities, partnerships, joint ventures, syndicates, and associations." The prohibition was strengthened by Measure SS, which Los Angeles voters approved in 2022. (URL NEEDED: link to LA Ethics Commission's published explainer of the corporate-contribution ban.) The Fair Political Practices Commission treats non-monetary in-kind goods and services as contributions for the purposes of contribution limits and source restrictions.

The first of the two contributions was filed under the name KOMOS TEQUILA. The legal entity behind the Komos Tequila brand is Komos, Inc., identified by name throughout the brand's terms of use at komos.com and in its website footer ("© 2025 Komos, inc."). Komos was founded in spring 2019 by Master Sommelier Richard Betts and entrepreneur Joe Marchese, and is operated under Casa Komos Brands Group (CKBG) per BusinessWire's February 9, 2021 launch coverage and Robb Report's profile (republished on the official ckbg.com press archive). E. & J. Gallo Winery's Spirit of Gallo subsidiary made a strategic investment in August 2022 and assumed U.S. distribution rights.

The second contribution was filed under the name DELTA SPECIAL OPERATIONS CORP. The contributor's filed name self-identifies as a corporation by including the "Corp" suffix. The address on file is in Santa Rosa Valley, in Ventura County's Camarillo area. (URL NEEDED: California Secretary of State business search confirmation of entity status and registered agent.)

The legal characterization of either contribution is the FPPC's call and the LA Ethics Commission's, not this site's. What is documentary on the face of the filings:

Pratt's mayoral campaign reported, in its own CA Form 460 filings, two non-monetary in-kind contributions from entities whose filed names identify them as corporations. Los Angeles prohibits corporate contributions to candidates.

A separate question is the accuracy of the filing itself. The LA Ethics dataset shows the contributor name as "KOMOS TEQUILA" rather than the legal entity name "Komos, Inc." Whether the campaign's identification of the contributor on its own forms is itself a reporting issue is a question for the LA Ethics Commission's review process.

Act IV: The Meldman contribution dispute

The campaign's CA Form 460 Schedule A filing reports a personal contribution of $1,885 from Michael Meldman, dated April 14, 2026, identified as "Real Estate Developer, Discovery Land Company." Discovery Land is Meldman's luxury real-estate firm. He is a longtime Republican federal donor (FEC records show approximately $84,200 to the National Republican Congressional Committee in 2021 alone, plus contributions to Trump-aligned committees in subsequent cycles).

Meldman, through Discovery Land Company, denies the contribution. Per The Real Deal's April 28, 2026 reporting on real-estate money in the LA mayoral race: "A spokesperson for Discovery Land Company said the information is inaccurate and that Meldman did not contribute to the campaign."

That places the LA Ethics filing and the donor's own representative in direct contradiction. The CA Form 460 was signed and filed by the Pratt campaign's treasurer under penalty of perjury. The filing identifies Meldman by name and employer. Discovery Land's spokesperson, on the record to a real-estate trade publication, says the contribution did not happen. Both statements cannot be true. The donor map's job is to show the contradiction. The legal question of which side is correct is for the FPPC's review process, not this site.

The same Meldman name has historically been linked, in industry coverage going back to 2017, to spirits ventures including the Casamigos tequila brand he co-founded with George Clooney and Rande Gerber (sold to Diageo in 2017 for up to $1 billion). At the start of this research the donor map presumed Meldman might also be tied to Komos Tequila, the legal entity behind the Feb 1 in-kind contribution to Pratt's campaign. That presumption was wrong. Per BusinessWire (February 9, 2021) and Robb Report (via the official Casa Komos Brands Group press archive), Komos was founded by Master Sommelier Richard Betts and entrepreneur Joe Marchese in spring 2019. Komos is operated under Casa Komos Brands Group (CKBG). Spirit of Gallo (E. & J. Gallo Winery) made a strategic investment and assumed U.S. distribution rights in August 2022. No primary or named-publication source reviewed for this beat connects Mike Meldman to Komos in any capacity.

Komos remains the legal entity that contributed $1,800 in beverages to Pratt's campaign on February 1, 2026. Komos remains a corporation. Los Angeles still prohibits corporate contributions to candidates. The Komos finding does not depend on Meldman.

Act V: He's the new Caruso

Rick Caruso is a Los Angeles billionaire developer who ran for mayor in 2022 as a recently-converted Republican against Karen Bass. He lost. He has since founded Steadfast LA, the private-sector-led Palisades-rebuild organization that partners with Bass's city government. He is, in 2026, a quieter figure than in 2022.

But the donor base he assembled in 2022 is not quiet. It is funding Spencer Pratt.

Cross-referencing Pratt's named max-out donors against the LA City Ethics database for Caruso's 2022 mayoral committee returns the following overlaps:

That is the headline of this beat. Five named donors. The same Republican LA donor coalition that paid for Caruso's run against Bass in 2022 is now paying for Pratt's run against Bass in 2026. Different candidate. Different vehicle. Same money.

Pratt's broader donor list extends the pattern. The April 2026 reporting period saw a coordinated wave of out-of-state luxury real-estate developer max-outs land within a two-week window: Meldman from Scottsdale, Arizona on April 14 (the contribution Discovery Land now denies). David Wolf of Wolf Residential Development from Kamas, Utah on April 15. Douglas Reinhardt of ECG from Laguna Niguel on April 11. Kurt Rappaport of Westside Estate Agency from Beverly Hills on April 16. Brian Timmons of the Quinn Emanuel law firm on April 17. Ted and Michele Waitt on April 7. The pattern is not a small-dollar national grievance fundraising surge. It is a coordinated set of high-dollar checks from a recognizable donor cohort.

The fundraising operation behind these events is documented as well. Pluvious Group, run by Trey Kozacik (trey@pluvious.com), is the Pratt campaign's primary fundraiser organizer per LA Times coverage and the campaign's own eFundraising-Connections records. Pluvious organized the Feb 14 LALovesSpencer reception at Perry's Beach Club, the April 28 fundraiser at Geoffrey H. Palmer's Beverly Hills home (Palmer is a Los Angeles mega-developer and longtime Republican megadonor), and the April 30 MandevilleCanyon event. Pluvious's prior client list, per LA Times reporting from December 7, 2019, includes a 2019 Trump fundraiser in Los Angeles and a 2019 Jackie Lacey fundraiser. The Pratt campaign's fundraising backbone is documented Trump-orbit consultancy.

Pratt's third-largest documented payee on the campaign's CA Form 460 Schedule E is Highland Political LLC, which received $63,893 in 11 transactions across the same reporting period for "Fundraising Events" services and travel reimbursement. Tennessee Secretary of State business search confirms Highland Political LLC is not registered in Tennessee, despite the LinkedIn listing showing a Nashville address. The domain highlandpolitical.com does not resolve. FEC records show zero other political clients for Highland Political LLC. The Pratt campaign is its only known client in any accessible federal or state campaign-finance database.

Act VI: The merchandise vendor

The expenditure side of Pratt's filings carries a finding the contribution side does not.

The campaign's CA Form 460 Schedule E (expenditures) for the same reporting period reports $24,050 paid to a vendor named NEWPORT BLEACH, in three transactions, all coded as "Campaign Paraphernalia, Miscellaneous." The vendor address is Newport Beach, California. The same Schedule E lists $63,893 to Highland Political LLC for fundraising-event services and travel reimbursement, $25,850 to Tag Strategies for campaign consulting, and smaller payments to a roster of other consultants and staff. Newport Bleach is the campaign's third-largest expenditure category overall.

Newport Bleach is operated by graphic designer Tyler Nichols, who works publicly under the brand "Tyler Treats" at tylertreats.com. His own portfolio identifies Pratt Daddy Crystals as a former graphic-design client, alongside Anaheim Ducks NHL warm-up shirt designs and concert-merchandise work for various bands. His Twitter biography names Newport Bleach. His Newport Bleach blog credits him as the author of news posts including a Heidi Montag-related piece ("Heidi Montag - Wet Hot Summmer"). Forbes covered his work designing the visual world of the Moviestarz debut album on November 11, 2025. This Modern Struggle Magazine reported on October 10, 2025 that "Newport Bleach designed a custom tee to help raise money for reality stars Heidi Montag and Spencer Pratt when they lost their home to the LA fires." The same vendor that produced fire-relief fundraising merchandise for the Pratts in 2025 is producing PRATT2026 campaign merchandise in 2026.

What this beat does not allege

Boundaries on the receipts above

  • This beat does not allege Spencer Pratt knew the contributors of the two February in-kind contributions were corporations, or that he personally directed the acceptance.
  • This beat does not allege Newport Bleach is owned or controlled by Spencer Pratt, Heidi Pratt, or any related party. Public records identify graphic designer Tyler Nichols as the operator. Whether the underlying legal entity is an LLC, Inc., DBA, or sole proprietorship is open.
  • This beat does not allege Tyler Nichols has done anything wrong. He is a graphic designer with a documented five-year vendor history with the Pratt family. The structural finding concerns the campaign's choice of vendor, not the vendor's conduct.
  • This beat does not allege Michael Meldman did or did not make the contribution the campaign filed under his name on April 14, 2026. The campaign's CA Form 460 reports the contribution under penalty of perjury. Discovery Land Company's spokesperson, on the record, says the contribution did not occur. The donor map preserves both statements as documentary; resolving which is correct is for the FPPC's review process.
  • This beat does not allege a connection between Michael Meldman and the Komos Tequila brand. The donor map's earlier internal research presumed such a link; primary-source review of BusinessWire, Robb Report, and Casa Komos Brands Group's own press materials shows Komos was founded by Richard Betts and Joe Marchese with no Meldman involvement. The earlier presumption was wrong.
  • This beat does not allege Spencer Pratt's Palisades fire-victim status is fabricated. Public reporting confirms his home and his parents' home were destroyed.
  • This beat does not allege Pratt's lawsuit against the City of Los Angeles and LADWP is meritless or improperly brought. Whether the lawsuit succeeds on the merits is for the courts. The structural finding concerns the conflict-of-interest posture of a candidate suing the office he is running to hold, plus his own public statement on the question.
  • This beat does not allege the donors named here did anything illegal. Individual contributions at the legal maximum are legal. The structural finding is the cohort, not any single donor.
  • This beat does not characterize the Pratt campaign as a "grift" or any related characterization. The receipts are documentary; the editorial framing belongs to the reader.

The donor map's job is to surface the structural pattern in the receipts. The legal characterization of any contribution belongs to the FPPC and the LA Ethics Commission. The editorial characterization of the candidate belongs to voters.

Open questions

Whether Pratt's campaign will return the two corporate-named in-kind contributions. California Government Code Section 84308 and LA Ethics rules permit a candidate to return a contribution that exceeds the limit or violates a source restriction within a defined return window. Whether Pratt's campaign treats the Komos and Delta Special Operations Corp contributions as returnable, retains them, or amends the filings to reidentify the contributors as individuals, is open as of the latest reporting period.

Whether Spencer Pratt would recuse from administrative or budget decisions affecting his own pending lawsuit against the City of Los Angeles and LADWP if he wins the office. A candidate suing the office he is running to hold is in a structural conflict-of-interest posture. The campaign has not, in named-publication coverage reviewed for this beat, publicly committed to a recusal protocol or to dropping the lawsuit if elected. Whether the lawsuit will be settled, dropped, or pursued in parallel with a hypothetical Pratt mayoralty is open.

Whether the Pratt family has a formal licensing arrangement with Tyler Nichols / Newport Bleach for use of Heidi Montag's name, song titles, album branding, and image on merchandise. The Newport Bleach Heidi product collection contains 28 items branded with Heidi Montag references. Whether these are produced under license, under a personal-vendor handshake, or under some other arrangement, is not disclosed publicly. Whether any portion of Newport Bleach merchandise revenue flows back to the Pratt family is open.

Whether the campaign will publicly explain why a Pennsylvania-based individual (Alexandra Evers, Sewickley, PA) is on payroll for an Los Angeles mayoral campaign. Evers received $2,732 in "Campaign Workers Salaries" while also contributing $1,138 to the campaign. The campaign has not, in named-publication coverage reviewed, addressed her role.

Whether the candidate will address the donor-cohort overlap with the 2022 Caruso campaign. The five named donors who funded both Caruso 2022 and Pratt 2026 represent a documented cohort. Whether Pratt characterizes the overlap as coincidental, as continuity of a Republican LA donor lane, or otherwise, is open.
