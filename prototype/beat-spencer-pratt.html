Five Republican LA donors · 2022 Caruso · 2026 Pratt · same names, different candidate

Five donors who wrote checks to Rick Caruso's 2022 mayoral run against Karen Bass are now writing checks to Spencer Pratt's 2026 run against Karen Bass. Same Republican LA donor lane. Different candidate.

Spencer Pratt is running for Los Angeles mayor. He is also a plaintiff in a lawsuit against the City of Los Angeles and the Department of Water and Power, which he blames for the destruction of his and his parents' homes in the January 2025 Palisades fire. The Hills cast member, 41 years old, registered as a Republican in 2020, currently polling at about 11 percent. He qualified for the May 6 NBC4 mayoral debate. His campaign pitch is fire victim grievance against Mayor Karen Bass. His parallel litigation seeks damages from the city he is asking voters to elect him to govern.

That is the simplest way to describe the structural posture of the Pratt 2026 campaign. The picture sharpens further when you read the LA City Ethics campaign finance filings instead of the campaign website.

The committee Pratt for Mayor 2026 has raised $539,617 from 1,634 donors through April 18. Roughly a quarter of that, $131,251, is unitemized small dollar contributions. The remaining three quarters tells a different story than fire victim populism. The top donor list is loaded with luxury real estate developers from Scottsdale, Kamas, and Phoenix. With a Disney heir. With a Schwab. With the Gateway Computers founder. With Casamigos co-founder Michael Meldman, whose Discovery Land Company spokesperson now publicly denies the contribution the campaign filed under his name. And five of those names also wrote max-out checks to Rick Caruso when he ran against Karen Bass in 2022.

The campaign filed two non-monetary contributions in February from entities whose filed names are corporate. Los Angeles prohibits corporate contributions to candidates. And the campaign paid $24,050 to a Shopify-based merchandise brand operated by a longtime Pratt family graphic designer whose own portfolio identifies Pratt Daddy Crystals as a former client and whose current product line includes 28 Heidi Montag-themed celebrity tees alongside PRATT2026 campaign tees on the same store.

This investigation reads Pratt's CA Form 460 Schedule A and Schedule C filings, his Schedule E expenditure record, and cross-references against the same database for Rick Caruso's 2022 mayoral committee. All dollar figures and donor names are sourced to the LA City Ethics Commission campaign finance public dataset on data.lacity.org. The full source list and methodology appear at the bottom of this page.

The pitch

Spencer Pratt and his wife Heidi Montag became reality-TV figures on MTV's The Hills from 2006 to 2010. After the show, they stayed in Pacific Palisades. They ran a crystal merchandise business called Pratt Daddy. They had children. They appeared on a 2019 spinoff show called The Hills: New Beginnings. Pratt registered as a Republican in 2020, switching from Independent.

In January 2025, the Palisades fire destroyed their home and the home of Pratt's parents. By the spring of 2026 he was running for Los Angeles mayor as a Republican against incumbent Karen Bass and progressive councilmember Nithya Raman. UCLA Luskin polling around the May 6 NBC4 mayoral debate had Pratt at 11 percent, ahead of Raman's 9. He qualified for the debate stage. Bass had publicly said before the debate she had "never heard of" him and accused him of "exploiting the grief of people in the Palisades." Pratt responded, on Fox News and in an emotional ad featuring his mother, that Bass had "let my house burn down."

That is the campaign pitch. Fire-victim populism. The donor map's job is to show what the receipts say next to that pitch.

Two corporate in-kind contributions

Every California political campaign has to file a form called the CA Form 460 listing where its money comes from and where it goes. The form has different sections ("Schedules") for different kinds of contributions. Schedule A lists cash donations from named people. Schedule C lists non-cash contributions: donated goods and services, things like free beverages at an event or donated event security. These count toward the same dollar limits as cash donations.

Pratt's Schedule C filings for February 2026 list two non-cash contributions. Both at the legal maximum a single donor can give in an LA mayoral primary. Both filed under names that, on their face, identify corporate entities (companies) rather than individual people.

Los Angeles bans companies from giving money to candidates running for city office. The rule lives in the LA City Charter Section 470, "Limitations on Campaign Contributions in City Elections." It applies to all kinds of business entities: for-profit corporations, foreign corporations, partnerships, joint ventures, syndicates, business associations. The corresponding administrative rules are published by the LA Ethics Commission's Laws hub. California's campaign finance regulator (the Fair Political Practices Commission, or FPPC) treats donated goods and services the same as cash for purposes of contribution limits and source rules. So a corporation giving $1,800 worth of free tequila to a campaign is treated, under the law, the same as a corporation handing the campaign a $1,800 check.

He is suing the city

Spencer and Heidi Pratt sued the City of Los Angeles and its city-owned utility, the Department of Water and Power (LADWP), on January 21, 2025, two weeks after the Palisades fire destroyed their home. Their co-plaintiffs are Spencer's parents Janet Pratt and William Pratt, plus more than twenty other Palisades property owners. The case was filed in Los Angeles County Superior Court. Their lawyer is the McNulty Law Firm of Los Angeles, and lead attorney Peter J. McNulty is also the official spokesperson-lawyer ("Plaintiffs' Liaison Counsel") for the broader group of Palisades-fire cases now joined together into one coordinated proceeding. The Pratt complaint is part of that bigger consolidated case, Grigsby v. City of Los Angeles, No. 25STCV00832 (LA County Superior Court, Department 7, Judge Samantha Jessner presiding at the Spring Street Courthouse).

The plaintiffs' theory of the case: around 10:30 p.m. on January 7, 2025, power lines knocked down by the Palisades fire could have sparked a second fire that destroyed additional homes, including the Pratts'. LADWP's defense is that the lines in question had been shut off manually around 2:15 p.m. that afternoon, hours earlier. On February 19, 2026, Judge Samantha Jessner ruled that property and business owners could move forward with a specific kind of claim known as "inverse condemnation." In plain terms, that is a claim that the government's actions or failures effectively took or destroyed your property, which obliges the government to pay for it. The California Court of Appeal refused the City's and the State's requests to stop those claims. The lawsuit is now in the evidence-gathering phase. No trial date has been set. More than 10,000 residents, business owners, and insurance companies are now part of the coordinated case.

Spencer Pratt is a plaintiff in that consolidated lawsuit. He is a plaintiff against the city government he is now asking voters to elect him to lead. He is a plaintiff against LADWP, the city-owned utility that the next mayor will appoint commissioners to oversee. His mother, Janet Pratt, is a co-plaintiff in the same lawsuit. She is also listed on his Schedule A donor list at a $158 itemized contribution from January 19, 2026. The dollar figure is small. The structural detail is the dual disclosure: the candidate's mother is on the public record as both a plaintiff against the city and an itemized donor to the campaign asking voters to put her son in charge of it.

Spencer Pratt is running for mayor of Los Angeles. He is also suing the City of Los Angeles.

Pratt has been asked publicly whether he would drop the lawsuit if elected. On March 27, 2026, in an interview with Fox 11 LA reporter Marla Tellez, he answered:

"Absolutely not. The city failed over 10,000 people. 12 of them died, 7,000 structures. And that lawsuit is going forward. Thankfully, I'm not running to be the judge. The case is gonna be in front of a judge, and the judge will have the ruling. So there would be no conflict because the mayor does not control the outcome of that lawsuit."

That is Pratt's own answer to the conflict of interest question. The answer rests on a narrow reading of what the mayor of Los Angeles actually does. In reality, the LA mayor has several specific powers that touch this lawsuit directly: the mayor appoints the LADWP's governing commissioners (subject to City Council confirmation), oversees the City Attorney's office that defends the city in lawsuits, controls the city's legal-defense budget, and signs or vetoes any settlement money the city agrees to pay. Whether the mayor "does not control the outcome" of a lawsuit in which the mayor himself is one of the people suing the city is a fair question for voters to weigh. The donor map's job is to show that he has been asked and to print what he said.

Whether Spencer Pratt and his co-plaintiffs would eventually receive any settlement money or court-awarded damages from this lawsuit, and whether any portion of that money would benefit the candidate or his immediate family personally, is an open question. The lawsuit asks for damages "according to proof," meaning whatever amount the evidence eventually supports, and does not name a specific dollar figure.

A related update on Pratt's posture in the public race: a televised mayoral forum had been scheduled for May 13, 2026, organized by the League of Women Voters and the Pat Brown Institute for Public Affairs and broadcast by Fox 11 LA, with five candidates invited (Bass, Raman, Pratt, businessman Adam Miller, and community advocate Rae Huang). Pratt was the first candidate to decline, citing a scheduling conflict. Mayor Bass withdrew next, stating she would travel to Sacramento to lobby state officials on homelessness and Palisades fire recovery. Nithya Raman then withdrew, saying she had agreed to appear specifically to debate Bass. The forum was canceled. Only Adam Miller and Rae Huang remained on the invitation list. The cancellation was reported by Breitbart, KTLA, Westside Current, and TheWrap.

The Meldman contribution dispute

The campaign's CA Form 460 Schedule A filing reports a personal contribution of $1,885 from Michael Meldman, dated April 14, 2026, identified as "Real Estate Developer, Discovery Land Company." Discovery Land is Meldman's luxury real estate firm. He is a longtime Republican federal donor (FEC records show approximately $84,200 to the National Republican Congressional Committee in 2021 alone, plus contributions to Trump-aligned committees in subsequent cycles).

Meldman, through Discovery Land Company, denies the contribution. Per The Real Deal's April 28, 2026 reporting on real estate money in the LA mayoral race: "A spokesperson for Discovery Land Company said the information is inaccurate and that Meldman did not contribute to the campaign."

That places the LA Ethics filing and the donor's own representative in direct contradiction. The CA Form 460 was signed and filed by the Pratt campaign's treasurer under penalty of perjury. The filing identifies Meldman by name and employer. Discovery Land's spokesperson, on the record to a real estate trade publication, says the contribution did not happen. Both statements cannot be true. The donor map's job is to show the contradiction. The legal question of which side is correct is for the FPPC's review process, not this site.

The same Meldman name has historically been linked, in industry coverage going back to 2017, to spirits ventures including the Casamigos tequila brand he co-founded with George Clooney and Rande Gerber (sold to Diageo in 2017 for up to $1 billion). At the start of this research the donor map presumed Meldman might also be tied to Komos Tequila, the legal entity behind the Feb 1 in-kind contribution to Pratt's campaign. That presumption was wrong. Per BusinessWire (February 9, 2021) and Robb Report (via the official Casa Komos Brands Group press archive), Komos was founded by Master Sommelier Richard Betts and entrepreneur Joe Marchese in spring 2019. Komos is operated under Casa Komos Brands Group (CKBG). Spirit of Gallo (E. & J. Gallo Winery) made a strategic investment and assumed U.S. distribution rights in August 2022. No primary or named-publication source reviewed for this beat connects Mike Meldman to Komos in any capacity.

Komos remains the legal entity that contributed $1,800 in beverages to Pratt's campaign on February 1, 2026. Komos remains a corporation. Los Angeles still prohibits corporate contributions to candidates. The Komos finding does not depend on Meldman.

He's the new Caruso

Rick Caruso is a Los Angeles billionaire developer who ran for mayor in 2022 as a recently-converted Republican against Karen Bass. He lost. He has since founded Steadfast LA, the private-sector-led Palisades-rebuild organization that partners with Bass's city government. He is, in 2026, a quieter figure than in 2022.

But the donor base he assembled in 2022 is not quiet. It is funding Spencer Pratt.

Two structural patterns repeat across this list. The first is couples writing two max-out checks under separate names: Ted and Michele Waitt, Mark and Kelly Ann Holscher, Casey and Catherine Adams, John and Callene Momtazee. Each pair effectively doubles a single household's contribution. The Holscher pair is the cleanest example: Mark Holscher is a Kirkland & Ellis partner; Kelly Ann Holscher is identified on the filing as "retired"; their joint contribution lands at $3,611 from one Pacific Palisades address. The pattern is legal. The Federal Election Commission and California's campaign finance regulator both permit each individual in a household to give independently. It is, even so, a method by which a single household reaches a multiple of the per-donor limit.

The second pattern is geographic concentration outside California. The Pratt fundraising appeal is supposed to be local: a Palisades fire victim running for LA mayor. But out of the named donors, at least seventeen wrote checks from outside California: Dallas, Mercer Island, Denton, Kamas, Scottsdale, Bentonville, Phoenix, Monmouth Beach, Las Vegas, Castle Pines, New York, Austin, Lake Forest, Henderson, North Miami Beach, Little Chute, Sewickley, Sarasota, Paradise Valley, Nashville, Miami, Stilwell. The donor coalition stretches across at least fourteen states.

Haim Saban is the founder and chairman of Saban Capital Group, the Israeli-American media billionaire (Forbes estimates net worth at approximately $3 billion) who co-produced Mighty Morphin Power Rangers in the 1990s, led the consortium that acquired Univision in 2007 for $13.7 billion, and built the Saban Center for Middle East Policy at the Brookings Institution. The Sabans never directly supported Karen Bass. The political-philosophical signal was visible a year earlier at the federal level. The email was widely understood as a public signal of the most prominent pro-Israel Democratic mega-donor putting the Biden coalition on notice. The 2026 Pratt contribution arrives roughly a year after that public break.

What this beat does not allege

Boundaries on the receipts above

  • This beat does not allege Spencer Pratt knew the contributors of the two February in-kind contributions were corporations, or that he personally directed the acceptance.
  • This beat does not allege Newport Bleach is owned or controlled by Spencer Pratt, Heidi Pratt, or any related party. Public records identify graphic designer Tyler Nichols as the operator. Whether the underlying legal entity is an LLC, Inc., DBA, or sole proprietorship is open.
  • This beat does not allege Tyler Nichols has done anything wrong. He is a graphic designer with a documented five year vendor history with the Pratt family. The structural finding concerns the campaign's choice of vendor, not the vendor's conduct.
  • This beat does not allege Michael Meldman did or did not make the contribution the campaign filed under his name on April 14, 2026. The campaign's CA Form 460 reports the contribution under penalty of perjury. Discovery Land Company's spokesperson, on the record, says the contribution did not occur. The donor map preserves both statements as documentary; resolving which is correct is for the FPPC's review process.
  • This beat does not allege a connection between Michael Meldman and the Komos Tequila brand. The donor map's earlier internal research presumed such a link; primary source review of BusinessWire, Robb Report, and Casa Komos Brands Group's own press materials shows Komos was founded by Richard Betts and Joe Marchese with no Meldman involvement.   • This beat does not allege Spencer Pratt's Palisades fire victim status is fabricated. Public reporting confirms his home and his parents' home were destroyed.
  • This beat does not allege Pratt's lawsuit against the City of Los Angeles and LADWP is meritless or improperly brought. Whether the lawsuit succeeds on the merits is for the courts.   • This beat does not allege the donors named here did anything illegal. Individual contributions at the legal maximum are legal. The structural finding is the cohort, not any single donor.
  • This beat does not characterize the Pratt campaign as a "grift" or any related characterization. The receipts are documentary; the editorial framing belongs to the reader.

The donor map's job is to surface the structural pattern in the receipts. The legal characterization of any contribution belongs to the FPPC and the LA Ethics Commission. The editorial characterization of the candidate belongs to voters.

Open questions

Whether Pratt's campaign will return the two corporate named in-kind contributions. California Government Code Section 84308 and LA Ethics rules permit a candidate to return a contribution that exceeds the limit or violates a source restriction within a defined return window. Whether Pratt's campaign treats the Komos and Delta Special Operations Corp contributions as returnable, retains them, or amends the filings to reidentify the contributors as individuals, is open as of the latest reporting period.

Whether Spencer Pratt would recuse from administrative or budget decisions affecting his own pending lawsuit against the City of Los Angeles and LADWP if he wins the office. A candidate suing the office he is running to hold is in a structural conflict of interest posture. The campaign has not, in named-publication coverage reviewed for this beat, publicly committed to a recusal protocol or to dropping the lawsuit if elected. Whether the lawsuit will be settled, dropped, or pursued in parallel with a hypothetical Pratt mayoralty is open.

Whether the Pratt family has a formal licensing arrangement with Tyler Nichols / Newport Bleach for use of Heidi Montag's name, song titles, album branding, and image on merchandise. The Newport Bleach Heidi product collection contains 28 items branded with Heidi Montag references. Whether these are produced under license, under a personal-vendor handshake, or under some other arrangement, is not disclosed publicly. Whether any portion of Newport Bleach merchandise revenue flows back to the Pratt family is open.

Whether the campaign will publicly explain why a Pennsylvania-based individual (Alexandra Evers, Sewickley, PA) is on payroll for an Los Angeles mayoral campaign.
