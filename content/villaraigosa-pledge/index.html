The pledge he broke. The donors who took it.

In 2018, Antonio Villaraigosa publicly promised never to take fossil-fuel money. In 2026, oil and gas companies are his largest organized donor bloc, while the institutional donor class that put $22 million into his last governor run quietly walked. Even Reed Hastings moved on.

2018 pledge · 2026 ledger · the same fossil-fuel companies on both sides of the line

The 2018 promise is on the left. The 2026 receipts are on the right. Same companies. No retraction. No re-signing.

In 2018, Antonio Villaraigosa was running for governor and signed a pledge. No contributions from oil companies. No contributions from named fossil-fuel executives. The pledge was the new rule going forward.

He has not re-signed that pledge in 2026.

He has, however, accepted $464,800 in primary-and-general contributions from California's biggest oil and gas producers and a major natural gas vehicle-fueling company: $72,800 from California Resources Corporation (the state's largest oil-and-gas producer, spun off from Occidental Petroleum), $78,400 each from Chevron, Marathon Petroleum, and Berry Corporation, plus $156,800 from Clean Energy (the natural-gas-vehicle fueling firm). His campaign bills him as the working-class affordability candidate. Restrictions on California oil production he calls "poppycock." The donors agree.

The 2018 promise is gone. The donors who would have been excluded under the 2018 pledge appear at the top of the 2026 ledger. And the donor class that put $22 million into his 2018 IE through Reed Hastings's checkbook? That money walked. Hastings's 2026 governor money went to Matt Mahan instead. The candidate who ran in 2018 as the donor class's investment vehicle is running in 2026 as the candidate the donor class moved off of, while still taking maxes from the industry he had once promised to refuse.

This beat sits alongside two other 2026 California Governor primary investigations. $31 million to bury a class traitor documents the regulated-industry coalition spending against Tom Steyer. Three Becerras documents the Democratic candidate the donor class is consolidating behind. This page documents the third Democrat in the consolidation pattern: the candidate who broke a promise and watched his historical donor base leave anyway.

The 2018 pledge

The Los Angeles Times documented Villaraigosa's 2018 pledge in its May 26, 2025 reporting on the 2026 race. Villaraigosa had signed a public commitment, during his prior 2018 gubernatorial campaign, not to accept contributions from oil companies or from named fossil-fuel executives at fossil-fuel entities. The pledge came shortly after he had accepted maximum-allowable contributions from several oil donors months earlier in 2017, but was the new rule going forward.

In 2026, he has not re-signed that pledge. He has not made any public statement that he no longer considers himself bound by it. He has simply accepted the maximums and not addressed the contradiction. The LA Times noted his current "all-of-the-above" energy framing, in which he describes restrictions on California oil production as "poppycock."

Single-payer opposition meets healthcare-industry money

Villaraigosa is the only major Democratic gubernatorial candidate to explicitly oppose creating a state single-payer healthcare system. The LA Times (November 8, 2025) reported that at a 2025 candidate forum he separated himself from Betty Yee, Xavier Becerra, and Tony Thurmond on the question. He has called single-payer "snake oil."

The healthcare-sector donors to his 2026 committee are entities that benefit from continuation of the existing multi-payer system rather than its replacement:

The Fresenius contribution is the most analytically clean example. Fresenius is the world's largest dialysis provider; it has actively opposed proposals for stronger California healthcare regulation, including litigation that the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals decided April 7, 2026. Its donation to the only major Democrat opposing single-payer aligns with its regulatory interests against the policy that would most threaten its margins.

AltaMed is the largest federally qualified health center (FQHC) network in the United States. AltaMed receives substantial Medi-Cal managed-care reimbursement that depends on the existing public-private structure. Villaraigosa was a paid advisor to AltaMed after leaving the LA mayor's office in 2013 (LA Times November 24, 2017, tax-return reporting).

Mercury Public Affairs (November 2018 - October 2021): Villaraigosa joined Mercury as co-chairman after losing the 2018 governor primary; promoted to partner in July 2020. Actum LLC (January 2022 - August 2022): After leaving Mercury, Villaraigosa co-founded Actum LLC with Mercury co-founder Kirill Goncharenko, Barbara Boxer, and Fabian Nuñez. Each of these entities has regulatory interests before the state government Villaraigosa was advising.

Even Reed Hastings gave up

The single sharpest editorial fact in this beat is that the donor class that bankrolled Villaraigosa's 2018 governor IE has, in 2026, walked.

In 2018, Reed Hastings (then Netflix CEO) gave $7 million to the IE committee Families & Teachers for Antonio Villaraigosa for Governor 2018, Sponsored by the California Charter Schools Association Advocates. Eli Broad gave another $1.5 million. Per FPPC's June 2018 Primary Election top-contributors list, approximately $22 million flowed through California Charter Schools Association Advocates IE to support Villaraigosa in the 2018 cycle. KQED documented the spending pattern in its April 13, 2018 reporting.

In 2026, no equivalent CCSA-sponsored IE has materialized for Villaraigosa. The FPPC's February 12, 2026 top-contributors list for the June 2026 primary shows no Villaraigosa-supporting IE at the $1 million reporting threshold. Reed Hastings's 2026 California governor money instead went to Matt Mahan's IE committee, $1 million, per the same FPPC top-contributors disclosure.

The structural read: the institutional donor class that organized around Villaraigosa in 2018 has rationally moved to a different vehicle in 2026 (Mahan, the San Jose mayor with the Silicon Valley billionaire bloc behind him). That movement is not Villaraigosa's fault. It is the donor class allocating capital toward the candidate they expect to win, not the candidate they last invested in.

The 2018 institutional donor base walked. The regulated-industry donor base stayed because the alternatives (Steyer, Porter) are worse for their interests. That is a portrait of who Villaraigosa is in 2026: the candidate the institutional class moved off of, holding onto the regulated-industry money he had once promised to refuse.

The donor class is rationally allocating against its threats

Read the full 2026 California gubernatorial Democratic donor maps side by side and a pattern emerges. Steyer's commercial-Prop-13 reform threatens fossil-fuel margins (rentier capital, tax structure for commercial real-estate-heavy firms). The fossil-fuel coalition opposes Steyer (the Class Traitor beat documents this) and supports Villaraigosa. Single-payer healthcare threatens the multi-payer healthcare incumbents. The healthcare incumbent coalition opposes single-payer and donates to Villaraigosa, Becerra, and other major Democrats not running on single-payer.

Villaraigosa's 2026 platform centers on "affordability for working-class Californians": lower housing costs, lower energy and gas prices, better schools, and public safety. The structural contradiction is not labor versus business (he genuinely has building-trades union backing). The structural contradiction is that:

  • The candidate pitching gas-price relief is funded by the companies whose revenue depends on gas prices staying high under California's carbon-cap structure.
  • The candidate opposing single-payer healthcare (the policy with the largest documented potential to lower working-class healthcare costs) is funded by healthcare entities that profit from the multi-payer status quo.
  • The candidate selling himself as the anti-special-interest insurgent took $381,820 from a Newsom-curated nonprofit funded by California utilities, AT&T, and Disney.

The donor class is not making a moral judgment about Villaraigosa. It is rationally allocating capital toward the candidates whose platforms protect its margins. That allocation is the structural story of the 2026 California Democratic primary, not the personal story of any one candidate.

Open questions

Items in his public record that remain unresolved:

Whether the 2018 pledge had an explicit expiration. Was it a single-cycle commitment or a continuing one? Villaraigosa has not publicly addressed it either way.

His specific income from Mercury Public Affairs and Actum LLC. Villaraigosa stated publicly he would not register as a lobbyist personally, which is the disclosure mechanism that would have made the figures public. He has not voluntarily disclosed them otherwise.
