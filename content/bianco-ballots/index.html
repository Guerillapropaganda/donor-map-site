The 650,000-ballot seizure

In November 2025, California voters approved Proposition 50, redistricting the state's congressional map in response to the Trump administration's federal-redistricting push. The measure passed statewide with 64 percent support per California Secretary of State results.

In Riverside County, the sheriff's office under Chad Bianco took 650,000 ballots from that election under the authority of a search warrant. CalMatters reported on March 26, 2026 that the seizure was tied to an alleged election fraud investigation. CalMatters' April 21, 2026 investigation, drawn from emails obtained under public records request, documented that the investigation pathway had been suggested to Bianco in a September 2022 email from Steve Tuminello, a figure in the Constitutional Sheriffs and Peace Officers Association network. Tuminello addressed Bianco as a "Constitutional Sheriff" and urged him to pursue an election fraud investigation.

California Attorney General Rob Bonta filed legal challenges to the seizure. The Riverside County Board of Supervisors voted 4 to 1 to deny Bianco's request for county-funded outside counsel to defend the seizure. The deciding vote against him came from his own party's Board majority.

The judge he had endorsed

The warrant authorizing the seizure was signed by Judge Jay Kiel, whom Bianco had personally endorsed in Kiel's 2022 judicial election campaign. Kiel had publicly praised Bianco. The structural relationship between the sheriff who needed the warrant and the judge who issued it was not arm's length. CalMatters reported the relationship as part of its April 21, 2026 email investigation.

The structural model the Constitutional Sheriffs movement endorses is one where the county sheriff's authority supersedes federal and state law enforcement. The sheriff endorsing the judge who then issues warrants for the sheriff's investigations is that model running as designed.

Bianco's authority claim does not require any single bad actor in the warrant chain. It requires only that the political relationships among local-government actors operate as the Constitutional Sheriffs movement intends. Bianco endorsed Kiel in 2022. Kiel signed the 2025 warrant. The sheriff seized 650,000 ballots from a state election. None of the three steps requires anyone to have lied in any document.

The FPPC aggregation question

The largest single donor pair on Bianco's filings is Downs Energy and M & D Development, LLC, both based at a single Corona, California, mailbox-service address and both managed by the same siblings: Michael Downs (president and owner of Downs Energy; managing partner of M&D Development) and Sherry Messner (responsible agent and officer of M&D; listed as owner of Downs Energy).

Downs Energy gave Bianco $39,200 on July 7, 2024 for the June primary and another $39,200 on December 30, 2024 for the November general election. M&D Development gave two contributions of $39,200 each, both dated December 30, 2024. Combined: $156,800. Per Cal-Access RCPT_CD, those are the two largest single donor totals in the committee's filings.

Per FPPC rules, contributions from entities controlled by the same individuals must be aggregated and treated as a single donor's contributions. If aggregated, the combined $156,800 would exceed the maximum allowable from one source ($78,400 across both primary and general). The SF Chronicle reported the question on March 31, 2026; the FPPC spokesperson declined to comment on the specific donations. The Bianco campaign responded that the companies are independently operated and the donations comply with law. No formal FPPC enforcement action has been confirmed as of the May 2, 2026 research date.

The Constitutional Sheriffs network

The Constitutional Sheriffs and Peace Officers Association (CSPOA) is the main organizational vehicle for what is commonly called the "constitutional sheriff" movement: the doctrine that elected county sheriffs hold authority that supersedes federal and state law enforcement within their counties. The movement's leader, Richard Mack, also served on the Oath Keepers board. CSPOA's view of sheriff authority is the explicit framework for the kind of action Bianco took with the 650,000-ballot seizure: the sheriff's office acting independently of state-level election authority.

CalMatters' April 21, 2026 investigation documented that Bianco was approached in a September 2022 email by Steve Tuminello, a figure in the constitutional sheriff network, who urged the election-fraud investigation pathway and addressed Bianco directly as a "Constitutional Sheriff." Bianco had attended week-long sessions at the Claremont Institute promoting Trump conservatism and received that institute's "Sheriff of the Year" award.

The CSPOA pathway from Tuminello's 2022 email to the 2025 ballot seizure to the 2026 governor's race is, when read together, the operational arc of one structural model. The sheriff who claims county-supreme authority. The judicial ally who issues the warrant. The state attorney general who challenges it. The county board that refuses to fund his defense. The candidate who turns the model into a statewide bid.

The 2014 Oath Keepers prior

In 2014, four years before Bianco was first elected sheriff, he paid one year of membership dues to the Oath Keepers as a Riverside Sheriff's Department lieutenant. The membership was confirmed by two independent named publications using leaked Oath Keepers membership data:

  • LAist, October 5, 2021, reporter Frank Stoltze. Source: a data breach of Epik (a web host serving right-wing groups) executed by the Anonymous hacker group on September 13, 2021. Bianco's name appeared on the Oath Keepers membership list extracted from the leak.
  • NPR, October 6, 2021, reporter Scott Neuman. Source: Distributed Denial of Secrets (DDoSecrets), a transparency collective. J.J. MacNab of the George Washington University Program on Extremism drew attention to the leak.

Bianco's own response, via LAist and NPR, was that he joined approximately February 2014 as a lieutenant before becoming sheriff, paid one year's dues, does not remember actively participating, and does not even remember joining. He has defended the Oath Keepers as "not anti-government" and blamed January 6 on "a few fringe people." He has not denied the membership.

Brian Levin of California State San Bernardino's Center for the Study of Hate and Extremism noted in NPR's coverage that in 2014 the Oath Keepers were "less radical, less confrontational" than at the time of January 6. The historical context matters. The 2014 Oath Keepers membership is not the central editorial claim of this beat. It is the historical context for the structural pattern that runs from CSPOA in 2022 through the ballot seizure in 2025 to the gubernatorial campaign in 2026.

Why this is structural, not personal

Every elected sheriff has donors. Most sheriffs serve their donor class quietly, on jail contracts, vendor relationships, and policing priorities, until the donor class is satisfied and the sheriff is re-elected. The system runs smoothly when the local enforcement model serves the local economic interests funding it.

The system breaks down when a sheriff turns the local model into a national-political statement. Bianco's 650,000-ballot seizure was not local enforcement of local crime. It was a county sheriff's intervention in a statewide election under a doctrine (constitutional-sheriff supremacy over state authority) that explicitly contests the structural framework California's election system runs on. The same enforcement architecture that funds his sheriff's office (developer money, county contractor money, law-enforcement union money) is now funding his gubernatorial campaign.

The donor class behind a Republican county sheriff running for higher office is structurally identical, in shape, to the donor class behind a Democratic billionaire running for that same office: a coalition of regulated industries whose business operates in the political space the candidate would influence. Different party. Different geography. Same model.

The polling has him at 12 to 14 percent. The CNN debate qualified him on $1 million raised plus 3 percent in two California gubernatorial polls by April 27. He cleared the threshold. The system that put him on that stage is the system this beat documents.
