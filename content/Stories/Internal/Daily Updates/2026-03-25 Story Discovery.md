Elect Chicago Women and Affordable Chicago Now, that collectively spent over $19.7 million across four Illinois Democratic congressional primaries (IL-2, IL-7, IL-8, IL-9) on March 18, 2026.

### [GOLD] FTC-CVS Caremark Insulin Settlement. PBM Price Manipulation Quantified at $7 Billion

**Rating: Gold**. A federal enforcement action documenting corporate price manipulation of a lifesaving drug with specific dollar figures. Connects to the vault's pharma/healthcare donor tracking and raises questions about PBM lobbying influence on drug pricing policy.

The FTC reached a proposed settlement on March 24 with CVS Caremark, one of the three largest pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) in the United States, over charges that its PBM subsidiary artificially inflated insulin prices and impeded access to affordable insulin. The FTC estimates the settlement will save Americans up to $7 billion in out-of-pocket costs over 10 years.

**The PBM price manipulation mechanism:** PBMs negotiate drug prices between manufacturers and pharmacies/insurers. CVS Caremark allegedly used its market position to steer patients toward higher-priced insulin formulations where Caremark earned larger rebates, even when cheaper alternatives were available. The effect: patients paid more at the pharmacy counter so CVS could collect larger rebates from manufacturers. The FTC called this "manipulating insulin prices" and "impeding access."

**The enforcement pattern:** This is the second PBM settlement in two months. Cigna's Express Scripts reached a final settlement with the FTC in February 2026 on identical charges. UnitedHealth's OptumRx, the third major PBM, remains in litigation. Together, CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx control approximately 80% of the PBM market. All three face the same FTC complaint filed September 2024.

**Donor-class read:** CVS Health's PAC contributed $1.4M in the 2024 cycle across both parties. UnitedHealth Group's PAC contributed $3.2M. The PBM industry spent $35.9M on federal lobbying in 2024. These companies have been lobbying against PBM transparency legislation for years, the same practices the FTC is now penalizing through enforcement. The question for the vault: which members of Congress received PBM money while blocking PBM reform legislation? This is a research lead, not yet a mapped connection.

**Vault connections:** Updates [[Eli Lilly]] (major insulin manufacturer whose pricing is downstream of PBM decisions) and the broader pharma/healthcare donor sector. The PBM industry itself. CVS Health, UnitedHealth Group, Cigna, is not yet tracked as a distinct donor cluster in the vault. This is a gap.

> [!money] $7 billion in patient overcharges from a single PBM
> The FTC's $7B estimate represents documented overcharges from CVS Caremark's insulin pricing practices alone. If similar patterns hold for Express Scripts and OptumRx, which control a combined ~50% additional market share, the total patient overcharge across all three PBMs could exceed $15-20 billion over a decade. These are not abstract market inefficiencies, they are quantified harm to diabetic patients, enforced by market concentration in an industry that lobbies Congress to preserve that concentration.

### [SILVER] MFN Drug Pricing: Trump Bypasses Congress With Tariff Leverage. Pharma Capitulates Temporarily

**Rating: Silver**. Documents an unusual donor-to-policy dynamic: a president using tariff threats to extract concessions from an industry that heavily lobbies Congress to block the same policy legislatively. The pharma lobby's response reveals the limits of legislative capture when executive power is applied directly.

Trump has now secured Most-Favored-Nation (MFN) drug pricing agreements with 16 of 17 targeted pharmaceutical manufacturers. The mechanism: companies that align their US prices with the lowest prices they charge in comparable countries receive a 3-year suspension of pharmaceutical tariffs. Companies that refuse face tariffs.

**The latest round (announced late February/March):** Amgen, Boehringer Ingelheim, Bristol Myers Squibb, Genentech (Roche), Gilead Sciences, GSK, Merck, Novartis, and Sanofi all signed. This represents a near-total capitulation by the industry.

**The Congressional disconnect:** Despite Trump's MFN deals, Congress has given the approach a lukewarm reception. STAT News reports that MFN pricing divides Trump and Congress in health care reconciliation talks. Congressional Republicans are reluctant to codify MFN in legislation because doing so would permanently threaten pharma's pricing power. The industry's $263M annual lobbying budget is directed at Congress, not the White House. Trump has bypassed that firewall entirely by using tariff authority that doesn't require legislation.

**The structural contradiction:** Pharma spent $263M lobbying Congress to block legislative drug pricing reform. Trump achieved a version of the same outcome through executive tariff power, no legislation needed. The industry's Congressional capture is intact, but irrelevant. This exposes a limitation of the standard donor-to-policy model: when executive authority is broad enough, the legislative lobbying investment can be circumvented.

**Vault connections:** Updates [[Eli Lilly]], [[Pfizer]], and the broader Pharma & Healthcare sector. The 3-year deal terms mean pharma is accepting short-term price reductions while likely lobbying for the tariff framework to expire or be weakened after 2028. Track whether pharma PAC spending shifts toward 2028 presidential candidates who might reverse MFN.

### [SILVER] DISCLOSE Act of 2026, 182 Cosponsors, Zero Chance of Passage

**Rating: Silver**. Legislative marker, not legislative action. Documents which politicians are publicly aligned against dark money while the system they're criticizing continues operating. Useful for tracking the contradiction between stated positions and structural inaction.

Senator Sheldon Whitehouse and Rep. Chris Pappas led 182 colleagues in reintroducing the DISCLOSE Act, which would require super PACs, 501(c)(4) groups, and corporations spending over $10,000 in elections to disclose donors who contribute more than $10,000.

**New provisions in the 2026 version:** The updated bill now covers payments to social media influencers to promote or oppose candidates, a significant expansion acknowledging the growing role of paid influencer campaigns in elections. It also narrows harassment exemptions to prevent abuse of the exemption process.

**Support:** All 47 senators who caucus with Democrats. 139 House Democrats. Zero Republicans. The bill has zero chance of passage in a Republican-controlled Congress.

**The structural irony:** The DISCLOSE Act is reintroduced every Congress. It has never passed. Its function is to create a public record of which party supports donor transparency and which does not, but it does nothing to stop the dark money it decries. Meanwhile, the Democratic senators who cosponsor the DISCLOSE Act continue to benefit from dark money operations on their own side (see: AIPAC's Illinois shell PACs, above; DNC-aligned 501(c)(4)s). This is the **Dark Money Symmetry** pattern: both parties run dark money operations while one party publicly opposes them.
