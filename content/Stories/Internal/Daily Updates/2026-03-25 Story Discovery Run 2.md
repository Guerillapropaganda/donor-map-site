### [GOLD] Wall Street's $600 Billion Windfall — The "One Big Beautiful Bill" Guts the CFPB

**Rating: Gold** — The OBBBA's CFPB funding cut is documented in CRS reports. The Big Six banks' $600B market value gain is documented in financial press. The vault's existing [[JPMorgan Chase]] profile (147 lines, recently expanded) already documents JPMorgan's 70% revolving-door lobbying operation and Both-Sides Illusion contribution pattern. This story connects those pieces: the specific legislative mechanism that delivered the donor class's policy priority.

The "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (OBBBA), signed approximately eight months ago, included Section 30001 which slashed the CFPB's funding cap from 12% to 6.5% of Federal Reserve earnings — cutting the agency's budget from ~$755M (FY24 obligations) to a cap of $249M. By January 2026, the Bureau implemented sweeping reductions in force, leaving investigative arms dormant. Total financial regulatory enforcement actions have dropped ~37% compared to 2024 levels. The CFPB has granted early termination of consent orders involving Apple, U.S. Bank, and Regions Bank. In eight months, the agency has let more than 40 lawbreakers off the hook.

**The beneficiaries:** The Big Six — [[JPMorgan Chase]], [[Bank of America]], [[Citigroup]], [[Goldman Sachs - Wall Street Titan|Goldman Sachs]], Wells Fargo, and Morgan Stanley — have collectively added approximately $600 billion to their combined market value since the OBBBA was signed, reaching $2.37 trillion by March 2026.

**Rating: Silver** — Useful update to vault's healthcare donor tracking. State-level pushback is accelerating while federal legislation stalls — a classic Genuine Win + Structural Limit pattern. Connects to [[Blackstone Group]], [[Apollo Global Management]], [[Carlyle Group]] profiles.

Private equity healthcare acquisitions continued through January 2026 despite growing scrutiny. Meanwhile, the Health Over Wealth Act (introduced by Markey/Jayapal in 2024) requiring PE-owned facilities to disclose debt, executive pay, lobbying spending, and service reductions has not advanced in Congress. Seven states (Hawaii, Indiana, New York, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia) have proposed new restrictions in 2026, joining California and Oregon which enacted laws in 2025.

**The structural pattern:** Federal legislation that would require PE disclosure of lobbying and political spending cannot pass. State-level regulation is expanding but fragmented and varies in strength. PE firms continue acquisitions in the gaps between state jurisdictions. The Health Over Wealth Act's requirement to disclose "lobbying and political spending" is precisely the provision that PE lobbying is designed to block — the legislation would expose the mechanism that prevents its own passage.
