### 4. Fifth Circuit Kills FTC Merger Filing Overhaul. US Chamber Victory

On March 19, the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals denied the FTC's motion to stay a district court ruling that struck down the agency's overhauled Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) merger filing requirements, the first major update to premerger notification rules in nearly 50 years. The ruling is now effective immediately, reverting to the old, less burdensome filing form.

The district court found two independent grounds for invalidation: (1) the new rules exceeded the FTC's statutory authority under the HSR Act, and (2) the rule was "arbitrary and capricious" because the FTC failed to adequately weigh costs against benefits. The plaintiff was the US Chamber of Commerce, joined by other business groups.

> [!money] Who benefits from weaker merger oversight
> The old HSR form requires significantly less information from merging parties, making it harder for the FTC to identify anticompetitive deals before they close. This benefits every major corporate donor pursuing acquisitions, including vault-tracked entities across tech ([[Amazon]], [[Palantir Technologies|Palantir]]), healthcare ([[UnitedHealth Group - Optum]]), defense ([[Lockheed Martin]], [[Northrop Grumman]]), and Wall Street ([[Goldman Sachs]]). The US Chamber of Commerce, which brought the suit, is funded by virtually every major corporate donor in the vault. This ruling, combined with DOGE-driven FTC staff reductions, creates a permissive environment for corporate consolidation.

> [!contradiction] "Pro-competition" administration guts competition enforcement
> The Trump administration's stated support for competition and innovation is contradicted by its tacit acceptance of court rulings that weaken the FTC's enforcement toolkit. FTC Chair Andrew Ferguson has not aggressively defended the merger rules. Meanwhile, the same administration that claims to fight corporate monopolies appointed SEC and CFTC chairs who have systematically rolled back financial regulation for their donor constituencies.
