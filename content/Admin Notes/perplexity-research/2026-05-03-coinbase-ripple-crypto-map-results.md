# Coinbase Global + Ripple: California Regulatory Exposure and CA Gov 2026 Crypto Donor Map

## Executive summary

California’s concrete 2025-2026 crypto regulatory exposure is the Digital Financial Assets Law implementation schedule, not a newly located DFPI enforcement action against Coinbase or Ripple. DFPI says certain crypto companies serving Californians must hold a DFAL license or have submitted a completed application by July 1, 2026, and DFPI began accepting online license applications on March 9, 2026 ([DFPI](https://dfpi.ca.gov/regulated-industries/digital-financial-assets/), [DFPI April 2026 bulletin](https://dfpi.ca.gov/news/monthly-bulletins/monthly-bulletin-april-2026/)). Ripple is directly visible in that regulatory process through a 2025 comment letter on DFPI’s DFAL rulemaking, while the directly located DFPI action against Coinbase was a June 6, 2023 staking-rewards action outside the requested 2024-2026 enforcement window ([DFPI Ripple comment letter](https://dfpi.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Ripple-Comment-to-DFPI_Redacted.pdf), [DFPI Coinbase action](https://dfpi.ca.gov/press_release/dfpi-issues-action-against-coinbase-citing-staking-rewards-program-violates-securities-law/)).

The CA Gov 2026 crypto-money map has four verified nodes in the May 3, 2026 Cal-Access export: Chris/Christian Larsen to Katie Porter and Steve Hilton, Brian Armstrong to the pro-Mahan IE, Elena Nadolinski of Coinbase to Mahan’s candidate committee, and a small non-Ripple-Labs “ripple Capital partners” donor to Hilton. No matching Coinbase/Ripple/Fairshake/Stand With Crypto state contribution record was located for Becerra, Steyer, Bianco, Villaraigosa, or Thurmond in the targeted gubernatorial committee set reviewed ([Cal-Access bulk export](https://campaignfinance.cdn.sos.ca.gov/dbwebexport.zip)). Antonio Villaraigosa’s Coinbase exposure is advisory-income/role exposure rather than a located Coinbase donation: the LA Times reported he joined Coinbase’s global advisory council as a paid adviser in 2024 and later reported campaign-filing disclosures that he had advised Coinbase Global before the governor campaign ([LA Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2024-04-30/antonio-villaraigosa-coinbase-cryptocurrency-adviser), [LA Times](https://www.latimes.com/california/story/2026-03-16/villaraigosas-dreams-for-political-comeback-meet-reality-again)).

## Scope and source rule

This report uses Tier 1 sources for primary government, court, SEC, DFPI, FEC, California Legislative Information, and Cal-Access records. It uses Tier 2 named-publication political and finance press where primary records did not provide narrative context. Aggregators, crypto-industry promotional outlets, social media, blogs, partisan outlets, and unsourced claims are excluded.

## Bottom line

For California regulatory exposure, the clearest mechanism is DFAL licensing and rulemaking, with Ripple participating in the DFPI rulemaking record and Coinbase facing a prior 2023 DFPI staking action outside the requested window. For CA Gov 2026 money, the most material verified crypto-related contribution is Brian Armstrong’s $500,000 to the pro-Mahan IE, followed by Chris/Christian Larsen’s Porter-Hilton hedge and Coinbase employee-level money to Mahan; Villaraigosa’s exposure is not a located donation but his paid Coinbase advisory role reported by the LA Times. Under the requested source rules, no primary-source evidence was located that Coinbase PAC, Fairshake, Stand With Crypto, or Ripple Labs as entities are spending directly in the 2026 California governor race.
