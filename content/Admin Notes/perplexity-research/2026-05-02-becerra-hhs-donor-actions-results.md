# HHS Donor Actions Audit: Becerra Tenure (March 19, 2021 – January 20, 2025)

**Audit Scope**: Federal Register notices, CMS rule announcements, HRSA award records, HHS-OIG enforcement disclosures, DOJ press releases, and contemporaneous trade reporting. Covers HHS regulatory, enforcement, and contracting actions taken during Xavier Becerra's tenure as Secretary that may have materially benefited entities later identified as maximum/major donors to his 2026 California gubernatorial campaign.

**Analytical Test Applied**: (1) Did the HHS Secretary's office hold decision authority over the relevant action? (2) Was the action favorable to the entity's stated business interests beyond routine baseline treatment? (3) Is there a temporal or material nexus between the action and the entity?

**Campaign Finance Baseline**: The Ballot Book's donor registry for "Becerra for Governor 2026" ([theballotbook.com](https://theballotbook.com/campaign_finance/committees/19177/receipts?page=1&tab=by_donor)) confirms the following entities among top donors (by percentage of total receipts as of May 2026, total raised ≈ $5.74 million):

| Donor Name as Filed | % of Total | Affiliation |
|---|---|---|
| Altamed Health Network Inc. | 0.7% | AltaMed Health Services |
| Alonzo Cantu (Developer, Cantu Construction) | 0.7% | Founder/Board, DHR Health |
| Leonard Schaeffer (Retired) | 0.7% | Founding Chairman/CEO, WellPoint (now Elevance/Anthem) |
| Raul Ayala (Not Employed) | 0.7% | Ambulatory Medical Officer, Adventist Health Central California |
| Kelly Ayala (Physician, The Permanente Medical Group) | 0.6% | Permanente Medical Group / Kaiser Permanente |
| Border Health Federal PAC | 0.7% | PAC associated with DHR Health/Cantu network |

No direct contribution from Prime Healthcare Services, Dr. Prem Reddy, Kaiser Permanente the institution, or Anthem/Elevance Health the corporation was identified in the available campaign finance data. [UNVERIFIED whether Prime Healthcare or its executives contributed through other vehicles not yet itemized.]

## Executive Summary

Six entity clusters were examined. Two present **material, entity-specific HHS actions with verified donor status**: (1) DHR Health/Alonzo Cantu, where CMS under Becerra approved an unprecedented physician-owned hospital expansion exception in December 2022, and (2) AltaMed Health Services, which received continued HRSA Section 330 and Teaching Health Center federal awards during the period. Two present **sector-wide favorable actions with no entity-specific HHS decision**: Anthem/Leonard Schaeffer and Kaiser/Kelly Ayala, whose interests were served by industry-wide Medicare Advantage rate increases and telehealth policy, but no entity-specific regulatory relief is documented. One—Prime Healthcare—presents **an enforcement action against the entity (not favorable)** that concluded with a CIA executed during Becerra's tenure. One—Adventist Health/Raul Ayala—has **limited but real HRSA grant receipts** tied to specific named programs, though the amounts are routine competitive awards.

The most legally significant finding is the DHR Health CMS expansion approval: it was entity-specific, contested, made over major hospital industry opposition, and the entity's principal is a named maximum donor.

## 1. Anthem / Leonard Schaeffer

### Donor Status
Leonard Schaeffer, founding Chairman and CEO of WellPoint (now Elevance Health/Anthem), is confirmed as a maximum donor (0.7% of total receipts, ~$40,250 at max individual contribution) to Becerra for Governor 2026 ([theballotbook.com](https://theballotbook.com/campaign_finance/committees/19177/receipts?page=1&tab=by_donor)). Schaeffer currently holds the Judge Robert Maclay Widney Chair at USC and is a senior adviser; he is no longer an Elevance officer ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/people/leonard-d-schaeffer/)). No institutional contribution from Anthem or Elevance Health is identified.

### HHS Actions Under Becerra

**Medicare Advantage Rates (CY 2022–2024)**

CMS finalized Medicare Advantage (MA) rate announcements for plan years 2022, 2023, and 2024 during Becerra's tenure. The CY 2022 final rate, published January 15, 2021 (days before Becerra's confirmation), set a 4.08% average payment increase ([CMS](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/2022-medicare-advantage-part-d-rate-announcement-fact-sheet)). The CY 2023 announcement, published April 4, 2022, maintained a stable effective growth rate of 4.88% ([CMS](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/2023-medicare-advantage-part-d-rate-announcement)). Most consequentially, the CY 2024 final rate—published March 31, 2023—increased average MA payments by **3.32%**, up significantly from the 1.03% increase proposed in the Advance Notice following proposed risk adjustment methodology changes ([CMS](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/fact-sheet-2024-medicare-advantage-part-d-rate-announcement)). Critics including the Center for Medicare Advocacy characterized this as the result of "a well-funded and high pressure insurance industry lobbying campaign" that successfully diluted proposed risk-adjustment reforms ([Medicare Advocacy](https://medicareadvocacy.org/final-ma-payment-rates-for-2024/)).

Anthem/Elevance is one of the nation's largest MA insurers. Elevated MA payment rates directly benefit its revenue. The 3.32% CY 2024 increase translated to approximately $13.8 billion in additional industry-wide payments ([TechTarget](https://www.techtarget.com/healthcarepayers/news/366603612/HHS-Finalizes-332-Increase-in-2024-Medicare-Advantage-Payments)).

**OIG Enforcement of Anthem**

During Becerra's tenure, the HHS-OIG published a Medicare Advantage compliance audit (May 2021) of Anthem Community Insurance Company (Contract H3655) finding that Anthem received at least $3.47 million in net overpayments from unsupported diagnosis codes and recommending repayment ([HHS-OIG](https://oig.hhs.gov/reports/all/2021/medicare-advantage-compliance-audit-of-specific-diagnosis-codes-that-anthem-community-insurance-company-inc-contract-h3655-submitted-to-cms/)). This was an adverse finding against Anthem, not a favorable action. No parallel favorable enforcement exemption or favorable OIG ruling for Anthem/Elevance specifically was identified. [NEGATIVE FINDING on entity-specific OIG enforcement.]

**ACA Marketplace**

The American Rescue Plan (March 2021) enhanced marketplace premium tax credits, and Becerra oversaw the implementation of a Special Enrollment Period that enrolled 1.5 million Americans between February and June 2021 ([CMS](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/2021-marketplace-special-enrollment-period-report-3)). Anthem/Elevance (branded as Wellpoint in some states) participates in ACA marketplace products and benefited from the enrollment surge and enhanced subsidies. However, this was a statutory benefit under the ARP, not a Secretary-level regulatory preference for Anthem specifically.

**Star Ratings Litigation**

Elevance Health filed suit against Becerra (as defendant in his official capacity) in December 2023 over alleged miscalculation of 2024 MA Star Ratings ([Georgetown Litigation Tracker](https://litigationtracker.law.georgetown.edu/litigation/elevance-health-inc-et-al-v-becerra-et-al/)). This is an adverse, not favorable, HHS action toward Anthem/Elevance.

### Materiality Test Result

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Secretary decision authority | Yes — CMS rate-setting is HHS Secretary delegated authority |
| Entity-specific favorable action | **NO** — MA rates are industry-wide; OIG audit was adverse |
| Material beyond baseline | **NO** — Rate increases are annual; CY 2024 increase followed industry lobbying, not individual preference |
| Donor connection temporal | Yes — Schaeffer donated to 2026 campaign after Becerra left office |

**Finding**: The CY 2024 MA rate increase (1.03% proposed → 3.32% final) benefited Anthem/Elevance alongside all MA insurers, but no entity-specific favorable HHS action for Anthem is documented. The OIG issued an adverse audit against Anthem during the period. **No material entity-specific nexus identified. [NEGATIVE FINDING on preferential treatment.]**

## 3. Prime Healthcare Services

### Donor Status
No direct contribution from Prime Healthcare Services, Prime Healthcare Foundation, or Dr. Prem Reddy to Becerra for Governor 2026 was found in the itemized donor records reviewed. [UNVERIFIED: California FPPC filings have a higher itemization threshold ($100+); contributions below threshold would not appear. Small contributions from Prime-affiliated individuals may exist.] The task brief characterizes Prime as a current major/maximum donor; this **could not be verified** from available campaign finance data as of May 2026.

### HHS Actions Under Becerra

**HHS-OIG Corporate Integrity Agreement #2 — Effective June 2021**

Prime Healthcare's first CIA (CIA #1) was executed August 3, 2018 following a $65 million FCA settlement for false claims at 14 California hospitals, and closed December 3, 2021 ([HHS-OIG](https://oig.hhs.gov/compliance/corporate-integrity-agreements/browse-cias/prime-healthcare-services-inc-reddy-prem-md/)). A **second CIA (CIA #2)** was executed **June 9, 2021** — during Becerra's tenure — following Prime's $37.5 million settlement with DOJ and the California AG for FCA and Anti-Kickback Statute violations ([HHS-OIG CIA listing](https://oig.hhs.gov/compliance/corporate-integrity-agreements/browse-cias/prime-healthcare-services-inc/); [DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/prime-healthcare-services-and-two-doctors-agree-pay-375-million-settle-allegations-kickbacks)). The CIA #2 has an **estimated completion date of June 2026**, placing its entire active period within and immediately after Becerra's tenure.

**CIA #2 Conduct Covered**

The FCA violations resolved by the July 2021 DOJ settlement and associated CIA included: (1) kickbacks paid by Prime when it overpaid to acquire Dr. Arunasalam's physician practice to induce referrals; (2) billing for procedures performed by a suspended physician; and (3) false claims for implantable medical hardware ([DOJ](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/prime-healthcare-services-and-two-doctors-agree-pay-375-million-settle-allegations-kickbacks)).

**Nature of CIA #2 — Favorable or Adverse?**

A CIA is the mechanism by which OIG agrees **not to exercise permissive exclusion authority** (i.e., not to exclude the provider from Medicare/Medicaid) in exchange for compliance program obligations. The CIA is therefore simultaneously an enforcement outcome (adverse — Prime paid $37.5 million and accepted monitoring) and a favorable outcome relative to the alternative (exclusion from federal programs would have been catastrophic for a hospital system). The OIG's willingness to execute a CIA rather than pursue exclusion is a **discretionary favorable decision by the HHS-OIG** under the Secretary's umbrella.

**Modifications, Escalations, or Favorable Interpretations During CIA #2**

No documented CIA #2 modifications, escalations for Prime violations, or favorable interpretive guidance to Prime were identified in publicly available OIG records during Becerra's tenure. CIA reporting obligations (annual reports, independent review organization audits) were standard. [UNVERIFIED: Internal OIG correspondence on CIA compliance status is not publicly disclosed.]

**Subsequent FCA Action (2026)**

WalkerNash AI's case tracker records a new Prime Healthcare/Prem Reddy FCA penalty of $1.3 million in Pennsylvania in 2026 ([WalkerNash](https://walkernash.ai/cases/2026-prime-healthcare-services-and-ceo-dr-prem-reddy)), after Becerra's departure.

### Materiality Test Result

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Secretary decision authority | Yes — OIG operates under HHS Secretary; CIA execution requires OIG concurrence |
| Entity-specific action | Yes — CIA #2 is entity-specific; choice of CIA over exclusion is discretionary |
| Favorable beyond baseline | **AMBIGUOUS** — CIA is standard enforcement resolution; but it protected Prime from exclusion |
| Donor connection | **[UNVERIFIED]** — No confirmed contribution from Prime/Reddy identified |

**Finding**: CIA #2 was executed at the start of Becerra's tenure and is an adverse enforcement action (Prime paid $37.5M), though one that preserved Prime's Medicare/Medicaid billing privileges through the non-exclusion component. The non-exclusion decision is discretionary at OIG. No CIA modifications or favorable deviations from standard compliance processes were identified. **Donor status [UNVERIFIED]; enforcement action adverse to Prime, though non-exclusion is materially favorable relative to worst case.**

## 4. DHR Health / Alonzo Cantu

### Donor Status
Alonzo Cantu, identified on campaign finance records as "Developer at Cantu Construction," is confirmed as a maximum donor (0.7% of total receipts, approximately $40,250) to Becerra for Governor 2026 ([theballotbook.com](https://theballotbook.com/campaign_finance/committees/19177/receipts?page=1&tab=by_donor)). Cantu is the founder and a Board of Managers member of DHR Health (Doctors Hospital at Renaissance, Ltd.), one of the nation's largest physician-owned hospital systems, headquartered in Edinburg, Texas. The **Border Health Federal PAC** (also 0.7% of Becerra receipts) is directly associated with DHR Health/Cantu ([Edinburg Politics](https://edinburgpolitics.com/2021/02/10/dhrhealth-border-health-pac-south-texas-better/); [Transparency USA](https://www.transparencyusa.org/tx/committee/border-health-pac-55547-mpac)), making the combined DHR/Cantu contribution cluster approximately 1.4% of total receipts.

### HHS Actions Under Becerra

**CMS Section 6001 / ACA Physician-Owned Hospital Expansion Exception — December 2022**

This is the most directly material HHS action in the entire audit. Under ACA Section 6001 (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn), physician-owned hospitals (POHs) that received Medicare reimbursement before March 23, 2010 were grandfathered, but prohibited from expanding their facility capacity. A narrow "high Medicaid facility" exception allows CMS to approve expansion for hospitals meeting certain indigent-care thresholds.

DHR Health filed an amended expansion exception request on October 14, 2021, seeking CMS approval to operate a **new remote hospital campus in Brownsville, Texas** — 55 miles from its Edinburg main campus — under this exception ([CMS Filing](https://www.cms.gov/files/document/2021-dhr-expansion-exception-request.pdf)).

The Federation of American Hospitals (FAH) and the American Hospital Association (AHA) filed a joint comment letter on March 11, 2022 urging CMS to **reject** the application, arguing it exploited a regulatory loophole and that the 55-mile separation meant the "remote location" was effectively a new hospital, not an expansion ([FAH](https://fah.org/fah-aha-oppose-physician-owned-hospital-expansion-request/)).

**CMS approved the expansion on December 20, 2022**, publishing the notice in the Federal Register ([Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/12/20/2022-27566/medicare-program-approval-of-request-for-an-exception-to-the-prohibition-on-expansion-of-facility)). FAH called the decision "extremely disappointing," stating it "weaken[ed] the law banning new physician-owned hospitals" and exposed a loophole allowing a POH to establish effectively a new facility 55 miles away ([FAH reaction](https://fah.org/sets-an-unfortunate-precedent-fah-reacts-to-cms-decision-allowing-a-physician-owned-hospital-to-expand/)). DHR subsequently opened its Brownsville hospital in March 2023 with 59 beds and has since announced further expansions ([myRGV](https://myrgv.com/featured/2025/12/04/dhr-brownsville-expansion-project-to-add-more-spaces-services/)).

The CMS decision was made under the authority delegated from the HHS Secretary. Secretary Becerra was in office on December 20, 2022.

**AKS Case 4:11-cv-02565 — Dismissed February 25, 2022**

A 2011 qui tam False Claims Act / Anti-Kickback Statute complaint (Case 4:11-cv-02565, S.D. Tex.) named Doctors Hospital at Renaissance, Ltd., Alonzo Cantu personally, Lone Star National Bank, and others. The relators alleged that Cantu used bank loans as leverage over DHR physicians to force patient referrals. On **February 25, 2022**, U.S. District Judge Andrew Hanen granted defendants' motions to dismiss, dismissing FCA Counts I, II, and III against Cantu, Lone Star National Bank, and related entities ([GovInfo/USCOURTS](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-txsd-4_11-cv-02565/pdf/USCOURTS-txsd-4_11-cv-02565-0.pdf)).

The **United States did not intervene** in this qui tam case (it is styled as a relator suit with the government as the real party in interest). HHS/DOJ's decision not to intervene—made prior to Becerra's tenure or during it—was effectively favorable to DHR/Cantu by declining to pursue the case with government resources. The dismissal was a court ruling on motions, not a government decision per se, though non-intervention was an executive choice. [UNVERIFIED: The specific date the DOJ/HHS formally declined intervention and whether any declination decision was made during Becerra's tenure.]

### Materiality Test Result

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Secretary decision authority | Yes — CMS expansion exception decisions are Secretary-delegated authority |
| Entity-specific favorable action | **YES** — DHR Health named specifically in Federal Register approval notice |
| Material beyond baseline | **YES** — FAH/AHA described it as setting "unfortunate precedent"; over major opposition |
| Donor connection | **CONFIRMED** — Cantu (0.7%) + Border Health PAC (0.7%) = ~1.4% of total receipts |

**Finding**: The December 20, 2022 CMS approval of DHR Health's physician-owned hospital expansion exception is the **most clearly material, entity-specific HHS action in this audit**. It was made over the express opposition of the FAH and AHA, was characterized by industry critics as unprecedented regulatory overreach, and directly enabled DHR to open a new hospital campus. The entity's principal (Cantu) and the associated Border Health Federal PAC are confirmed maximum donors. The temporal sequence (approval Dec. 2022 → campaign contributions to a candidate who left office Jan. 2025) does not demonstrate advance quid pro quo, but the relationship is material and warrants scrutiny. **High materiality; entity-specific; Secretary-level decision authority confirmed.**

## 6. Kaiser Permanente / Permanente Medical Group / Kelly Ayala

### Donor Status
Kelly Ayala, identified as "Physician at The Permanente Medical Group," is confirmed as a donor (0.6% of total receipts, approximately $34,500) to Becerra for Governor 2026 ([theballotbook.com](https://theballotbook.com/campaign_finance/committees/19177/receipts?page=1&tab=by_donor)). No institutional contribution from Kaiser Permanente, the Permanente Medical Group, or Kaiser Foundation Health Plan was identified.

### HHS Actions Under Becerra

**Medicare Advantage Rates**

Kaiser Permanente is the nation's largest integrated MA plan. As discussed under the Anthem section, CMS finalized MA rate increases for CY 2022 (4.08%), CY 2023 (~8.5% total revenue change), and CY 2024 (3.32%) during Becerra's tenure ([CMS 2022](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/2022-medicare-advantage-part-d-rate-announcement-fact-sheet); [CMS 2023](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/2023-medicare-advantage-part-d-rate-announcement); [CMS 2024](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/fact-sheet-2024-medicare-advantage-part-d-rate-announcement)). These increases applied industry-wide. Kaiser publishes its own MA plan rate cards for employer groups (e.g., the San Francisco Health Service System reviewed a 6.19% Kaiser Senior Advantage premium increase for 2024 retirees ([SFHSS](https://sfhss.org/sites/default/files/2023-06/June%208,%202023%20KP%20Sr%20Advantage%20(CA)%20Fully%20Insured%20Medicare%20Retiree%202024%20Rates%20Contributions.pdf))), but these are plan-level rate setting, not HHS regulatory preference.

**CalAIM / Medi-Cal Managed Care**

CMS approved California's CalAIM (California Advancing and Innovating Medi-Cal) initiative on **December 28–29, 2021**, early in Becerra's tenure, extending California's Section 1115 Medicaid demonstration and Section 1915(b) managed care waiver for five years ([CMS](https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/press-releases/cms-announces-extensions-calaim-support-greater-health-equity-across-communities); [Athene Law](https://www.athenelaw.com/cms-authorizes-the-implementation-of-calaim-through-approvals-of-medicaid-waivers-to-california/)). CalAIM expanded Community Supports and Enhanced Care Management under Medi-Cal managed care. Kaiser Permanente operates one of the largest Medi-Cal managed care plans in California and expanded its Medi-Cal enrollment substantially under CalAIM's implementation. CalAIM was designed and submitted by California DHCS to CMS, and approval was made through the standard Medicaid waiver process. CMS also approved the CY 2022 Medi-Cal managed care capitation rates in February 2022 ([California Hospital Association](https://calhospital.org/cms-approves-calendar-year-2022-medi-cal-managed-care-rates/)).

**COVID-19 Public Health Emergency and Telehealth Flexibilities**

Secretary Becerra repeatedly renewed the COVID-19 Public Health Emergency (PHE) declaration throughout his tenure, with the last renewal on January 11, 2023 extending through April 11, 2023, before the PHE was finally allowed to expire on May 11, 2023 ([Baker Donelson](https://www.bakerdonelson.com/HHS-extends-Public-Health-Emergency-Declaration-Act); [RCPA](https://paproviders.org/federal-public-health-emergency-extended-to-january-2023/)). PHE renewals maintained Medicare telehealth flexibilities that significantly expanded covered services, eliminated geographic restrictions, and allowed audio-only telehealth. Kaiser Permanente is one of the nation's leading telehealth providers and repeatedly advocated for permanent telehealth flexibilities ([Kaiser Permanente](https://about.kaiserpermanente.org/news/medicare-telehealth-flexibilities-should-be-here-to-stay)). The PHE renewals directly preserved Kaiser's high-volume telehealth business model. However, PHE renewal is a Secretary-level epidemiological determination, not a Kaiser-specific regulatory decision.

**Enforcement**

No HHS-OIG enforcement actions specifically targeting Kaiser Permanente during Becerra's tenure were identified.

### Materiality Test Result

| Factor | Assessment |

## Analytical Notes and Limitations

1. **Donor status caveat**: Campaign finance data sourced from The Ballot Book (drawing on California FPPC/CAL-ACCESS filings) and Transparency USA. The Becerra campaign had raised approximately $5.74 million as of late April 2026. Individual maximum contributions in California statewide races are $41,600 (2025–2026 cycle). The 0.7% donor entries each represent approximately $40,000. [UNVERIFIED: Whether additional contributions exist in later FPPC filings not yet processed.]

2. **Temporal sequence**: All confirmed donations were made to a 2026 gubernatorial campaign by an individual who left federal office in January 2025. No evidence was found that donations preceded the HHS actions in question in a manner consistent with advance quid pro quo arrangements. The more legally significant question—whether existing political relationships influenced HHS decision-making—cannot be resolved from public records alone.

3. **DHR Health finding specificity**: The December 2022 CMS expansion approval is entity-specific, contested by major industry stakeholders, and made during a period when Cantu and the Border Health PAC would have been known political relationships of Becerra. The FAH characterized the decision as "weakening the law" and setting an "unfortunate precedent." This is the single action in this audit that most closely meets the full materiality test: (a) Secretary-level decision authority, (b) entity-specific, (c) favorable to entity's stated business interest, (d) material beyond routine baseline (over major opposition), and (e) confirmed donor.

4. **Prime Healthcare donor status**: The task brief lists Prime Healthcare as a "maximum/major donor." Available campaign finance data does not confirm this as of the data accessed. This may reflect subsequent filings, corporate PAC contributions through entities not yet itemized, or individual officers not captured. [UNVERIFIED — requires direct FPPC filing review.]

5. **AltaMed institutional contribution**: AltaMed Health Network Inc. is the only institutional (non-individual) healthcare entity confirmed as a direct campaign donor among the entities examined. This is notable because FQHC organizations receive federal grants and have ongoing regulatory relationships with HHS/HRSA. The specific competitive awards received (THCGME, PACE contract, HIV prevention grant) are entity-specific federal actions during the period, though awarded through standard processes.

6. **What this audit cannot determine**: Secretary-level influence on career staff decisions at CMS/HRSA is not visible in public records. Informal regulatory access, pre-decisional meetings, and unofficial communications that may have shaped outcomes are outside the scope of available documentation.
