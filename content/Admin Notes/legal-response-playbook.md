# Legal Response Playbook

Reference document for responding to legal threats against The Donor Map. Not legal advice. Consult a lawyer for any actual legal situation.

## DMCA takedown requests

GitHub Pages will take the site down automatically on receipt of a DMCA notice unless you counter-notice. Most DMCA claims against factual reporting are bogus (DMCA is for copyright, not defamation), but you must respond correctly.

**Timeline:**
1. GitHub notifies you via email
2. You have ~24 hours before the repo is disabled
3. Counter-notice gives GitHub 10-14 business days to restore
4. If the claimant doesn't file a lawsuit in that window, the content goes back up

**Counter-notice process:**
- GitHub's guide: https://docs.github.com/en/site-policy/content-removal-policies/guide-to-submitting-a-dmca-counter-notice
- You must provide: physical signature (or electronic), identification of removed content, statement under penalty of perjury that you believe the removal was a mistake, consent to jurisdiction, your contact info
- Do NOT use a template verbatim. Customize it to the specific situation.

**When a DMCA counter-notice usually wins:**
- The content is factual reporting, not a copy of their copyrighted work
- The content is commentary, criticism, or analysis (fair use)
- The claimant is using DMCA to suppress unfavorable-but-accurate information (abuse of process)

**When to worry:**
- The content actually does contain their copyrighted material (unlikely for this project, but check)
- The claimant is a large organization with a legal team willing to file a lawsuit

## Defamation claims

Defamation is a completely different legal track from DMCA. Much more serious.

**Key legal standard (US):**
- Public figures (politicians, major donors) must prove "actual malice" (you knew it was false or acted with reckless disregard for the truth). Very hard to prove.
- Private figures need only prove negligence. Lower bar.
- Truth is an absolute defense. If the claim is true and sourced, it is not defamation.
- Opinion is protected. "Koch Industries is the largest private polluter in America" (fact, defensible) vs "Koch Industries is evil" (opinion, protected).

**Our defenses:**
1. Source registry traces every factual claim to a government or journalistic source
2. Claim-object pattern separates facts from synthesis
3. Class analysis is clearly opinion/analysis, not factual assertion
4. Corrections policy shows good faith

**If you receive a defamation threat:**
1. Do NOT respond publicly (no tweets, no blog posts, no public statements)
2. Do NOT modify or remove the content (this can be seen as an admission)
3. Save the communication (screenshot, preserve headers)
4. Contact a lawyer immediately (see contacts below)
5. Review the specific claims through the source registry. Can you back every statement?
6. If any claim is genuinely unsourced or wrong, fix it through the corrections process

## Cease-and-desist letters

A C&D is just a letter. It has no legal force by itself. It is the other side saying "we want you to stop or we might sue."

**Response framework:**
1. Read carefully. What specifically do they want removed/changed?
2. Check: is the content accurate and sourced?
3. If accurate: no obligation to remove. Respond politely acknowledging receipt.
4. If there is an actual error: fix it through the corrections process, document the correction
5. If the C&D includes a dollar figure or threatens litigation: escalate to a lawyer immediately

## Rules

1. Never respond to a legal threat on social media or in public
2. Never auto-remove content in response to a threat. Evaluate first.
3. Always respond in writing, always considered, always through the corrections process if the complaint has merit
4. Document everything. Save every email, letter, and communication.
5. When in doubt, call a lawyer before responding
