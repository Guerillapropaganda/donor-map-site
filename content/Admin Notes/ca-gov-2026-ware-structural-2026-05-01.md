# Why Butch Ware Cannot Win the 2026 California Governor's Race

**A structural investigation. The argument is about the system. Ware is the case study.**

The 2026 California Governor's race has eight candidates with FPPC committees, raising between $327,000 (Tony Thurmond, the elected Superintendent of Public Instruction) and $14 million (Tom Steyer, who is funding 99% of his own race through a slogan-branded coalition vehicle). Dr. Rudolph "Butch" Ware III, the 2024 Green Party vice-presidential nominee on the Jill Stein ticket, **also has a registered Cal-Access committee** ("Ware for Governor 2026," filed approximately January 14, 2025) and a formal Green Party of California endorsement (June 16, 2025). He completed the California Secretary of State candidacy filing within the official February 9 to March 6, 2026 window. He was on the tentative certified candidate list released March 23-24, 2026.

He is not on the certified June 2 ballot.

On March 16, 2026, the California Secretary of State removed him. The stated reason, per CalMatters and the Santa Barbara Independent: California requires gubernatorial candidates to submit two copies of five years of tax returns, one unredacted and one with certain items redacted. Ware's redacted copy showed his phone number through what was described as a software malfunction in the redaction process. Three court challenges since have all failed: Sacramento Superior Court denied reinstatement on March 26; the California Court of Appeal summarily denied on April 17; a federal lawsuit filed April 27 in the Eastern District of California is pending before Judge William B. Shubb but has produced no temporary restraining order or preliminary-injunction reinstatement as of May 2.

This is not a story about Butch Ware's individual choices or qualifications. It is a story about a system that requires those choices, those qualifications, and that infrastructure long before voters get the chance to weigh in, and then has procedural mechanisms to remove a candidate who has cleared the upstream barriers. By the time California voters meet the candidates, the system has already finished selecting which candidates voters can meet.

This essay maps five mechanisms.

## Mechanism 1: Top-two primary mathematics

California's gubernatorial election has run under Proposition 14's top-two open primary since 2012 (CA Elections Code §8000-8200, ratified June 2010, first applied to statewide office in 2012). The two highest vote-getters in the June primary advance to the November general election, **regardless of party**. There is no separate Green Party, Libertarian, or Peace and Freedom slot.

The third-party ceiling under this system is no longer hypothetical. Across every contested California statewide race from 2012 through 2024, drawn directly from the California Secretary of State Statement of Vote archive, the highest single third-party finish was **6.6 percent** (Green candidate Ellen Brown, 2014 Treasurer race, in a thin-field race against an unopposed-effective Democratic incumbent). The average highest third-party finish per race was approximately **2.4 percent**. The range was 0.2 percent (Green Josh Jones, Governor 2018) to 6.6 percent. The advance threshold (the second-place finisher's vote share) averaged approximately 18 to 25 percent in contested races. In no contested statewide race during this 12-year window did a third-party or minor-party candidate come within 10 percentage points of the advance threshold.

In gubernatorial primaries specifically:

- **2014 Governor:** Brown (D) 54.3%, Kashkari (R) 19.4%, Donnelly (R) 14.8%. Top Green: Luis Rodriguez at 1.5%. Gap to advance: 17.9 points.
- **2018 Governor:** Newsom (D) 33.7%, Cox (R) 25.4%, Villaraigosa (D) 13.3%. Top third-party: Peace and Freedom Gloria La Riva at 0.3%. Gap: 25.1 points.
- **2022 Governor:** Newsom (D) 55.9%, Dahle (R) 17.7%. Top Green: Luis Rodriguez at 1.8%. Gap: 15.9 points.

The structural ceiling for third-party gubernatorial candidates appears to be approximately 2 percent. The advance threshold to reach the November ballot is approximately 17 to 25 percent. The gap is roughly 15 to 20 percentage points and has not been closed in any post-Prop 14 cycle.

The top-two structure is not theoretically hostile to minor parties. In practice it has eliminated them entirely from statewide November ballots since 2012.

## Mechanism 2: Ballot access threshold

Reaching the primary ballot, which is upstream of the top-two cut, is an operations problem with two costs.

**The filing fee** (CA Elections Code §8020) is set at 1 percent of the first year's salary of the office sought. For Governor in 2026 that fee is approximately $2,250. This fee alone is not prohibitive.

**The signatures-in-lieu route** (CA Elections Code §8068) requires collecting registered-voter signatures equal to 1 percent of the district's registered voters to fully offset the filing fee. For statewide office in 2026, registered voters number approximately 22 million; the 1 percent threshold is approximately 220,000 signatures. At market rates documented by the Sacramento Bee (March 2026) and the New York Times (April 2026), professional signature-gathering currently runs approximately $3 to $15 per signature. A statewide signatures-in-lieu campaign at $5 per signature is approximately $1.1 million in operational cost; at $10 per signature, $2.2 million. This is the figure that prices out grassroots candidates.

A separate amendment from February 2012, documented by the LA Times in October 2012 ("How Prop. 14 weakened democracy"), increased the minimum number of signatures required for minor-party candidates from a maximum of 150 to between **1,500 and 10,000** depending on the office. This was passed by the Legislature after Prop 14 took effect, tightening the access mechanism that the ballot proposition itself did not specifically target.

A candidate who pays the $2,250 filing fee directly and collects the minimum 65 to 100 nomination-paper signatures (CA Elections Code §8062) can appear on the primary ballot. Ware did this. The ballot-access cost was not the binding constraint on his candidacy. The downstream procedural mechanism (Mechanism 5 below) was.

At the party-committee level, the asymmetry is structural. The California Democratic Party state committee has raised approximately $220 million across 2017 to present. The California Republican Party state committee runs at $18 to 24 million per cycle. The Green Party of California state committee operates at approximately $143,000 across the same eight-year window, a Democratic-to-Green ratio of approximately 1,540 to 1. Even the Republican-to-Green ratio is approximately 130 to 1.

No third-party or minor-party candidate for California Governor or U.S. Senate between 2014 and 2024 has been identified as having raised over $250,000 in their own primary committee. No IE committee primarily formed to support a minor-party statewide candidate has been identified as raising over $50,000 in named-publication coverage during the same period.

Major donors give to candidates they expect to influence. Influence requires being competitive. Major donors do not give to candidates whose ceiling under Mechanism 1 is 2 percent of the primary vote. The donor pool has rationally pre-selected against third-party candidates as a class.

## Mechanism 4: Debate-stage exclusion

The 2026 cycle has produced multiple major debates with documented inclusion criteria.

The **USC/ABC debate** scheduled for March 2026 (canceled March 24 after backlash over candidate-of-color exclusion) used both a polling threshold and a fundraising threshold set by an outside political expert. Six candidates met both criteria; the four Democrats of color who did not were the basis of the cancellation criticism. No minor-party candidates were among the 10-plus candidates invited or considered.

The **KTLA/Inside California Politics debate** on April 22, 2026 included six leading candidates based on polling and fundraising (CalMatters, March 10, 2026).

The **Pomona College/televised debate** on April 29, 2026 used a 1 percent polling threshold (KQED, April 30, 2026). The KQED debate with a 1 percent threshold excluded most minor-party candidates whose polling is typically below 0.5 percent or unmeasured entirely. Ware received no debate invitations in 2026.

The **CNN debate** on May 5, 2026 qualified seven candidates (Bianco, Hilton, Becerra, Mahan, Porter, Steyer, Villaraigosa) on a $1 million raised plus 3 percent in two California gubernatorial polls by April 27. Ware met neither.

The pattern across cycles is consistent: debate inclusion follows polling and fundraising thresholds. Third-party candidates cannot meet polling thresholds because they have no debate-driven name recognition, and they cannot gain name recognition without debate inclusion. This circular exclusion is self-reinforcing across every cycle documented.

## Mechanism 5: Downstream procedural removal

The fifth mechanism, surfaced sharply by Ware's 2026 experience, operates after a candidate has cleared the upstream four. It is the procedural disqualification mechanism.

Ware filed within the official window. The Green Party of California formally endorsed him on June 16, 2025. He completed the candidacy paperwork. He was on the tentative certified list released March 23-24, 2026. Then:

On **March 16, 2026** the California Secretary of State removed him from the certified ballot. The stated reason was a redaction defect: California requires gubernatorial candidates to submit two copies of five years of tax returns, one unredacted and one with certain items redacted. The redaction in Ware's submitted copy showed his phone number through what the campaign described as a software malfunction in the redaction process.

On **March 26, 2026** Sacramento Superior Court Judge James Arguelles denied Ware's lawsuit (Ware v. Weber, Case No. 26WM000074) seeking reinstatement.

On **April 17, 2026** the California Court of Appeal summarily denied the appeal (Ware v. Superior Court of Sacramento County, C106053).

On **April 27, 2026** Ware filed a federal lawsuit (Ware v. Weber, 2:26-cv-1643, E.D. Cal., assigned to Judge William B. Shubb) challenging the constitutionality of the California gubernatorial tax-return-disclosure law and alleging discriminatory enforcement. The case is pending. As of May 2, 2026, no temporary restraining order or preliminary-injunction reinstatement had been issued.

Ware does not appear on the certified June 2, 2026 primary ballot.

The mechanism here is not an upstream barrier. He cleared the upstream barriers. The mechanism is a downstream procedural rule whose enforcement is technically defensible on its face (tax returns must be redacted properly per California law) but whose practical effect is to remove a third-party candidate who would, under Mechanism 1, have polled below 2 percent and not advanced anyway. The system did not need to remove him to ensure he would not win the primary. It removed him anyway.

This is the mechanism the structural argument would have missed without the 2026 case-study evidence. Mechanisms 1 through 4 explain why no third-party candidate has won a contested California statewide race since 2012. Mechanism 5 explains why even a third-party candidate who has cleared the upstream barriers can still be procedurally disqualified between the certified-list release and the primary itself, with three layers of court review available but none expected to resolve in time for the candidate's name to appear on ballots that the Secretary of State has begun mailing on May 4, 2026.

## The verdict (system-level)

The five mechanisms together do not require any single bad actor. No party operative is conspiring to keep Ware out. No outlet is editorially blacklisting him. The Secretary of State applied a tax-return redaction rule that exists for legitimate reasons (candidate financial transparency under California Election Code requirements voters approved). The system simply *requires* candidates to clear thresholds (fundraising, ballot access, polling, media, procedural compliance) that exist for legitimate reasons in isolation but that *aggregate* into a structural lock-out for any candidate without the resource base of a major party plus the institutional infrastructure to navigate every procedural detail without a single defect.

The lock-out is not absolute. Ross Perot (1992 federal), Jesse Ventura (1998 Minnesota), Bernie Sanders (Vermont, then Democratic presidential primaries), Angus King (Maine) have demonstrated paths around or through similar structures, but always with one of: extraordinary personal wealth, prior major-party affiliation, statewide office held under prior rules, or the institutional support of one of the two major parties even in nominally third-party form.

A candidate without those advantages, running as a true minor-party challenger, does not win statewide office in California in 2026. The structural argument is not that it is *hard*. The structural argument is that the system has already *settled* the question, before voters have been consulted, by the time the FPPC clock starts. And if upstream procedural friction is somehow cleared by a candidate without major-party infrastructure, downstream procedural mechanisms remain as a backstop.

**Butch Ware as a person is not the story. The system that filed him, certified him, removed him, denied his three court challenges, and produced his absence from the June 2 ballot before voters had any chance to weigh in is the story.** The verdict applies equally to a Libertarian challenger, a Peace and Freedom challenger, a No Party Preference challenger without a billionaire's checkbook (Tom Steyer would not be in this race without his own wealth — same mechanics, different beneficiary), or any future third-party candidate operating in California's post-2012 election structure.

## Whether Prop 14 was *designed* to do this

The historical record supports "emergent effect" rather than deliberate design. The proponent voter-guide arguments for Prop 14 (CA SOS 2010 voter guide archive) framed the measure entirely as anti-gridlock reform empowering independent voters; minor parties were not mentioned. The principal political architect, Republican state Senator Abel Maldonado, modeled the measure on Washington State's top-two system adopted in 2008; his stated goal was producing more centrist legislators (The Atlantic, December 2015), not eliminating Greens or Libertarians.

However, the practical consequence was both foreseeable and foreseen. The 2010 *argument against* Prop 14, signed by minor-party representatives, explicitly predicted minor-party elimination from general-election ballots. Both major parties opposed Prop 14 (KQED, June 4, 2010), based on loss of nominating-process control rather than concern for minor parties. The Legislature's February 2012 amendment increasing minor-party signature requirements after Prop 14 took effect (LA Times, October 24, 2012) created an additional barrier *after the fact*, suggesting reinforcing rather than founding intent.

In November 2024, three California minor parties (Green, Libertarian, Peace and Freedom) filed a federal lawsuit against Secretary of State Shirley Weber arguing the system creates "an unconstitutionally insurmountable barrier." The plaintiffs cited that "in almost every contest for elective office covered by the top-two primary system, in which at least two major party candidates appeared in the primary, no minor party or independent candidate has been allowed access to the general election ballot in California."

The preponderance of primary-source evidence indicates Prop 14's elimination of minor parties from general-election ballots was a foreseeable but not stated-intent consequence. The structural lock-out was not designed. It emerged from the mathematical reality of an electorate where Democratic and Republican voter registration collectively exceeds 60 percent, compounded by resource asymmetries (Mechanisms 2-4) and downstream procedural mechanisms (Mechanism 5).

That the lock-out was not designed does not make it less real. It does, however, change the editorial framing: this is not a conspiracy to be exposed. This is a structure to be understood.
