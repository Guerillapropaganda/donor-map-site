# Beat Drafts — Editorial Sketches

Three new beat candidates emerged from the May 2 returns. Drafted here as editorial sketches, not full HTML. Each sketch shows: headline + deck (the punch test), lede (the thesis), key receipts (the hardest facts), structural argument (why it's its own beat), and a Groundbreaking? verdict.

Format per draft: roughly the length of a long lede plus a tight argument frame. Enough to tell whether to graduate to full prototype build.

## Draft #1 — Villaraigosa: The Pledge He Broke

**Headline:** *The pledge he broke. The donors who took it.*

**Deck:** In 2018, Antonio Villaraigosa publicly promised never to take fossil-fuel money. In 2026, oil and gas companies are his largest organized donor bloc. Even his old donor class gave up.

### Lede

In 2018, Antonio Villaraigosa was running for governor and signed a pledge: no contributions from oil companies, no contributions from named fossil-fuel executives. He had just accepted maximum-allowable contributions from several oil donors months earlier, but the pledge was the new rule going forward.

He has not re-signed that pledge in 2026.

He has, however, accepted **$307,000 in primary-and-general maxes from California's biggest oil and gas producers**: $72,800 from California Resources Corporation (the state's largest), $78,400 each from Chevron, Marathon Petroleum, and Berry Corporation. His campaign bills him as the working-class affordability candidate. Restrictions on California oil production he calls "poppycock." The donors agree.

The 2026 pledge is gone. The donors who would have been excluded from the 2018 ledger appear at the top of the 2026 one. And the donor class that put $7 million into his 2018 IE through Reed Hastings's checkbook? They walked. Hastings's 2026 governor money went to Matt Mahan instead. The candidate who ran in 2018 as the donor class's investment vehicle is running in 2026 as the candidate the donor class moved off of, while still taking maxes from the industry he had once promised to refuse.

### Receipts (Cal-Access RCPT_CD primary extraction, May 2 2026)

**Fossil-fuel donor stack to committee 1471635:**
- California Resources Corporation and Subsidiaries — $72,800 (state's largest oil-and-gas producer, spun off from Occidental)
- Chevron Corporation and Subsidiaries — $78,400
- Marathon Petroleum Corporation — $78,400
- Berry Corporation and Affiliated Entities — $78,400 (Kern County oil)
- Clean Energy — $156,800 (natural gas vehicle-fueling, not renewable)
- **Total fossil-fuel-and-natural-gas: $464,800 cumulative**

**Healthcare donor stack to a single-payer opponent:**
- AltaMed Health Network — $78,400 (FQHC; Villaraigosa was paid advisor post-2013)
- COPE Healthcare Consulting — $78,400
- Fresenius Medical Care — $15,000 (German-owned dialysis giant, has litigated against California healthcare regulation per 9th Circuit Apr 7, 2026)

**Post-mayor income trajectory** (LA Times tax returns 2017): $167K as mayor 2011 → $1.38M in 2014. Mercury Public Affairs clients included Hungary, Qatar, Turkey, California Charter Schools Association, Westlands Water District. Actum LLC retained Hungary's government. As Newsom's "infrastructure czar" (Aug 2022 - Jul 2023), Villaraigosa's $381,820 salary was paid by California Forward, a nonprofit funded by AT&T ($10K), SoCalGas ($17.5K), Disney ($10K), Southern California Edison ($10K), Doordash ($10K), Port of San Diego ($30K) — entities with regulatory interests before the state.

**The 2018 vs 2026 IE money flip:**
- 2018: Reed Hastings $7M to California Charter Schools Association IE supporting Villaraigosa. Eli Broad $1.5M to same. ~$22M total CCSA IE flowed to him.
- 2026: Hastings's $1M went to Matt Mahan's IE instead. No equivalent CCSA-sponsored IE has materialized for Villaraigosa.

### The structural argument

Villaraigosa's 2026 platform centers on "affordability for working-class Californians." The structural contradiction is not labor vs business (he genuinely has building-trades support). The contradiction is that the candidate pitching gas-price relief is funded by the companies whose revenue depends on gas prices staying high under California's carbon-cap structure. The candidate opposing single-payer healthcare (the policy with the largest documented potential to lower working-class healthcare costs) is funded by healthcare entities that profit from the multi-payer status quo. The candidate selling himself as the anti-special-interest insurgent took $381K from a Newsom-curated nonprofit funded by California utilities and Disney.

The donor class is rationally allocating against its threats. Steyer threatens fossil-fuel margins through commercial-Prop-13 reform. Villaraigosa does not. So fossil fuel money flows to Villaraigosa and against Steyer simultaneously. Becerra defends Chevron's $39,200. Villaraigosa cashes its $78,400. The pattern across the Democratic field is consistent: donors are choosing the candidates whose platforms protect their margins.

### The "even Reed Hastings gave up" angle

The most editorially powerful single fact here is that Reed Hastings, who put $7 million into Villaraigosa's 2018 IE, dropped him for 2026. Hastings's 2026 charter-school money went to Mahan. That is the donor class itself rejecting Villaraigosa as their preferred 2026 vehicle. Villaraigosa is now running as the candidate the institutional donor class has moved off of, while still taking the contributions from the regulated industries whose policy interests overlap with his platform. The two stories together are an honest portrait of the 2026 Villaraigosa coalition: the major institutional donor base has moved on; the regulated-industry donor base is hedging on him because the alternatives (Steyer, Porter) are worse for their interests.

### Open questions

1. The exact date of the 2018 pledge document needs primary-source link (LA Times May 26, 2025 references it; the original signed pledge document URL isn't in the report).
2. Confirmation that Hastings's 2026 governor money went specifically to Mahan via Cal-Access (FPPC top-contributors list verifies $1M Hastings → Mahan IE per the source-hardening report).
3. Whether the 2018 pledge had an expiration or whether Villaraigosa has explicitly stated he no longer considers himself bound by it.

### Groundbreaking? **Yes — second-tier groundbreaking.**

Not first-tier ("$31M to bury a class traitor" was first-tier because the target was actively organizing against the donor class and the donor class was visibly retaliating). Villaraigosa is second-tier groundbreaking because the story is hypocrisy plus structural rejection: he broke the pledge, the donor class he wanted moved on anyway, and he is now coalition-locked into the regulated-industry money he once promised to refuse. That is a sharper portrait than "he's a normal Democrat with bad donors." The editorial frame "even his own donor class moved off him" is the hook that makes this not just an oppo dump.

### Recommendation: **GRADUATE to full prototype HTML.**

Pair with Race Map (June 1) as a thread, OR ship standalone in early June if Villaraigosa polls higher than expected on June 2. Either way the receipts are sharp enough to publish.

## Draft #2 — Bianco: The Sheriff Who Seized the Ballots

**Headline:** *The sheriff who seized the ballots. The judge he had endorsed.*

**Deck:** Riverside County Sheriff Chad Bianco took 650,000 ballots from the 2025 Prop 50 referendum on a warrant from a judge he had endorsed in 2022. Then he ran for governor on the same enforcement model.

### Lede

In November 2025, California voters approved Proposition 50, redrawing the state's congressional districts. In Riverside County, Sheriff Chad Bianco's office seized **650,000 ballots** from that election — claiming an election fraud investigation. The warrant authorizing the seizure came from Judge Jay Kiel, whom Bianco had endorsed in his 2022 judicial campaign and who had publicly praised Bianco. California Attorney General Rob Bonta filed legal challenges. The Riverside County Board of Supervisors, in a 4-1 vote, refused to authorize county funds to pay for outside counsel defending Bianco's actions.

Three months later, Bianco was a 2026 California gubernatorial candidate polling 12-14 percent and headed to the May 5 CNN debate stage.

The donors who funded his rise included **$156,800 from two companies sharing the same sibling managers** (Downs Energy and M&D Development, both run by Michael Downs and Sherry Messner from a Corona mailbox). The Fair Political Practices Commission had been asked to look at whether those contributions should be aggregated as exceeding the $78,400 single-source maximum. **$132,523 came from law-enforcement union PACs**: PORAC, Santa Barbara Deputy Sheriffs, San Diego Deputy Sheriffs, and his own Riverside Sheriffs Association Public Education Fund.

And in 2014, before he became sheriff, Bianco was a paid member of the Oath Keepers — a fact LAist confirmed in 2021 from Anonymous's Epik leak and NPR confirmed independently from DDoSecrets's parallel release. Bianco has not denied it. He has called the Oath Keepers "not anti-government" and blamed January 6 on "a few fringe people."

### Receipts (Cal-Access RCPT_CD primary extraction May 2 2026, plus named-publication primary sources)

**The ballot seizure:**
- 650,000 ballots seized from the 2025 Prop 50 referendum (CalMatters Mar 26, 2026)
- Warrant from Judge Jay Kiel, whom Bianco endorsed in 2022 (CalMatters Apr 21, 2026 — full email investigation)
- AG Bonta's challenge; County Board 4-1 vote denying outside counsel funding

**Donor structure** (Cal-Access RCPT_CD, committee 1479095, total $4,469,855):
- Downs Energy + M&D Development — combined $156,800 from sibling-managed entities; FPPC aggregation question pending (SF Chronicle Mar 31, 2026)
- Highland Fairview Operating Co. — $39,200 (developer of 40.6M sq ft World Logistics Center in Moreno Valley, Bianco's jurisdiction; 2013 federal corruption investigation history)
- R.J. Noble Company — $35,000 (Riverside County road contractor)
- Mediwaste Disposal — $20,000 (medical/hazardous waste contractor; whether they hold the Riverside County jail medical-waste contract is open)
- Walker Evans Construction & Investments — $39,200 (off-road racing legend Walker Evans, whom Bianco eulogized publicly)

**Law-enforcement money:**
- Peace Officers Research Association of California PAC — $39,200 (PORAC endorsement Aug 21, 2025; first time PORAC split between R and D in same gov race)
- Riverside Sheriffs Association Public Education Fund — $38,123 (Bianco's own department's labor org)
- Santa Barbara County Deputy Sheriffs Association — $30,000
- Deputy Sheriffs Association of San Diego County PAC — $25,000

**Oath Keepers + Constitutional Sheriffs:**
- 2014 Oath Keepers paid membership confirmed via Anonymous Epik leak (LAist Oct 5, 2021) and DDoSecrets (NPR Oct 6, 2021)
- Bianco quoted defending Oath Keepers as "not anti-government"
- Constitutional Sheriffs and Peace Officers Association affiliation confirmed; 2026 ballot-seizure investigation reportedly influenced by CSPOA network email from Steve Tuminello in September 2022 (CalMatters Apr 21, 2026)
- Claremont Institute's "Sheriff of the Year" award

### The structural argument

Three threads converge. (1) **The donor-jurisdiction conflict**: Highland Fairview is the largest industrial real-estate developer in his county; RJ Noble is a county road contractor; Mediwaste likely holds his jail's medical-waste contract. None of these are FPPC-illegal. They are exactly what the structural critique of sheriff-as-elected-prosecutor predicts: the people whose business operates inside your enforcement perimeter fund your campaign for higher office. (2) **The judge-warrant feedback loop**: Bianco endorsed Judge Kiel in 2022. Kiel issued the warrant authorizing Bianco's 650K-ballot seizure in 2025. The Constitutional Sheriffs movement explicitly holds that county sheriffs' authority supersedes federal and state law enforcement; Tuminello's email called Bianco a "Constitutional Sheriff." When the structural model is "my judicial allies authorize the actions I want to take and my municipal allies fund my next office," the system works as designed. (3) **The Oath Keepers prior**: not the editorial focal point in this beat. It is the layer the rivals will lead with. The structural story is the Highland Fairview / Kiel warrant / CSPOA architecture, with Oath Keepers as historical context.

### The Republican-side equivalent of the Class Traitor frame

Class Traitor was about a Democratic billionaire whose platform threatened the regulated-industry donor class, leading them to spend $20M against him. This beat is about a Republican county sheriff whose enforcement architecture serves the donor class within his county, leading them to fund his rise to higher office. Symmetric structural story, opposite direction. Both are donor-class-defends-its-interests stories. The headline "The Sheriff Who Seized the Ballots" puts the Prop 50 incident at the front because that is the most-shareable single fact; the ballot seizure is then the entry point to the donor-jurisdiction conflict and the CSPOA architecture underneath.

### Open questions

1. Whether Highland Fairview / Mediwaste / RJ Noble hold current Riverside County contracts at the dollar level that would create an explicit conflict-of-interest violation under California's local-government conflict rules. CalMatters has the email investigation; specific contract amounts may need Riverside County Clerk records request.
2. The status of AG Bonta's legal challenge to the Prop 50 ballot seizure; resolution after the May 2 research date is open.
3. Whether the FPPC has formally opened an aggregation inquiry into Downs Energy + M&D Development (SF Chronicle reported FPPC declined comment on the specific donations).

### Groundbreaking? **Yes — first-tier groundbreaking.**

This is a first-tier story because the editorial frame ("the sheriff seized 650,000 ballots on a warrant from a judge he had endorsed") is one sentence and stops the reader cold. The donor-jurisdiction conflict and CSPOA architecture support the structural claim that Bianco is not a "rogue sheriff" but the operational example of the Constitutional Sheriffs model — the local-enforcement-as-political-vehicle system explicitly endorsed by the movement. The Oath Keepers material adds national-political-violence resonance without requiring it as the central claim. **This is the GOP-side investigation equivalent in editorial weight to the Class Traitor beat.**

### Recommendation: **GRADUATE to full prototype HTML.**

Could ship standalone post-primary if Bianco performs (he qualified for the May 5 debate, polling 12-14%). Or fold into Race Map June 1 as the GOP exemplar. My instinct: **standalone is stronger** because the ballot-seizure-plus-warrant-judge story is too sharp to bury inside a multi-candidate Race Map. Suggest standalone target window: June 8-15 (post-primary, before the runoff field is set).

## Draft #3 — Bearstar Octopus: One Shop, Three IEs, Newsom-Orbit

**Headline:** *One shop. Three IEs. The Newsom-orbit machine running both sides of the 2026 California governor's primary.*

**Deck:** Bearstar Strategies has been on three independent expenditures in the same race: pro-Swalwell, anti-Steyer, pro-Becerra. The same firm. The same address book. The same Newsom-orbit consultancy named in a federal indictment four months before the launch.

### Lede

In early March 2026, Bearstar Strategies, Inc. — the political consultancy built around Ace Smith, Sean Clegg, Juan Rodriguez, and Newsom's former chief of staff Jim DeBoo — was the operational shop behind a pro-Eric-Swalwell independent expenditure committee that launched with statewide TV advertising. Two weeks later, sexual-misconduct allegations forced Swalwell's exit from the race; the pro-Swalwell IE dissolved and began refunding donors.

By April 1, the same firm was on the anti-Steyer committee filings — paid $49,800 by California Is Not For Sale (FPPC #1489677) for "Digital Media/Oppose/Tom Steyer/Governor/Statewide." Jim DeBoo was, per Politico's April 17 reporting and the Sacramento Bee's April 20 follow-up, leading that effort.

By May 1, the same firm was on a pro-Xavier-Becerra independent expenditure committee that had just launched. KCRA-TV's May 1 coverage flagged the launch as notable specifically because "some of the same consultants now connected with Bearstar were previously linked with a group tied with that case" — referring to the November 2025 federal indictment of Dana Williamson, the former Newsom chief of staff who was an architect of "The Collaborative," the Democratic public-affairs hub that includes DeBoo, Clegg, and lobbyist Greg Campbell. Williamson was charged on 23 federal counts of bank and tax fraud, conspiracy, and the rest. The indictment alleged she billed Becerra's dormant state campaign account for bogus consulting services and claimed luxury goods as business expenses. DeBoo and Clegg have not been charged.

This is the operational architecture of the 2026 California Democratic gubernatorial primary, when you read the Cal-Access expenditure files instead of the candidate webpages.

### Receipts (Cal-Access EXPN_CD primary extraction May 2 2026, plus named-publication confirmation)

**Bearstar Strategies, three IEs in one primary cycle:**
- Pro-Swalwell IE — launched early March 2026 with TV ads; dissolved and refunded after Swalwell's exit (Politico Apr 17, 2026)
- Anti-Steyer IE — $49,800 paid April 1, 2026 from committee 1489677 (Cal-Access EXPN_CD filing 3138008, VERIFIED)
- Pro-Becerra IE — launched May 1, 2026 (KSFO/KCRA May 1, 2026)

**Polaris Campaigns, Inc., the largest single anti-Steyer vendor:**
- $13,782,095 across five Cal-Access EXPN_CD payments to committee 1489677 (Mar 31 / Apr 3 / Apr 6 / Apr 13 / Apr 17)
- Every line filed under "Digital Media/Oppose/Tom Steyer/Governor/Statewide"
- Polaris's other 2026 California work: $50,870,203 from Newsom's Yes-on-Prop-50 redistricting committee (FPPC #1380675), the largest single payee on that campaign

**The Williamson indictment context:**
- Dana Williamson — Newsom chief of staff and architect of The Collaborative — indicted November 2025 on 23 federal counts (LA Times Nov 13, 2025)
- The Collaborative members: DeBoo (managing director), Clegg / Bearstar (partner), Williamson, Campbell (lobbyist), Shannon Murphy (M Strategic Communications)
- Consumer Watchdog Jan 13, 2026: FBI surveillance notices reportedly received by "many associates and clients" of The Collaborative

**The compliance architecture:**
- Treasurer/compliance for the anti-Steyer committee 1489677: Deane & Company (president Shawnda Deane, who previously served as treasurer for Steyer's own Need to Impeach PAC — Capital Research Center July 2019)
- Likely Form 410 signatory for 1489677: Laiza Negrete, VP Political Affairs at California Association of Realtors (the committee's $5M direct funder), who previously worked at Deane & Company
- Spokesperson: Amelia Matier (also spokesperson for No on Prop 30 in 2022, Yes on Prop 5 in 2024)

### The structural argument

Newsom is not on the 2026 gubernatorial ballot. He is term-limited. But the political-consulting infrastructure that built and defended his career — Bearstar Strategies, Polaris Campaigns, Jim DeBoo personally, the Williamson-architected Collaborative, Deane & Company on treasury — is the operational backbone of the 2026 race anyway. They worked for Swalwell while he was the Newsom-orbit pick. When Swalwell collapsed, they pivoted to opposing Steyer (the donor-class threat) and supporting Becerra (the donor-class-acceptable alternative). The simultaneous involvement in three IEs across one primary is a structural fact about California political consulting under one-party-dominant conditions: there is one major consulting infrastructure, and it operates wherever the influence opportunities are.

The Williamson indictment is the load-bearing structural detail. It is not that Bearstar is criminally implicated. It is that the consulting infrastructure operating the 2026 race is the same network whose senior architect is currently under federal indictment for billing a candidate's dormant campaign account for fabricated consulting. KCRA noticed. Consumer Watchdog noticed. The structural question — whether Becerra knew the consulting infrastructure now supporting his IE was the same network that had allegedly defrauded him while he was at HHS — is one the named-publication record cannot yet answer. The fact that the pattern is documented and ongoing is itself the story.

### Open questions

1. Whether DeBoo, Clegg, or other Bearstar principals have been interviewed in the Williamson investigation. Bloomberg Law confirms the indictment; the witness list is sealed.
2. The pro-Becerra IE's full FPPC filing — committee name, ID, and donor list. KSFO/KCRA confirms launch but the committee may not yet appear in the FPPC top-contributors list (which requires $1M+ raised).
3. Whether any FPPC coordination complaint has been filed regarding Bearstar's simultaneous involvement in opposing-side committees within the same primary. Politico noted the configuration; no complaint has surfaced in named-publication coverage as of May 2.

### Groundbreaking? **Yes — first-tier structural-story groundbreaking.**

Different shape than Class Traitor or Bianco-Ballots. Those are candidate-centric stories with structural dimensions. This is a structural story with no single candidate as the protagonist. The protagonist is the consulting infrastructure itself. Process journalism. The "groundbreaking" claim is that the same firm visibly working three sides of the same primary, with one side's senior architect currently under federal indictment, is editorially significant in a way that has not been covered in any named publication as a single integrated story. Politico, Sac Bee, LA Times, KQED have each reported pieces of it. None has put the full architecture into one piece.

### Recommendation: **GRADUATE to full prototype HTML.**

Already in beats-catalog as upcoming with target ship May 27 (Bearstar Octopus). Material is now primary-source rich enough to support full prototype build. Could fold the Williamson/Collaborative thread (C-004) in as a third act of this same beat rather than separate.
