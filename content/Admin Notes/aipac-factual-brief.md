## Organizational structure

AIPAC (American Israel Public Affairs Committee) is organized as a 501(c)(4) tax-exempt 'social welfare' organization under the Internal Revenue Code. It was founded in 1954 under the name American Zionist Committee for Public Affairs (AZCPA) by Isaiah L. Kenen; the name was changed to American Israel Public Affairs Committee in 1959 to broaden its membership base. As of January 2026, AIPAC reports more than six million members across all 50 states. AIPAC maintains 17 regional offices and a headquarters in Washington, D.C. As a 501(c)(4), AIPAC is not required to publicly disclose the identity of its individual donors; its funding comes from member dues and voluntary contributions from its membership base, not from the Israeli government or foreign entities. AIPAC has stated publicly that it does not accept funding from foreign governments. In addition to the 501(c)(4) lobbying entity, AIPAC operates two electoral vehicles: AIPAC PAC (a traditional federal PAC, Committee ID C00797670, founded December 2021) and the United Democracy Project (UDP, a super PAC, Committee ID C00799031, also founded 2021). AIPAC spent $3,324,268 on direct lobbying in 2024, and its combined PAC and super PAC disbursements for the 2023–2024 election cycle totaled approximately $126.9 million, according to FEC data. During the first six months of 2025, AIPAC's lobbying expenditures totaled $1.8 million. AIPAC's reported annual operating budget has been cited at approximately $70 million, with approximately 200 full-time employees. The organization's CEO as of early 2026 is Elliot Brandt.

## Donor categories

AIPAC's political giving flows through three vehicles: its 501(c)(4) entity (which does not publicly disclose donors), AIPAC PAC (a traditional PAC that discloses donors per FEC rules), and the United Democracy Project (UDP), a super PAC that discloses all contributions of $200 or more to the FEC. According to OpenSecrets data for the 2023–2024 election cycle, AIPAC and related pro-Israel organizations contributed a combined $78,105,582 across the pro-Israel donor category, with AIPAC itself accounting for $43,452,013 of that total. Analysis of publicly disclosed FEC filings for UDP — which received over $73 million in the 2023–2024 cycle — reveals that the dominant donor industries are: (1) Finance, Investment Management, Hedge Funds, and Private Equity, which collectively represent the largest share of identifiable large gifts, with executives from firms in asset management, hedge funds, venture capital, and private equity accounting for roughly 70% of gifts over $1 million; (2) Real Estate and Real Estate Development, which is the second-largest identifiable category, with executives from real estate investment, property management, and development firms appearing prominently throughout the donor list; (3) Retired/High-Net-Worth Individuals, a large volume category without a single sector affiliation; (4) Retail, Fashion, and Manufacturing, a smaller but identifiable cluster; and (5) Technology and Venture Capital, a present but not dominant category. The Finance/Real Estate combination aligns with reporting by The Forward and In These Times, which independently described AIPAC's non-public donor list as 'dominated by finance and real estate professionals,' specifically noting executives from hedge funds, private equity, asset management, and large real estate enterprises. AIPAC PAC itself raised $34,339,752.62 in the 2023–2024 cycle per FEC records, with approximately 95% from individual contributions.

## State-level anti-BDS laws

As of 2024, 38 states have enacted anti-BDS laws or executive orders designed to discourage boycotts of Israel, according to Wikipedia's Anti-BDS laws article. The Israel Legal Advocacy Project, which maintains a running tracker, counted 37 states as of July 6, 2023, with New Hampshire added by executive order in July 2023 and Alaska by administrative order in February 2024, bringing the total to approximately 38–39 states with enforceable measures. The Just Vision Legislation Tracker (last updated January 2026) lists laws by state. These laws fall into two primary categories: (1) Contracting restrictions: Require government contractors to certify in writing that they are not engaged in, and will not engage in, a boycott of Israel for the duration of the contract. Applicable states include Alabama, Arizona, Arkansas, California, Georgia, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, West Virginia, and Wisconsin, among others. Most laws exempt contracts below $100,000 and companies with fewer than 5–10 employees, thresholds added in response to court rulings. (2) Investment/divestment restrictions: Require state pension funds and public investment entities to create 'scrutinized company' blacklists of entities boycotting Israel and to divest from such companies, and prohibit future investment in them. Applicable states include Arizona, Arkansas, Colorado, Florida, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Mississippi, Nevada, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Texas, and others. Several states have both contracting and investment provisions. Key court rulings: In Koontz v. Watson (D. Kan. 2017), the U.S. District Court for the District of Kansas granted a preliminary injunction against Kansas's 2017 anti-BDS law (HB 2409), finding it likely unconstitutional as a viewpoint-based restriction on protected First Amendment speech. Kansas subsequently amended the law (HB 2482, 2018) to narrow its scope, and the case was dismissed by agreement. In Jordahl v. Brnovich (D. Ariz. 2018), the U.S. District Court for the District of Arizona granted a preliminary injunction against Arizona's HB 2617, holding it likely violated the First Amendment by burdening expressive conduct protected under NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co. The state amended the law (SB 1167, effective August 2019); the Ninth Circuit vacated the injunction and remanded with instructions to dismiss for mootness on January 6, 2020. In Amawi v. Pflugerville Independent School District (W.D. Tex. 2019), the U.S. District Court for the Western District of Texas declared Texas HB 89 unconstitutional as a content- and viewpoint-based restriction on protected speech under the First Amendment, finding that boycotts are inherently expressive conduct protected under Claiborne. Texas amended the law (HB 793, 2019) to exempt sole proprietors; the Fifth Circuit vacated the preliminary injunction and dismissed for mootness on April 27, 2020. In Martin v. Wrigley (N.D. Ga. 2021), the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Georgia ruled Georgia's anti-BDS certification requirement unconstitutional under the First Amendment as a compelled speech and viewpoint-based restriction, finding it 'no different than requiring a person to espouse certain political beliefs.' After Georgia amended the law in 2022, the case was dismissed as moot; the Eleventh Circuit affirmed the dismissal. In Arkansas Times LP v. Waldrip (8th Cir. 2022, en banc), the en banc Eighth Circuit upheld Arkansas's Act 710 on June 22, 2022, reversing a prior 2021 panel decision that had found it unconstitutional. The en banc majority held that the law regulates commercial conduct — not protected expressive activity — and therefore does not violate the First Amendment. The Supreme Court declined to hear the case on February 21, 2023. In Ali v. Hogan (D. Md./4th Cir.), the district court dismissed a challenge to Maryland's anti-BDS executive order for lack of standing and injury; the Fourth Circuit affirmed.

## Federal anti-BDS legislation

No federal anti-BDS legislation has been enacted into law as a standalone measure targeting the BDS movement specifically. The following bills were introduced in Congress during the last five years: (1) Combating BDS Act of 2021 (S. 2119, 117th Congress): Introduced June 17, 2021 by Sen. Marco Rubio (R-FL) and Sen. Joe Manchin (D-WV). The bill would clarify that state and local governments may adopt and enforce measures to divest from or restrict contracting with entities engaging in commerce- or investment-related BDS activity targeting Israel, without being preempted by federal law. Referred to committee; did not advance to a floor vote. (2) Combating BDS Act of 2023 (S. 1637, 118th Congress): Introduced May 17, 2023 by Sen. Marco Rubio (R-FL). Original cosponsors: Sens. Bill Cassidy (R-LA), Mike Braun (R-IN), Rick Scott (R-FL), Bill Hagerty (R-TN), and Steve Daines (R-MT). The bill is substantively identical to prior versions, authorizing state and local anti-BDS measures. Per LegiScan, the bill died in committee (Senate committee referral, 25% progression). (3) IGO Anti-Boycott Act (H.R. 867, 119th Congress): Introduced January 2025 by Rep. Mike Lawler (R-NY) and Rep. Josh Gottheimer (D-NJ), with cosponsors including Reps. Ben Cline (R-VA), Greg Steube (R-FL), and Elise Stefanik (R-NY). This bill would extend existing U.S. anti-boycott law (originally passed in the 1970s and updated in 2018) to cover boycotts organized by international governmental organizations (IGOs) such as the United Nations, in addition to foreign governments. An identical version passed the House by voice vote in February 2024 (118th Congress) after a 42–3 committee vote, but did not advance in the Senate. In the 119th Congress, a House floor vote was scheduled for May 5, 2025 but was canceled by House leadership following opposition from some members of both parties on First Amendment grounds. The bill's status remained pending as of mid-2025. (4) Countering Hate Against Israel by Federal Contractors Act (119th Congress): Reintroduced April 2025 by Rep. Claudia Tenney (R-NY-24) and Rep. Jared Moskowitz (D-FL-23). This bill would prohibit the federal government from entering into contracts with entities that support the BDS movement. Additional cosponsors include Reps. Mike Lawler (R-NY-17), Ben Cline (R-VA-6), Greg Steube (R-FL-17), and Elise Stefanik (R-NY-21). Referred to committee; status pending. No federal anti-BDS bill has been enacted as standalone law. The Export Administration Act anti-boycott provisions (updated via the Anti-Boycott Act of 2018, signed by President Trump, 50 U.S.C. § 4842) prohibit compliance with foreign-government-imposed boycotts of U.S. allies, and the Arab League boycott of Israel is the principal target of that existing law — but this is distinct from the BDS-specific legislation listed above.

## First Amendment case law

The following First Amendment cases challenging state anti-BDS laws have been adjudicated or remained active as of 2025: (1) Koontz v. Watson — U.S. District Court for the District of Kansas (2017–2018). Plaintiff Esther Koontz, a public school curriculum coach, refused to certify that she was not boycotting Israel as required under Kansas HB 2409 (2017) and was denied a state contract. The district court granted a preliminary injunction on January 26, 2018, finding the law likely unconstitutional as a viewpoint-based restriction on protected First Amendment speech and an unconstitutional condition on government contracting. The court relied on NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co. (1982) to hold that boycotts are 'inherently expressive' protected conduct. Kansas amended the law (HB 2482, 2018) to narrow its scope; the parties agreed to dismiss the case. (2) Jordahl v. Brnovich — U.S. District Court for the District of Arizona (2017–2018), appealed to the Ninth Circuit. Attorney Mikkel Jordahl refused to certify non-boycott compliance under Arizona HB 2617 and was denied payment for legal services provided to incarcerated individuals. The District Court of Arizona granted a preliminary injunction on September 27, 2018, holding the law likely violated the First Amendment. Arizona appealed and simultaneously amended the law (SB 1167, effective August 2019) to exempt sole proprietors and contracts below $100,000. On January 6, 2020, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit vacated the preliminary injunction and remanded with instructions to dismiss for mootness, because the amended law no longer applied to Jordahl's firm. The Ninth Circuit did not reach the merits of the First Amendment claim. (3) Amawi v. Pflugerville Independent School District (consolidated with Pluecker v. Paxton) — U.S. District Court for the Western District of Texas, Austin Division (2018–2019); appealed to the Fifth Circuit. Plaintiff Bahia Amawi, a Palestinian-American speech pathologist, lost her contract renewal with Pflugerville ISD after refusing to certify non-boycott compliance under Texas HB 89 (2017). The district court (Judge Robert Pitman) issued a consolidated ruling on April 25, 2019, granting a preliminary injunction and declaring HB 89 unconstitutional as: (a) a content- and viewpoint-based restriction on protected First Amendment expression; (b) imposing unconstitutional conditions on public employment; (c) compelling speech for an impermissible purpose; and (d) void for vagueness. Texas amended HB 89 with HB 793 (signed May 2019) to exempt sole proprietors and small contractors. On April 27, 2020, a unanimous Fifth Circuit panel (Senior Judge Grady Jolly) vacated the preliminary injunction and remanded with instructions to dismiss, finding the case moot because the amendment exempted all plaintiffs (all sole proprietors). The Fifth Circuit did not rule on the First Amendment merits. (4) Arkansas Times LP v. Waldrip — U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Arkansas (2018), Eighth Circuit three-judge panel (2021), Eighth Circuit en banc (2022). The Arkansas Times, an alternative newspaper, refused to certify non-boycott compliance under Arkansas Act 710 (2017) in order to renew advertising contracts with the University of Arkansas Pulaski Technical College. The district court dismissed the First Amendment challenge. A three-judge Eighth Circuit panel reversed on February 12, 2021, holding Act 710 unconstitutional because it infringed protected expressive activity. The Eighth Circuit granted rehearing en banc. On June 22, 2022, the en banc Eighth Circuit reversed the panel and upheld Act 710, with Judge Jonathan Kobes writing for the majority. The majority held that the law regulates only commercial conduct — purchasing and contracting decisions — not protected First Amendment expression, and that the law's definition of 'boycotting Israel' relates solely to commercial activities. The majority distinguished NAACP v. Claiborne Hardware Co. as protecting expressive activities accompanying a boycott, not the commercial purchasing decisions that are the core of a boycott. Judge Jane Kelley dissented, arguing the law was designed to prohibit both commercial and expressive conduct. The U.S. Supreme Court declined to hear the case on February 21, 2023. (5) Martin v. Wrigley — U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Georgia (2020–2022). Filmmaker and journalist Abby Martin was barred from speaking at Georgia Southern University after refusing to sign Georgia's anti-BDS certification (required for contracts of $1,000 or more). CAIR and the Partnership for Civil Justice Fund sued on her behalf. On May 21, 2021, District Court Judge Mark Cohen denied defendants' motion to dismiss and ruled the Georgia law unconstitutional, finding it constituted 'unconstitutional compelled speech,' was a viewpoint-based restriction, and that the certification 'is no different than requiring a person to espouse certain political beliefs or to engage in certain political associations.' Following the ruling, Georgia amended the law in 2022 to apply only to contracts exceeding $100,000 with companies having five or more employees (categories Martin did not meet). The district court subsequently dismissed the case as moot; the Eleventh Circuit affirmed the dismissal. Note on recent circuit trends: A Jerusalem Post report dated July 2023 cited rulings by the Fifth, Eighth, and Eleventh Circuits each upholding state anti-BDS laws, with the Supreme Court declining to review those decisions. The Arkansas Times en banc decision (8th Circuit, 2022) is the only appellate ruling to directly reach the First Amendment merits and uphold an anti-BDS law.
