### Who They Are

Wall Street Finance Networks. The Securities & Investment industry's political infrastructure — the bundling operations, alumni pipelines, revolving-door networks, and cross-firm coordination that make Wall Street the single largest source of political money in American elections. This node maps the network-level architecture: how individual firms ([[Goldman Sachs - Wall Street Titan|Goldman Sachs]], [[JPMorgan]], [[Citadel - Kenneth Griffin|Citadel]], [[Blackstone Group|Blackstone]]) coordinate through trade associations, bundler networks, and shared personnel to purchase regulatory outcomes across both parties.

The Securities & Investment industry contributed $1.88 billion in the 2024 election cycle — the highest total in the industry's history and nearly double the 2020 cycle ($1.11 billion). Of that, $1.39 billion (74%) came through soft money and outside spending groups enabled by Citizens United (2010). Individual contributions totaled $478 million; PAC contributions were $15.8 million — less than 1% of the total. The shift from direct contributions to outside spending groups is the defining structural change in Wall Street political finance since 2010.

The industry's party split has oscillated: Wall Street leaned Republican from 1990–2006 (averaging 53% R), swung to Democrats in 2008 (55.8% D, driven by Obama bundling), returned to Republicans 2012–2014 (68.6% R in 2012), and since 2018 has leaned Democratic (56.7% D in 2024). The both-sides pattern is structural, not ideological — Wall Street funds the party most likely to control the regulatory apparatus.

### The Infrastructure

Wall Street's political network operates through four channels:

**1. Bundler Networks:** Senior executives at major firms organize fundraising events and "bundle" contributions from colleagues. Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Blackstone bundlers have been among the top fundraisers for every presidential campaign from both parties since 2000. The bundling system converts individual $3,300 maxed-out contributions into six- and seven-figure packages delivered by a single bundler who gains direct access to candidates.

**2. Trade Associations:** The Securities Industry and Financial Markets Association (SIFMA) spent $7.62 million on lobbying in 2024 — the top lobbying client in the sector. The Investment Company Institute ($5.9M), Managed Funds Association ($4.73M), and firm-level lobbying (Morgan Stanley $5.05M, Apollo $4.48M, Blackstone $2.76M) add layers. These associations coordinate industry-wide positions on regulation while individual firms pursue firm-specific deregulation.

**3. Revolving Door:** Wall Street alumni occupy key regulatory and policy positions across administrations. Goldman Sachs alone has produced Treasury Secretaries (Robert Rubin under Clinton, Hank Paulson under Bush), White House economic advisors (Gary Cohn under Trump), and Fed officials. The revolving door operates in both directions — former regulators join Wall Street firms at compensation levels that dwarf government salaries.

**4. Super PAC / Outside Spending:** Post-Citizens United, Wall Street's outside spending exploded. In 2024, the top 10 Securities & Investment contributors alone spent over $900 million, dominated by Timothy Mellon ($197M), [[Citadel - Kenneth Griffin|Citadel/Kenneth Griffin]] ($108.5M), Susquehanna International ($101.5M), and [[Blackstone Group|Blackstone]] ($46.8M).

### What They Want

Wall Street's policy agenda is consistent across firms and cycles: regulatory minimization, tax advantage preservation, and bailout availability. The specific priorities shift with market conditions, but the structural demand is constant — the financial industry wants to privatize profits and socialize losses.

**Deregulation:** Repeal or weaken Dodd-Frank provisions (especially the Volcker Rule), resist fiduciary duty requirements, block SEC enforcement actions, prevent new capital requirements. Every major regulatory rollback since Glass-Steagall's repeal (1999) followed sustained Wall Street lobbying and campaign contributions.

**Tax Preservation:** Carried interest loophole protection (allows hedge fund and private equity managers to pay capital gains rates on income), capital gains rate reduction, estate tax minimization. The carried interest loophole alone saves the private equity and hedge fund industries an estimated $18 billion per decade.

**Bailout Infrastructure:** During the 2008 financial crisis, Wall Street firms that had spent decades lobbying against regulation received $700 billion in TARP bailouts plus trillions in Federal Reserve emergency lending facilities. The firms that caused the crisis were rescued; their executives kept their bonuses; no senior Wall Street executive was criminally prosecuted.

**Personnel Placement:** Ensuring Wall Street alumni occupy key regulatory positions (SEC, Treasury, CFTC, Federal Reserve) to shape enforcement and rulemaking from inside.

> [!money] The Both-Sides Investment — $8.5 Billion in 34 Years
> From 1990 through 2024, the Securities & Investment industry has contributed over $8.5 billion to federal elections (cumulative across all cycles per OpenSecrets data). The return on that investment: Glass-Steagall repeal, derivatives deregulation, a $700 billion bailout with no criminal prosecutions, Dodd-Frank weakened during drafting and further rolled back in 2018, and carried interest preserved through every Democratic and Republican administration. Wall Street does not invest in parties — it invests in outcomes. The party split follows regulatory control: when Democrats hold the SEC and Treasury, Wall Street funds Democrats; when Republicans control Congress, Wall Street funds Republicans. The investment is bipartisan because the return is structural.

### Class Analysis — The Regulatory Capture Machine

Wall Street Finance Networks represent the most successful donor-class operation in American politics. The structural function is threefold:

**1. Both-Sides Illusion:** Wall Street funds both parties simultaneously, ensuring that regardless of which party controls government, the financial industry's core interests (deregulation, bailout availability, tax advantages) are protected. The party split oscillates between 45-57% to either side, never approaching the lopsided ratios seen in fossil fuels or labor. This is not ideological flexibility — it is insurance.

**2. Revolving Door as Regulatory Capture:** The personnel pipeline between Wall Street and government ensures that regulators share the industry's worldview. When a Goldman Sachs partner becomes Treasury Secretary, the structural bias is built in — not through corruption, but through shared assumptions about what constitutes "responsible" economic policy. The revolving door operates as a class interest transmission mechanism.

**3. Too Big to Fail as Permanent Leverage:** The 2008 bailout established the precedent that Wall Street's failure threatens the entire economy. This gives the industry permanent leverage: any regulation that Wall Street claims would destabilize the financial system carries the implicit threat of another crisis. The industry created the systemic risk through consolidation, then used that risk as a shield against regulation.

> [!contradiction] The Free Market Fraud
> Wall Street firms publicly champion free markets, deregulation, and limited government — except when they need government bailouts, Federal Reserve emergency lending, or regulatory forbearance. The industry spent decades lobbying to eliminate the regulations that prevented the 2008 crisis, then demanded — and received — the largest government intervention in economic history to rescue itself from the consequences. The rhetorical commitment to free markets is a class position that applies only when markets are profitable; when markets fail, the commitment evaporates and taxpayer-funded rescue becomes "necessary for the economy."
