### Who They Are

The Ohio AFL-CIO is the state labor federation representing 700,000+ union members across manufacturing, construction, service, public sector, and other industries. The organization functions as the primary institutional voice for organized labor in Ohio politics, with significant presence in industrial regions (Youngstown, Cleveland, Cincinnati) and union strongholds. The federation operates political advocacy operations, voter contact infrastructure, and direct campaign support for union-friendly candidates across state and local politics.

### What They Want

The Ohio AFL-CIO advances labor interests including: collective bargaining protections, opposition to right-to-work legislation, prevailing wage protection in public contracting, workplace safety enforcement, and opposition to union-busting corporate practices. The federation frames these interests as working-class power and economic security while simultaneously supporting Democratic candidates who often pursue policies limiting labor's political power (corporate-friendly trade, privatization, automation without worker transition).

### Who They Fund

The Ohio AFL-CIO distributed an estimated $15-20M in direct support, voter contact, and campaign spending across 2020-2024 cycles. Funding flows to: Democratic gubernatorial and state legislative candidates, U.S. Senate Democrats in Ohio, Democratic ballot measures, and labor-aligned PACs. The federation also operates substantial voter contact operations, phone banks, mail, digital advertising, funded through member dues and leveraging union member volunteers.

### What They've Gotten

The Ohio AFL-CIO has secured policy victories including: prevailing wage protections in public contracting, opposition to right-to-work legislation (though national tide has moved against unions), and labor-friendly environmental policies in some municipalities. However, major structural defeats have occurred: Ohio's manufacturing base has contracted dramatically through deindustrialization, union density has fallen from 30% to 12%, and Democratic politicians have often pursued trade and privatization policies contrary to union interests. The federation's spending on Democratic candidates has not translated to labor power growth.

> [!contradiction] The Ohio AFL-CIO funds Democratic politicians who simultaneously pursue policies undermining union strength: trade deals moving manufacturing abroad, privatization of public sector jobs, automation without worker transition support. Democratic politicians accept labor union spending and volunteer support while implementing policies that weaken unions, a classic two-audience problem where unions are treated as essential campaign infrastructure but ignored on policy.

### Class Analysis

The Ohio AFL-CIO represents labor's constraint within Democratic Party politics: substantial organizational capacity and funding, yet systematically outgunned on policy by business interests and finance donors. The federation controls 700,000 member votes and can mobilize significant volunteer networks, genuine working-class power. Yet Democratic politicians repeatedly choose corporate donors over union interests on critical votes (trade, privatization, anti-union legislation). The AFL-CIO's $15-20M spending is dwarfed by Wall Street and tech donor investments in the Democratic Party, revealing labor's secondary position in Democratic coalitions. The federation's ability to deliver voter contact and volunteers remains valuable, but insufficient to overcome donor-class dominance of policy.
