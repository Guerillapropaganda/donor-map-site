# Student debt cancellation

## What it would do

Total outstanding federal student loan debt stands at approximately $1.6 trillion held by about 43 million borrowers as of 2024. The Biden administration used executive authority to cancel approximately $175 billion in student debt for specific borrower categories, including Public Service Loan Forgiveness recipients and borrowers defrauded by their institutions, after the Supreme Court struck down a broader cancellation plan in Biden v. Nebraska (2023). Legislative proposals such as the Student Loan Borrower Bill of Rights would cap interest accrual and establish new repayment protections. The average federal student loan balance at graduation is approximately $29,400, and roughly 7% of borrowers owe more than $100,000, primarily from graduate and professional programs.

> In February 2021, Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer (D-NY) and Sen. Elizabeth Warren (D-MA), joined by Rep. Ayanna Pressley (D-MA) and others, introduced a bicameral resolution calling on President Biden to cancel up to $50,000 in federal student loan debt per borrower through executive authority under Section 432(a) of the Higher Education Act; the resolution was non-binding and no standalone cancellation legislation advanced to a floor vote in either chamber. On August 24, 2022, President Biden announced executive action under the HEROES Act of 2003 to cancel up to $10,000 in federal student loan debt ($20,000 for Pell Grant recipients) for borrowers earning under $125,000 individually or $250,000 as households, covering an estimated 43 million borrowers. On June 30, 2023, the Supreme Court struck down the plan 6–3 in Biden v. Nebraska (143 S.Ct. 2355), with Chief Justice Roberts writing for the majority that the HEROES Act authorized only modest "waivers and modifications," not wholesale debt cancellation of $430 billion, applying the major questions doctrine; the Biden administration's subsequent SAVE income-driven repayment plan (2023) was then separately challenged by Republican-led states, and the Eighth Circuit ordered its termination in March 2026.
