This is not a one-party story

The Republican leading the same primary has the same shape of conflict from the opposite direction.

Steve Hilton owns more than $1 million of stock in a private AI company called Sierra Technologies. His wife is the head of communications at the same company. He is running on rolling back California AI regulation. The disclosure is on his own sworn Form 700, filed under penalty of perjury.

The mechanisms differ. Hilton owns equity directly in a for-profit AI company; the Steyer family donates to a nonprofit AI-policy lobby. The shape of the conflict is identical: a family member is professionally inside the policy ecosystem the candidate would govern.

A vote for either of the two leading candidates from each party is a vote for an AI policy environment that materially affects a member of that candidate's household or family. That is on the public record because California's disclosure regime makes it on the public record. Voters can decide either kind of conflict is acceptable, or that either kind is disqualifying. The point is that both are visible.

California's governor decides what California does about artificial intelligence. The governor signs or vetoes the AI bills the legislature passes. The governor picks the people who run the agencies that police AI companies. Tom Steyer is the Democratic candidate for that job. He is running on tighter AI rules. The organization that has been the principal voice for tighter California AI rules over the last two decades was founded by his brother, has been run by his brother since 2003, and has received at least $5 million from the candidate's own family.

Tom Steyer is one of nine candidates on the June 2 California gubernatorial primary ballot. He is a former hedge-fund manager who ran briefly for the 2020 Democratic presidential nomination. His current campaign is largely self-funded from his own fortune. The most recent independent tracker from Evitarus (May 2 2026) puts him at 12 percent statewide.

Tom's brother, Jim Steyer, has had a parallel public career in California for the same period. Jim is a Stanford-trained civil rights lawyer who founded Common Sense Media in 2003 and has been its CEO for the 23 years since. Common Sense started as a movie and TV ratings service for parents. Over time it has become the most consistent voice before the California legislature on bills regulating social media, AI chatbots, and children's online safety. CalMatters reported on May 4, 2026 that Tom and his wife Kat Taylor have donated at least $5 million to Common Sense Media.

Three California bills currently pending or recently acted on define the policy footprint Common Sense has built. Two are active in the legislature right now. One was vetoed by Governor Newsom last fall. If Tom Steyer is elected governor, the next round of these bills would land on his desk.

What Common Sense Media is

Common Sense Media is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit headquartered in San Francisco. Jim Steyer founded it in 2003 alongside cofounder Daniel Goldin. The original product was a parental-guidance ratings system for movies, television, books, websites, apps, and games, similar in concept to Motion Picture Association ratings but cataloging far more types of content. The ratings service is still its public-facing product.

The organization's policy footprint has grown substantially since its founding. Its first major California legislative push came in 2005 with a state law restricting the sale of violent video games to minors. The U.S. Supreme Court struck down that law on First Amendment grounds in 2011 (Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association). The loss did not slow the organization's California advocacy.

In 2018 Common Sense was a publicly named supporter of California's landmark consumer-privacy law, the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), which created the right for California residents to ask companies to delete personal data. Over the last five years the organization's California work has shifted heavily toward AI and social-media-safety legislation. By 2025 Common Sense was the most consistently named advocate before the California legislature on bills regulating tech-company conduct toward minors.

The organization's current revenues, programmatic spending, and Jim Steyer's compensation are reported on its IRS Form 990 filing. Form 990 is the public tax return that 501(c)(3) nonprofits file annually with the IRS. Common Sense's filings are searchable on the ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer.

The three bills, in detail

These are the three California Assembly Bills CalMatters identified Common Sense Media as a named advocate behind in its May 4, 2026 reporting. All three are in the 2025-2026 California legislative session. Bill texts, authors, and status are sourced from the California Legislative Information system.

AB-1064
Vetoed by Newsom
"Leading Ethical AI Development (LEAD) for Kids Act"
What it would do
Restrict AI companion chatbots from being made available to minors unless the operator can show the chatbot cannot foreseeably encourage self-harm, suicidal ideation, violence, substance abuse, or eating disorders, and cannot provide unsupervised mental-health services or sexually explicit interactions. Operators must have actual knowledge a user is a minor before January 1, 2027, and a reasonable determination after that date.
Who introduced it
Assembly Member Bauer-Kahan (principal author), Assembly Member Pellerin (coauthor). Enrolled by both chambers on September 15, 2025. Vetoed by Governor Newsom shortly after enrollment, per CalMatters reporting.
Bill text and history: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov · AB-1064 (2025-2026)
AB-1709
Active · pending
"Covered platforms: age restriction: e-Safety Advisory Commission"
What it would do
Prohibit social media platforms from allowing users under 16 to create or maintain accounts. Require platforms to verify user age under California's Digital Age Assurance Act. Define "addictive features" such as algorithmic feeds, autoplay, and notifications. Establish an e-Safety Advisory Commission to oversee implementation. Authorize the Attorney General to adopt regulations.
Who introduced it
Assembly Members Lowenthal, Alvarez, Bauer-Kahan, Bonta, Hoover, Muratsuchi, Patterson, and Wicks; Senator Allen. The author list spans both parties. Last amended in Assembly on April 23, 2026.
Bill text and history: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov · AB-1709 (2025-2026)
AB-2023
Active · pending
"Companion chatbots: children's safety"
What it would do
Require AI companion-chatbot operators to conduct annual risk assessments, publish safety policies, implement crisis protocols for suicide and self-harm, set usage limits, provide parental controls, and prohibit content harmful to minors. Require either age verification under the Digital Age Assurance Act or application of child protections to all users. Beginning 180 days after Attorney General regulations (expected 2028), require operators to submit annual independent safety audits to the AG. Authorize civil enforcement by prosecutors and by injured children themselves.
Who introduced it
Assembly Members Wicks (principal author) and Bauer-Kahan; Senator Padilla (principal coauthor); Assembly Member Lowenthal (coauthor). Last amended in Assembly on April 27, 2026.
Bill text and history: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov · AB-2023 (2025-2026)

Two of the three bills (AB-1709, AB-2023) are actively pending in the legislature as of late April 2026. The third (AB-1064) was passed by both chambers and vetoed by the sitting governor. None of these are abstract policy proposals. All three are the actual legislative product of the advocacy work Common Sense Media has done in Sacramento.

What each bill would actually do

A bill's title is a summary; its operational consequences are different. Each of the three bills, if signed by the next governor, reaches a specific set of California companies, products, and users. The consequences are public, traceable, and where possible quoted from the bill text itself.

AB-1064 · the chatbot age-gate
What changes for users under 18
AI companion chatbots (Replika, Character.AI, Snap MyAI, others) become unavailable unless the operator can certify the chatbot cannot foreseeably encourage self-harm, suicide, violence, substance abuse, eating disorders, or sexually explicit content. The bill targets companion chatbots specifically, not general-purpose AI assistants used in education or productivity.
What changes for operators
Companies offering companion chatbots in California must build age-verification infrastructure and safety guardrails, or remove their products from California for users under 18. The compliance cost falls particularly hard on smaller operators that cannot fund extensive safety review. Larger operators with enterprise compliance budgets absorb it more easily, creating a structural advantage for incumbents.
AB-1709 · the social-media age-floor at 16
What changes for users under 16
Existing accounts on covered social media platforms must be removed or made inaccessible. New accounts cannot be created. The current federal baseline (COPPA) sets the social-media age at 13; this bill would raise California's effective floor to 16. Users between 13 and 15 lose access to platforms they currently use legally under federal rules.
What changes for platforms
Meta, TikTok, Snap, X, YouTube, and any platform with "addictive features" as defined by the bill (algorithmic feeds, autoplay, notifications) must implement age-verification technology under the Digital Age Assurance Act. NetChoice and similar tech-trade groups have legally challenged comparable bills in other states on First Amendment grounds. California's bill is likely to face the same litigation. The e-Safety Advisory Commission established by the bill creates a continuing regulatory layer separate from the AG's office.
AB-2023 · the chatbot audit regime
What changes for the AI industry
Annual third-party safety audits become mandatory for companion-chatbot operators serving California users. The audits begin in 2028 and must be submitted to the Attorney General. A new "AI safety audit" service industry emerges to perform them. Audit costs raise the floor for entering the California companion-chatbot market.
What changes for users and prosecutors
Injured children gain a private right of action against operators that fail to comply, in addition to enforcement by the Attorney General and local prosecutors. That creates a litigation surface that doesn't currently exist. Operators face both regulatory and tort exposure for the same underlying conduct. The bill prohibits chatbots from providing medical diagnoses without FDA approval, which materially constrains how AI products can be used for any health-adjacent function.
↓ THE STRUCTURAL POINT
What all three bills share
Each one expands the regulatory authority of California's executive branch over AI and social-media products. Each one creates new compliance obligations enforced by California state agencies. Each one creates a continuing role for an outside advocacy organization (the bills' named supporter or sponsor) to monitor and shape rulemaking. The next governor signs, vetoes, or shapes implementation of all of them.
Where Common Sense fits
As a named advocate behind all three bills, Common Sense's policy footprint expands as each one becomes law. The organization that has built its institutional credibility around advocating for these rules becomes the natural ongoing voice consulted during agency rulemaking, regulatory review, and enforcement-priority setting. That access is shaped by who occupies the governor's office.

What named California-policy figures say

Two figures quoted by name in CalMatters' reporting flagged the conflict-of-interest question directly. Peter Leroe-Munoz of the Bay Area Council, the major Bay Area business lobby:

"Common Sense Media would have an outsized influence on California tech policy if Mr. Steyer ends up becoming the governor." Peter Leroe-Munoz · Bay Area Council · CalMatters · May 4, 2026

And Jamie Court, president of Consumer Watchdog, framed it as a difference of policy posture between the two brothers:

"Jim might have a harder audience with Tom than another governor. Jim's a little bit more accommodating to the companies because he has to work with them. Tom shoots more from the hip. Tom might be a little more radical." Jamie Court · Consumer Watchdog · CalMatters · May 4, 2026

Tom Steyer's own response, also quoted by CalMatters, addresses the family relationship directly:

"My brother's been protecting kids for 50 years and I listen to him, but it's not like he's suddenly going to become me." Tom Steyer · CalMatters · May 4, 2026

Jim Steyer publicly endorsed Tom on X, calling him "the fighter that California needs right now."

Why this matters

Common Sense Media's California policy footprint is not novel and not hidden. It has been built openly over two decades. Jim Steyer is widely known in California-policy circles as the founder of the organization. Tom Steyer's family donations to it are reportable charitable giving. None of those facts are concealed.

The point that becomes load-bearing in the 2026 governor's race is this: the candidate who would sign or veto the next round of Common Sense's bills is funded by, and brother to, the person who runs Common Sense. A vote for Tom Steyer is a vote that brings the Common Sense policy program closer to enactment, with the Steyer family's money already inside the organization that drafted the proposals. A vote against him is the absence of that connection.

The mechanism is documented on the public record because California's disclosure regime worked: Common Sense Media files Form 990, donations to it appear in the giving-vehicle records of the Steyer family's own foundations, the bills it advocated for are searchable on the legislature's public service. The point of this story is not that the disclosure regime failed. The point is that what the disclosure regime makes visible deserves to be visible to voters, not just to lobbyists and reporters who already know the relationship exists.

The same kind of family-AI entanglement exists on the Republican side of the race, in the opposite policy direction. What this story does not say

Boundary lines · explicit

This story documents what the public record shows. It does not assert any of the following:

  • Not illegal. Donating to a 501(c)(3) nonprofit is routine philanthropy. Family members may legally found, run, fund, and serve nonprofits without that itself being a campaign-finance or ethics violation. Tom Steyer has not been sanctioned by any enforcement body for the family relationship to Common Sense Media.
  • Not proof of bribery. The records establish that the candidate's family has a direct, documented financial relationship with the organization advocating policy the candidate would sign or veto. They do not establish that Tom Steyer traded an official decision for a private benefit, or violated any conflict-of-interest rule.
  • Not a quid pro quo claim. Tom Steyer's policy positions on AI safety predate his current campaign. We are not asserting that Tom adopted his AI positions because his brother runs Common Sense, or that Jim shaped Common Sense's positions because of Tom's campaign. The relationship existed for two decades before the campaign.
  • Not equating this case to the Hilton case factually. Hilton owns equity directly in a for-profit AI company; Tom Steyer's family donates to a 501(c)(3) nonprofit and Tom himself does not own Common Sense Media. The mechanisms differ. The structural question — does the candidate's family have a financial interest in the outcome of the policy decisions the office makes — is what both cases share.
