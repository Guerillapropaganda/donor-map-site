Silicon Valley's venture capital industry wants Matt Mahan to be governor. The firms that fund the AI boom and the consumer-tech industry have put more than $26 million behind a single Democratic candidate. Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins, Khosla Ventures, Founders Fund, Y Combinator. The Stripe CEO, the Reddit CEO, the Coinbase CEO, the Netflix co-founder, the Google co-founder. Their portfolio companies are about to be regulated by California.

Matt Mahan is the current mayor of San Jose. He is running for California governor in the June 2026 Democratic primary. By total dollar volume, his campaign is one of the larger in the field. By structure, his campaign is alone.

Most major California gubernatorial campaigns are roughly two-thirds candidate committee and one-third independent expenditures. Mahan's ratio is inverted. Sixty-seven percent of the money backing him sits in two IE PACs that the candidate is legally barred from coordinating with. Sixty-one donors funded that two-thirds. The other 1,519 donors split the remaining one-third, and 99.6 percent of the candidate committee came from people writing four-figure-and-up checks.

That structure does not happen by accident. Donors decide whether to give through the candidate committee (capped at $78,400 across primary plus general) or through an IE PAC (no cap). The IE PAC is where the real money goes when the donor wants to write a check larger than what the candidate is allowed to receive. Mahan's IE PAC stack averages roughly $440,000 per donor. Multiple donors gave more than a million each. Michael Moritz of Sequoia gave three. Brian Armstrong, the chief executive of Coinbase, gave half a million. The list of names funding his campaign reads like an invitation list to an exclusive Silicon Valley investor dinner.

The funnel: Govern for California → Back to Basics

The donor class doesn't always send checks the obvious way. On April 13, 2026, an organization called Govern for California Action Committee transferred $1.5 million to Back to Basics in two same-day payments of $500,000 and $1,000,000.

Govern for California is a separate FPPC committee with its own donor list. The names that appear in current Cal-Access cover records as Govern for California funders include Roblox CEO David Baszucki at $200,000, Hyatt heir Nicholas Pritzker at $300,000, HRTG partner Kevin Kelly at $250,000, and Quiet Capital partner Brian Singerman at $50,000.

The candidate committee, where the small dollars are supposed to be

Candidate committees are usually where the grassroots layer of a campaign lives. Volunteers sending $25, $50, $100. The four-digit donors are also there, but they are supposed to be a smaller share than the volunteer base.

Mahan's candidate committee has 1,519 unique donors, which sounds like grassroots. The arithmetic underneath does not.

573 of those 1,519 donors gave $200 or less. That is more than a third of his donor base by count. Their combined contribution was $57,700. Of every $100 raised by Mahan's candidate committee, thirty-eight cents came from $200-or-less giving. The other $99.62 came from 946 donors writing four-figure-and-up checks, including 100 donors all sitting at the legal $78,400 ceiling. That is not a grassroots layer with a few large donors on top. That is a large-donor layer with a small-dollar layer underneath.

The named donors at the top of the candidate committee are the same donor class as the IE PACs, not a different one:

One hundred donors gave the legal maximum of $78,400. One hundred sixty-five donors gave $39,200 or more. The candidate committee's top tier is not "a few wealthy donors at the cap." It is one hundred donors all sitting at exactly the same number, the legal ceiling.

The cross-hedge: same donors, different candidates

The donor class isn't betting on Matt Mahan. It's betting on a position.

Rick Caruso, the Los Angeles developer billionaire, gave $1.5 million to Mahan's IE PAC plus $78,400 to Mahan's candidate committee. In December 2025, Caruso also gave $15,600 to Xavier Becerra's governor campaign, and his wife Tina Caruso gave another $15,600 the same day. Across both candidates, the Caruso household put more than $1.6 million into the 2026 Democratic primary, hedged across the two leading centrist Democrats. The bet isn't on Mahan. The bet is on either-Mahan-or-Becerra against Steyer.

Sergey Brin, the Google co-founder, gave $1 million to Mahan's Deliver IE plus $78,400 to Mahan's candidate committee. He also gave $39,200 to Steve Hilton, the Republican candidate. One Google co-founder, one Democrat, one Republican, both at maximum levels their respective committees would accept. The bet isn't on a party. The bet is on a tax and tech-policy position.

Joe Lonsdale, Palantir co-founder and managing director of the Austin-based venture firm 8VC, gave $78,400 to Mahan's candidate committee. He also gave $25,000 to Steve Hilton. Same shape as Brin: one billionaire, one Democrat, one Republican, both at maximum levels.

Michael Moritz, the Sequoia partner who is Mahan's largest single donor, is also a co-founder and funder of the San Francisco Standard, the local political news outlet that covers the San Francisco and California political scene. The San Francisco Chronicle named him as both. We make no claim about Standard coverage bias here. We observe that Mahan's largest donor is also a financial backer of a publication that covers Mahan's race. Those are both true facts.

The Thiel orbit, without Thiel

Peter Thiel does not appear in Matt Mahan's California donor records. Cal-Access has one direct Thiel state-level contribution in the 2024 through 2026 cycle: $3,000,000 to California Business Roundtable Issues PAC on December 29, 2025. That money did not go to Mahan, did not go to either pro-Mahan IE PAC, and did not go to Govern for California.

The Thiel orbit, however, is in Mahan's donor list. Brian Singerman spent sixteen years as a partner at Founders Fund (Thiel's venture firm) before launching Quiet Capital. He gave $1.13 million across four pro-Mahan committees. Joe Lonsdale co-founded Palantir with Thiel; he gave the FPPC max to Mahan's candidate committee plus $25,000 to Hilton. Reid Hoffman, Thiel's longtime business partner from PayPal and a Greylock co-founder, gave $39,200 to Mahan. Mike Solana, affiliated with Founders Fund, told Politico in January that Mahan is "the state's sole opportunity for a rational Democrat."

The San Francisco Chronicle named the pattern in March: Mahan's billionaire benefactors include "donors in the Palantir / Thiel-linked Silicon Valley network." That is the careful framing. Thiel-orbit support is documented. Thiel personal support is not. The orbit shows up in this candidate's donor list. The man at the center hedges his California donor-class position somewhere else entirely.

The donor class doesn't pick a candidate. It picks a position. Anti-labor. Anti-Steyer. Pro-tech-policy. Whichever centrist Democrat makes the November ballot is fine. Whichever Republican qualifies is fine as a hedge.

Labor: a record, not a position

CalMatters described Mahan's gubernatorial donor base on April 24, 2026 as "Silicon Valley executives, billionaires, and groups known to clash with Sacramento labor unions." That is the donor side. The candidate side has a record.

In August 2023, eight months into Mahan's first year as San Jose mayor, two unions representing about 4,500 city employees authorized a three-day strike by a 99 percent vote. The unions were IFPTE Local 21 and Municipal Employees' Federation AFSCME Local 101. The dispute was about cost-of-living adjustments. Union proposal: 18 percent over three years. City offer: 12 percent. The City Council averted the strike by approving a deal at 14.5 percent over three years.

Mahan was the only official to vote against ratifying the contracts.

"Our unions did their job. But our council did not do its job."
Matt Mahan, Sept 2023, on the city-worker raises he voted against

Three months later, the South Bay Labor Council's executive officer Jean Cohen told KQED she expected Mahan would face a labor-backed challenger because of dissatisfaction with his first year. He didn't, and ran unopposed for re-election in 2024. But the relationship with organized labor in his own city has been documented friction since then. He has not stated a position on AB5, the federal PRO Act, public-sector collective bargaining, project labor agreements, or CalPERS reform in his 2026 governor campaign.

There is one other piece of public record on Mahan and worker classification. In October 2022, before he took office as mayor, the California state labor commissioner received a complaint that his 2022 mayoral campaign had misclassified eighteen workers as independent contractors instead of employees. The roles included volunteer coordinator, deputy field director, communications director, and campaign manager. The complaint was settled by reclassifying the workers as employees under California AB5.

The candidate's own 2022 campaign was found to have crossed the line California's worker-classification law draws. Three years later, the same candidate is funded heavily by the venture capital firms that invest in companies whose business models depend on California's worker-classification rules holding still. Coincidence is not a claim. Pattern is a claim. The pattern is here.

Why this matters: lane three of the donor class

The 2026 California Democratic primary has three structural lanes by donor relationship:

Donor-class consolidation is Xavier Becerra. Healthcare donors triple-stacked behind him, the California Medical Association endorsement, Bearstar Strategies running the messaging operation. The donor class chose him; he is the consolidation lane. Three Becerras is the documentation.

Donor-class apostate is Tom Steyer. He made his money in the donor class and left. The donor class organized $21 million against him: Pacific Gas & Electric, the realtors, the chamber of commerce, the building industry, the prison-guards union. He is the apostate lane. $31 million to bury a class traitor is the documentation.

Donor-class concentration is Matt Mahan. The investing class of the AI and consumer-tech boom funds him heavily through IE PACs. Sixty-one donors fund two-thirds of his support. The funnel architecture (Govern for California → Back to Basics) adds an additional layer of donor obscurity. The cross-hedge with Becerra (Caruso) and Hilton (Brin) shows the donor class isn't really choosing a candidate, it's choosing a position. This is the concentration lane. This page is the documentation.

None of these lanes is illegal. None of them is corrupt by any prosecutable definition. All three are structurally distinctive enough that California voters have a right to know which lane each candidate occupies before they choose between them.

Open questions

Mahan's gubernatorial-campaign labor positions. No 2026 statement has been located on AB5, the PRO Act, public-sector collective bargaining, project labor agreements, or CalPERS reform. The CalMatters voter guide submitted by his campaign does not include positions on these specific policy areas. Press has not asked.

Mahan's gubernatorial-campaign cryptocurrency and digital-assets policy positions. No 2026 statement has been located on California's Digital Financial Assets Law (DFAL), the July 1, 2026 licensing deadline that runs concurrent with the Democratic primary, Coinbase enforcement matters, or the SEC v. Ripple resolution. Brian Armstrong's $500,000 contribution to the pro-Mahan IE has not been the subject of a press accountability question to Mahan or his campaign.

Govern for California's full Mahan-directed flow. The April 13, 2026 transfer of $1.5 million is documented. Earlier or later transfers to other Mahan-supportive committees may exist and are not yet fully traced.

The Moritz / San Francisco Standard editorial relationship. Moritz is documented as both Mahan's largest single donor and as a co-founder and funder of the San Francisco Standard. We make no claim about Standard coverage bias. The dual role is the open question.
