I've been trying to explain why this project is needed. Many things come back to me. The state of our country. The everyday woes people continually endure. But the main driving factor is accountability.

I watch hours of political pundits and reporters detailing crime after crime with one thought: **Why aren't these people in prison?**

Where is the accountability? People speak truth to power but the powerful just escape any consequences. What allows them to slip right by and face nothing?

I think the answer is clear. It's the money. The people funding these politicians are the ones holding up the systematic corruption. This project is an attempt to show the average everyday "normie" that maybe the policies they've been told about by their media consent speakers are a lie. The ulterior motives and two-speak politicians, the ones with a public persona and a private one, are only following orders to their actual masters.

**This is a systematic problem, not a cultural one.**

When I read into politics I try to look at it from a class and economic standpoint. But today's world consists of reactionary, small-minded solutions. We need a bigger lens. **WHO actually wants these policies implemented?** Who actually believes the taxes they pay shouldn't service their own lives, and should instead service the billionaires and controllers of society?

That's how my mind works. Question after question, trying to find those answers. I know the answers exist. But there are guardrails that prevent the normal working-class person from knowing the truth of how society really operates.

The politician works for incentives. The incentives come from the donors who fund their campaigns. That funding drove the legislation they supported in 2020 and the legislation they suddenly fearmonger against in 2024. Ideological standpoints don't really exist in a system based solely on profit.

**Money and power IS the ideological standpoint.**

The class-analysis question is the only one you should be asking: *Who pays, and who gets paid?*

My ideological standpoint isn't Democrat or Republican. It's who benefits from this policy, the worker or the powers that be? **It cannot be a partisan lens when Wall Street funds both sides.** Someone who calls themselves a Democrat but takes oil money WILL break party lines. So where does the ideology actually stand?

### Why Tier 1 Sources

I rank every source in this database: Tier 1 through Tier 4.

Tier 1 is the government record. FEC filings. State campaign finance. Congressional voting records. IRS 990s. Court documents. The original paperwork.

Tier 2 is investigative journalism with editorial standards. ProPublica, The Intercept, The Washington Post's investigations desk.

Tier 3 is secondary reporting. Ballotpedia, Wikipedia, trade press.

Tier 4 is partisan, single-sourced, or unverified.

The tiers exist because credibility is not a vibe. Credibility is a chain of evidence. When a profile says Koch Industries donated $2.3M to a politician, I don't say "sources say" or "according to reports." I link to the FEC filing. That's Tier 1. You can click it. You can read the original record.

If I can't find a Tier 1 source, I say so. If all I have is a Tier 4 source, I flag it with (PARTISAN SOURCE) or (UNVERIFIED) tags. I tell you exactly what I'm relying on.

This isn't neutrality. I have a point of view. It's accountability. My point of view has to survive your fact-checking.

### Why I Flag Contradictions

Every politician tells two stories. One to their voters, one to their donors. The gap between those stories is the most important fact about them.

I built this database around that gap.

A Contradiction Deep Dive is an investigation into a case where the stated position and the material position don't match. When a senator runs on breaking up big banks and then votes for a bill written by Goldman Sachs lobbyists, that's a contradiction. When a populist railing against corporate power takes money from the Koch Network, that's a contradiction. When two senators publicly feud while sharing the same donors, that's the Both-Sides Illusion.

I name these patterns. I've documented nine of them. And the patterns repeat across parties, across decades, across issues, because they're not glitches. They're the system working as designed.

Flagging contradictions is the part of this project that couldn't be automated if I wanted to. It requires reading the rhetoric, reading the record, and saying "these don't match." That's an editorial judgment. I make it. I cite it. I own it.

### Why I Publish My Standards

Most investigative outlets don't tell you how they work. I do.

My Quality Standards document defines what counts as a verified source, what promotes a profile from draft to ready, how I handle 404s, how I cite API endpoints, how I flag my own uncertainty.

I publish this because the alternative, "trust me," is not enough. In an era of AI-generated content, unnamed sources, and partisan framing, the only currency that matters is traceable evidence. A claim without a source is worth less than no claim at all.

My methodology is the warranty. If I violate it, you can point it out. If I don't meet my own gates, the profile doesn't get promoted. I'd rather say "I don't know yet" than tell you something I can't back up.

### Why I Use AI (And Why I'm Telling You)

Every independent investigative outlet operating at this scale is using AI. Most of them don't tell you.

I do. Here's what I use it for:

- **Drafting**: first-pass profile writing from verified source material
- **Aggregating**: pulling together sourced findings across multiple filings
- **Pattern detection**: flagging potential contradictions for human review
- **Verification**: running structured checks against my source tier rules

Here's what I use humans for:

- **Editorial judgment**: deciding what matters, what doesn't, what's a pattern vs. noise
- **Contradiction detection**: naming the gap between rhetoric and record
- **Source verification**: checking every URL, every filing ID, every tier assignment
- **Voice**: making sure what gets published is something a person would actually say

This combination is what makes the project possible for one editor. It's also what makes it vulnerable if I do it wrong. The methodology exists to keep the human layer honest. I'd rather use tools transparently than pretend I built everything by hand.

Someone has to do that work.

The Donor Map is a bet that human investigative judgment, cited evidence, class analysis, and a clear editorial voice will matter more in an AI-saturated media environment, not less.
